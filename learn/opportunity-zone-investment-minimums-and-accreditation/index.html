Opportunity Zone Investment Minimums & Accreditation

By Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 17 min read

Before you invest in a Qualified Opportunity Fund, two practical questions arise: how much does it take to get in, and do you qualify? This guide covers typical QOF minimums, accredited-investor rules, institutional vs. retail funds, planning your allocation, and how to confirm your eligibility before investing.

Beyond the tax mechanics, two practical questions determine whether you can actually invest in a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF): how much money does it take, and are you eligible? Sponsor-managed QOFs are securities, generally offered to accredited investors through private placements, with minimum investments set by the sponsor — often in the tens of thousands of dollars, sometimes far higher for institutional funds. Understanding the typical minimums, the accredited-investor rules, the difference between institutional and retail QOFs, how to plan your allocation, and how to confirm your eligibility helps you approach OZ investing realistically and prepared. This guide walks through what it takes to invest in a QOF — the minimums, the accreditation requirements, the fund types, the allocation planning, and the eligibility confirmation. Note that the rules and thresholds are time-sensitive and can change; verify the current rules with your advisor, as this is educational information, not investment advice.

Key takeaways
  • QOF minimums are sponsor-set and vary widely — often roughly $25,000 to $100,000+ for retail funds, and substantially higher for institutional funds.
  • Sponsor-managed QOFs are generally offered only to accredited investors (income/net-worth thresholds or professional credentials), because they're private-placement securities.
  • QOFs span retail funds (for accredited individuals, lower minimums) to institutional funds (for large investors, much higher minimums) — most individuals use retail funds.
  • Plan your OZ allocation prudently (a portion of a diversified portfolio), confirm your eligibility and the exact minimum in the offering documents, and verify the current rules, which can change.

Typical QOF minimums

QOF minimum investments are set by the sponsor and vary widely, but typically start in the tens of thousands of dollars. Many sponsor-managed retail QOFs have minimums in the range of roughly $25,000 to $100,000 or more, making them accessible to individual accredited investor s. So the entry point for a typical QOF is meaningful but not out of reach for many accredited investors.

Minimums vary by fund: some funds set lower minimums to broaden access, while others (especially institutional or specialized funds) set substantially higher minimums (hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars), targeting larger or institutional investors. So the minimum depends on the specific fund's structure and target investor base.

Accredited investor rules

Most sponsor-managed QOFs are offered only to accredited investors, because they're securities sold through private placements. An accredited investor is generally someone who meets certain income or net-worth thresholds — historically, individual income above a set level (or joint income above a higher level) in recent years, or a net worth above a set level excluding the primary residence — or who holds certain professional credentials. So accredited status is the typical gateway to investing in a sponsor-managed QOF.

The specific thresholds and qualifying criteria are set by securities regulations and can change over time, so you should verify the current accredited-investor definition rather than relying on remembered figures. The criteria have expanded in recent years to include certain professional certifications and other categories, beyond the traditional income and net-worth tests. So 'accredited' covers several qualifying paths.

Importantly, the OZ tax statute itself doesn't require you to be accredited to claim the tax benefits — the accreditation requirement comes from the securities laws governing the private-placement funds investors actually access. So in practice, investing in a sponsor-managed QOF generally requires accredited status. Institutional vs. retail QOFs

QOFs span a spectrum from retail-oriented funds to large institutional funds, with different minimums and investor bases. Retail (or individual-investor-oriented) QOFs are designed for accredited individual investors, with minimums often in the tens of thousands (e.g., ~$25,000 to $100,000+), broader investor pools, and structures suited to individuals. So retail QOFs are the funds most individual accredited investors access.

Institutional QOFs target large investors (institutions, family offices, very-high-net-worth individuals), with much higher minimums (often hundreds of thousands or millions), and may offer different terms, strategies, or scale. So institutional funds are generally out of reach for typical individual investors due to their high minimums, though some sophisticated individuals participate.

Between these, there's a range of funds at various minimum levels and investor orientations. Planning your allocation

Once you know you're eligible and can meet a fund's minimum, plan how much to allocate to OZ investments within your overall portfolio. Because OZ investments are illiquid, long-term (ideally 10 years), and higher-risk (often development), they generally warrant only a portion of a diversified portfolio — sized to capital you can commit for the long hold and afford to put at risk. So don't over-concentrate in OZ investments.

Consider how the minimum interacts with your allocation: if a fund's minimum is large relative to your intended OZ allocation, it may represent too big a single bet (concentration risk), suggesting a smaller fund, a multi-asset fund, or diversifying across funds. So balance the minimum against prudent sizing and diversification. Also coordinate the OZ investment with your gain (you reinvest the capital-gain portion within 180 days) and your liquidity needs.

Your financial advisor can help determine an appropriate OZ allocation given your goals, liquidity needs, and risk tolerance. Fees, structure, and what your minimum buys

Beyond the minimum and accreditation, understand what your investment actually buys and what it costs. A QOF interest typically gives you a passive ownership stake (often a limited-partnership or LLC membership interest) in the fund, with the sponsor or manager handling the OZ compliance, the project execution, and the reporting. So your minimum buys a passive, professionally-managed interest, not an active role.

Fees reduce your net return and vary by fund — they may include acquisition fees, asset-management fees, disposition fees, and (for some funds) carried interest or promote. So scrutinize the full fee schedule in the offering documents, since fees affect your projected outcome. Also review the structure (the entity, your rights, the distribution waterfall) and the liquidity terms (typically illiquid, with a long hold).

Understanding the fees and structure helps you assess whether a fund's minimum represents good value for the management, diversification, and access it provides. Frequently asked questions

What is the typical minimum to invest in a QOF?

QOF minimums are set by the sponsor and vary widely, but many retail (individual-investor-oriented) QOFs have minimums in the range of roughly $25,000 to $100,000 or more. So the entry point for a typical QOF is meaningful but accessible to many accredited investors. Some funds set lower minimums to broaden access, while institutional or specialized funds set substantially higher minimums (hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars), targeting larger or institutional investors. So the minimum depends on the specific fund's structure and target investor base. Because the minimum is sponsor-set, confirm the exact figure for any fund you're considering in its offering documents rather than relying on a general range. So expect a typical retail QOF minimum in the tens of thousands (often ~$25,000 to $100,000+), but verify the precise minimum for each fund, since it varies by sponsor and offering.

Do I have to be an accredited investor to invest in a QOF?

For most sponsor-managed QOFs, yes — they're securities sold through private placements, generally offered only to accredited investors. An accredited investor is someone who meets certain income or net-worth thresholds, or holds certain professional credentials. So accredited status is the typical gateway to a sponsor-managed QOF. Importantly, the OZ tax statute itself doesn't require you to be accredited to claim the tax benefits — the accreditation requirement comes from the securities laws governing the private-placement funds investors actually access. In theory, a non-accredited investor could form their own QOF or invest in a deal not structured as a private placement, but in practice the sponsor-managed funds most investors use require accredited status. So to invest in a typical sponsor-managed QOF, you'll generally need to be accredited — confirm the current criteria and your eligibility, since the thresholds can change.

What are the accredited investor thresholds?

Accredited-investor status is generally based on income or net-worth thresholds set by securities regulations — historically, individual annual income above a set level (or joint income above a higher level) in recent years, or a net worth above a set level excluding your primary residence. The criteria have also expanded to include certain professional certifications and other qualifying categories beyond the traditional income and net-worth tests. So 'accredited' covers several qualifying paths. Importantly, these thresholds and criteria are set by regulation and can change over time, so you should verify the current accredited-investor definition rather than relying on remembered figures. So expect to qualify via an income test, a net-worth test, or a professional-credential path, but confirm the exact current thresholds with your advisor — they're subject to change, and the specifics matter for determining your eligibility. Your broker-dealer will verify your status during the subscription process.

What's the difference between institutional and retail QOFs?

Retail (individual-investor-oriented) QOFs are designed for accredited individual investors, with minimums often in the tens of thousands (e.g., ~$25,000 to $100,000+), broader investor pools, and structures suited to individuals — these are the funds most individual accredited investors access. Institutional QOFs target large investors (institutions, family offices, very-high-net-worth individuals), with much higher minimums (often hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars), and may offer different terms, strategies, or scale. So institutional funds are generally out of reach for typical individual investors due to their high minimums, though some sophisticated individuals participate. Between these, there's a range of funds at various minimum levels and investor orientations. So if you're an individual accredited investor, you'll most likely access retail-oriented QOFs suited to your capital, while institutional funds serve larger investors — match the fund type to your capital and the minimums you can meet.

Can I invest in a QOF if I'm not accredited?

In practice, it's difficult, because most sponsor-managed QOFs are private placements limited to accredited investors. The OZ tax statute itself doesn't impose an accreditation requirement on the tax benefit — so technically, a non-accredited investor could self-certify their own QOF (Form 8996) and undertake their own OZ project, or potentially invest in an offering not structured as an accredited-only private placement. But the sponsored, professionally-managed funds most investors rely on generally require accredited status. So for the typical passive QOF investment, you'll usually need to be accredited. If you're not accredited but want OZ exposure, the realistic paths are limited and more involved (such as forming your own QOF, which carries significant compliance burdens). So while the tax law doesn't require accreditation, the practical investment options for non-accredited investors are narrow — discuss your situation and any available options with your advisor, and verify the current rules.

How much of my portfolio should I allocate to OZ investments?

There's no universal answer, but because OZ investments are illiquid, long-term (ideally 10 years), and higher-risk (often development), they generally warrant only a portion of a diversified portfolio — sized to capital you can commit for the long hold and afford to put at risk. So don't over-concentrate in OZ investments. Consider how a fund's minimum interacts with your intended allocation: if the minimum is large relative to what you want in OZ, it may represent too big a single bet (concentration risk), suggesting a smaller fund, a multi-asset fund, or diversifying across funds. Also coordinate with your gain (you reinvest the capital-gain portion within 180 days) and your liquidity needs. So treat OZ investments as one component of a diversified plan, sized appropriately for their illiquidity and risk — not a place to concentrate capital you may need. Your financial advisor can help determine an appropriate allocation for your goals and risk tolerance.

What does my QOF investment actually buy?

A QOF interest typically gives you a passive ownership stake — often a limited-partnership or LLC membership interest — in the fund, with the sponsor or manager handling the OZ compliance, the project execution, and the reporting. So your minimum buys a passive, professionally-managed interest, not an active role in the project. Your investment entitles you to your share of the fund's economics (distributions, if any, and your portion of the eventual sale proceeds and appreciation), subject to the fund's structure and distribution waterfall. The fund handles the 90% asset test, the substantial-improvement requirement, the Form 8996 self-certification, and the ongoing reporting at the fund level (you'll receive tax information for your own filings, such as Forms 8949 and 8997). So you're buying a managed, compliant OZ investment, with the sponsor doing the heavy lifting. Review the structure, your rights, and the distribution terms in the offering documents to understand exactly what your interest entitles you to.

Are there fees on QOF investments?

Yes — QOFs charge fees that reduce your net return, and they vary by fund. Fees may include acquisition fees (for sourcing and buying assets), asset-management fees (ongoing, for managing the fund), disposition fees (on sale), and for some funds carried interest or a promote (a share of profits above a threshold). So a QOF's total cost can include several fee layers. These fees compensate the sponsor or manager for the deal sourcing, compliance, execution, and management they provide, but they reduce what you ultimately keep. So scrutinize the full fee schedule in the offering documents (all layers, not just the headline figure), since fees affect your projected outcome. So don't overlook fees when evaluating a QOF — compare the fee structures of the funds you consider, understand how they affect your net return, and factor them into your decision. Your advisor can help you assess whether a fund's fees are reasonable for the value it provides.

How is my accredited status verified?

Accreditation is typically verified during the subscription process when you invest. Depending on the offering and the exemption it relies on, you'll generally either provide representations (attesting that you meet the accredited-investor criteria) or, for some offerings, undergo third-party verification (providing documentation such as tax returns, financial statements, or a letter from a CPA, attorney, or broker confirming your status). So expect to confirm your accredited status as part of subscribing to a QOF. The broker-dealer handling the offering (for Baker 1031, that's Aurora Securities, Inc., member FINRA/SIPC) facilitates this verification as part of the suitability and subscription process. So the verification is a standard step you'll complete before your investment is accepted — be prepared to demonstrate your eligibility through representations or documentation, as required by the specific offering. Your advisor can walk you through what's needed for the particular fund and exemption involved, since the requirements vary by offering.

Can I use retirement funds to invest in a QOF?

Generally, OZ investments are made with capital gains from taxable accounts, because the OZ benefit works by deferring and excluding tax on capital gains — benefits that don't apply within a tax-advantaged retirement account (where gains are already tax-deferred or potentially tax-free). So investing retirement funds in a QOF usually doesn't make sense, since you'd gain little from the OZ tax benefits inside an already-tax-advantaged account, while taking on the illiquidity and risk. There are technical structures (such as self-directed IRAs investing in alternatives), but using retirement funds for OZ investments is generally not the intended or advantageous approach. So OZ investing is typically done with taxable capital gains, not retirement funds. If you're considering using retirement assets for any alternative investment, consult your CPA and advisor about the suitability, the tax implications, and whether it makes sense — for OZ investments specifically, the tax benefits are designed for taxable capital gains, so retirement funds usually aren't the right source.

Do all QOFs have the same minimum?

No — QOF minimums vary widely by fund, because each sponsor sets its own. Retail funds often have minimums in the tens of thousands (e.g., ~$25,000 to $100,000+), some funds set lower minimums to broaden access, and institutional or specialized funds set much higher minimums (hundreds of thousands or millions). So there's no standard minimum across QOFs — it depends entirely on the specific fund's structure and target investor base. This variation means you can often find funds at different minimum levels to suit your capital, but you must check each fund's exact minimum in its offering documents. So don't assume a single figure applies — minimums range broadly, and the right fund for you depends partly on whether its minimum fits your intended allocation. So compare the minimums of the funds you're considering, and choose one whose minimum aligns with prudent sizing for your portfolio. Your advisor can help you identify funds with minimums suited to your situation.

What if a fund's minimum is more than I want to allocate?

If a fund's minimum exceeds what you prudently want to allocate to OZ investments, investing anyway could mean over-concentrating — putting too much in a single, illiquid, higher-risk holding. So rather than stretch beyond a sensible allocation, consider alternatives: look for a fund with a lower minimum that fits your intended allocation, choose a multi-asset (diversified) fund so the single investment is itself diversified, or simply allocate less to OZ if no suitable fund fits. So don't let a high minimum push you into an oversized bet. Prudent sizing — keeping OZ investments to an appropriate portion of a diversified portfolio, with capital you can commit long-term and afford to risk — should guide how much you invest, not the fund's minimum alone. So if the minimum is too large for your allocation, find a better-fitting fund or adjust your plan. Your advisor can help you identify funds with minimums that match prudent sizing for your portfolio and goals.

Will the accreditation or OZ rules change?

They can — both the accredited-investor criteria (set by securities regulations) and the OZ rules (set by tax law and regulation) are subject to change over time. The accredited-investor definition has expanded in recent years (adding professional-credential paths), and could be modified further. The OZ program has changed substantially: the 2025 One Big Beautiful Bill Act ('OZ 2.0') made the program permanent, introduced a rolling 5-year deferral for post-2026 investments, and set a new zone map effective January 1, 2027 (with the current map running through 2028). So both sets of rules are evolving. This is why you should verify the current accreditation thresholds and OZ rules before investing, rather than relying on older information — the details that affect your eligibility and benefits can shift. So treat both the accreditation criteria and the OZ rules as subject to change, confirm the current state with your advisor and CPA, and re-verify when you invest, since the program and the securities rules continue to develop.

Your accredited status is typically verified during the subscription process — you'll provide documentation or representations (and sometimes third-party verification) showing you meet the current income, net-worth, or professional-credential criteria. Then confirm the fund's exact minimum investment, fees, structure, liquidity terms, and OZ compliance details in the offering documents (the private placement memorandum and subscription materials). Working with a broker-dealer and advisor, a suitability review will also assess whether the investment fits your situation. So the steps are: verify your accredited status, review the fund's specific terms in the offering documents, and complete the suitability review. Confirming eligibility upfront prevents surprises and ensures you can actually invest in the fund you're considering. So work with your advisor and the broker-dealer to confirm your status and the fund's terms before committing — and verify the current rules, which can change.
