How to Read a REIT's FFO

By Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read

FFO — Funds From Operations — is the metric REIT investors use instead of GAAP net income. This guide explains why net income misleads for REITs, what FFO adds back, how to calculate and find FFO, how to use FFO per share and growth, and FFO's limitations.

If you try to evaluate a REIT using its GAAP net income, you'll often be misled — and that's not your fault. Real estate accounting includes a huge non-cash depreciation charge that distorts a REIT's reported earnings, making profitable, growing REITs look far less profitable than they are. To get around this, the REIT industry uses a standardized metric called Funds From Operations (FFO), defined by Nareit, which adds back real estate depreciation and removes one-time gains and losses on property sales to better reflect a REIT's recurring operating performance. Understanding how to read FFO — where to find it, how it's calculated, how to use FFO per share and its growth, and what FFO can't tell you — is essential to evaluating any REIT. This guide explains why net income misleads, what FFO adds back, how to calculate and find FFO, how to use FFO per share and growth, and FFO's limitations. This is educational information, not investment advice — verify current details and a REIT's own definitions before relying on them.

FFO is reported by listed and unlisted REITs alike, and our 2026 REIT investing guide shows where each type discloses its operating metrics.

Key takeaways
  • GAAP net income misleads for REITs because non-cash real estate depreciation understates earning power and property-sale gains distort earnings.
  • FFO = net income + real estate depreciation and amortization − gains on property sales (+ losses), per the Nareit standard.
  • Find FFO already reconciled in the REIT's earnings release and supplemental financial package, then use FFO per share and its growth.
  • FFO's limits: it doesn't subtract recurring maintenance capex (AFFO does), 'core' definitions vary by company, and it isn't a perfect cash measure.

Why Net Income Misleads for REITs

GAAP net income — the standard bottom-line profit measure for most companies — is misleading for REITs, primarily because of how real estate depreciation works. Under GAAP, a company depreciates its buildings over time, recording a large non-cash expense that reduces reported net income each year. For an ordinary company with depreciating equipment, this roughly reflects economic reality. But real estate often holds or gains value over time rather than wearing out, so the depreciation charge overstates the economic 'cost' and understates the REIT's true earning power.

Because a REIT's assets are mostly real estate, this depreciation charge is enormous — often large enough to make a profitable, cash-generating REIT show low or even negative GAAP net income, even as it collects steady rents and grows. A second distortion comes from gains and losses on property sales: when a REIT sells a building, GAAP net income spikes (or drops) with the one-time gain or loss, making earnings lumpy and hard to compare from period to period. So net income, dragged down by non-cash depreciation and jolted by one-time sale gains, paints a distorted picture of a REIT's recurring operating performance. That's the core problem FFO was created to solve.

What FFO Adds Back

FFO — Funds From Operations — is the REIT industry's standard measure of operating performance, defined by Nareit to correct the distortions in net income. The core idea is simple: start with GAAP net income, then add back the non-cash real estate depreciation and amortization (the charge that doesn't reflect a real cash cost for value-holding real estate), and remove the one-time gains on property sales (and add back losses), so that what remains reflects the REIT's recurring, real-estate operating performance rather than accounting artifacts.

So the FFO formula is: net income, plus real estate depreciation and amortization, minus gains on property sales (plus losses on sales). By adding back depreciation, FFO reverses the largest non-cash distortion in REIT accounting; by stripping out property-sale gains and losses, it removes the lumpy, one-time items that have nothing to do with recurring operations. The result is a measure that's far more representative of the cash a REIT generates from running its real estate, and one that's comparable across REITs because Nareit standardizes the definition. So FFO adds back depreciation and removes sale gains to reveal recurring operating performance that net income obscures.

Calculating and Finding FFO

Calculating FFO follows the Nareit formula directly: take net income (per GAAP), add back real estate depreciation and amortization, and subtract gains on the sale of property (adding back any losses). Some adjustments also remove impairment write-downs of real estate, since those, like depreciation, are non-cash charges on value-holding assets. The arithmetic is straightforward once you have the inputs; the work is mainly in locating the right figures.

You'll find FFO in a REIT's quarterly and annual reporting — specifically in its earnings release and, most usefully, its supplemental financial package (often just called the 'supplemental'), which most REITs publish alongside earnings. The supplemental typically presents a reconciliation that starts with net income and walks down to FFO, showing each add-back and subtraction, so you can see exactly how the REIT got there. REITs report FFO because investors and analysts expect it, so you rarely have to compute it from scratch — but checking the reconciliation helps you understand what's included. FFO Per Share & Growth

To make FFO useful for evaluating a REIT, look at it on a per-share basis and track its growth. FFO per share — total FFO divided by shares outstanding — is the REIT equivalent of earnings per share, and it's what matters to you as a shareholder, because a REIT that grows total FFO only by issuing lots of new shares isn't necessarily creating value per share. Watching FFO per share keeps the focus on the value accruing to each existing share rather than on headline totals.

FFO-per-share growth — its trend over multiple quarters and years — is one of the most important indicators of a REIT's operating health. Steady, rising FFO per share suggests a REIT is growing its recurring real-estate income on a per-share basis, which can support a growing dividend; flat or declining FFO per share is a warning sign. FFO per share also anchors valuation: the price-to-FFO (P/FFO) multiple — share price divided by FFO per share — is the REIT analog of the price-to-earnings ratio, used to compare a REIT's valuation to its peers and its own history, and one of the three pillars when you evaluate a REIT using FFO, AFFO, and NAV together. So FFO per share and its growth are the practical lenses for judging a REIT's performance and value.

Using FFO to Compare REITs

One of FFO's biggest strengths is comparability, which makes it the workhorse metric for comparing REITs. Because Nareit standardizes the FFO definition, two different REITs' reported FFO are calculated on a broadly consistent basis, so you can line up their FFO-per-share growth and their price-to-FFO multiples and make meaningful comparisons — something you can't reliably do with GAAP net income, which is distorted differently depending on each REIT's depreciation schedule and sale activity.

In practice, you use FFO to compare REITs along a few dimensions: which REIT is growing FFO per share faster, which trades at a higher or lower P/FFO multiple (and whether that gap is justified by growth, quality, or sector), and how each REIT's FFO covers its dividend. Comparing P/FFO across REITs in the same sector helps you gauge relative valuation, while comparing FFO-per-share growth helps you gauge relative momentum. The key is to compare like with like — REITs in the same sector with similar risk and growth profiles — since a data-center REIT and a net-lease REIT naturally trade at different multiples. So FFO's standardization is what makes cross-REIT comparison possible and meaningful.

Limitations of FFO

For all its usefulness, FFO has real limitations you should keep in mind. The most important is that FFO does not subtract recurring maintenance capital expenditures — the ongoing capital a REIT must spend to keep its properties competitive and leased (roofs, HVAC, tenant improvements, leasing commissions). Because these are real, recurring cash costs, FFO overstates the cash truly available to fund dividends. This is exactly the gap that AFFO (Adjusted FFO) is designed to close, by subtracting maintenance capex and other normalizations from FFO — which raises the question of whether AFFO or FFO matters more.

A second limitation is definitional drift: while Nareit's FFO is standardized, many REITs report 'adjusted,' 'core,' or 'normalized' FFO with company-specific adjustments (adding back acquisition costs, one-time items, or non-cash charges of their choosing), and these varying definitions aren't directly comparable across REITs. You have to check whether you're looking at Nareit FFO or a company-defined version. A third limitation is that FFO isn't a perfect cash-flow measure — it's an accounting-based proxy that still includes some non-cash items (like straight-line rent) and excludes others. So FFO is a powerful tool but not a complete one; it must be read alongside AFFO, the payout ratio, and a careful read of the REIT's balance sheet.

So FFO's limitations — it ignores recurring maintenance capex (AFFO addresses this), 'core' definitions vary and aren't comparable, and it isn't a perfect cash measure — mean it should be used with care and alongside other metrics. FFO and net income measure profitability differently, and for REITs the gap is large and important. Net income is the GAAP bottom line, which for a REIT is dragged down by a huge non-cash real estate depreciation charge and jolted by one-time gains or losses on property sales — so it tends to understate a REIT's recurring earning power and make results lumpy. FFO corrects these distortions: it starts with net income, adds back real estate depreciation and amortization (since real estate often holds value rather than wearing out), and removes the one-time property-sale gains (and adds back losses), leaving a measure of recurring real-estate operating performance. The practical result is that FFO is usually substantially higher than net income for a REIT, and far more representative of the cash the REIT generates from operations. Because Nareit standardizes FFO, it's also comparable across REITs in a way net income isn't. So the key difference is that net income reflects distorted GAAP accounting while FFO reflects recurring operating performance — which is why REIT investors rely on FFO, not net income, to evaluate a REIT's results.

What is FFO per share?

FFO per share is a REIT's total Funds From Operations divided by its shares outstanding — the REIT equivalent of earnings per share (EPS). It matters because, as a shareholder, you care about the operating performance accruing to each share you own, not just the headline total. A REIT can grow its total FFO simply by issuing lots of new shares to buy more properties, but if FFO per share doesn't grow, existing shareholders aren't necessarily better off. Watching FFO per share keeps the focus on per-share value creation. FFO-per-share growth — its trend over multiple quarters and years — is one of the most important indicators of a REIT's operating health: steady, rising FFO per share suggests growing recurring income on a per-share basis, which can support a growing dividend, while flat or declining FFO per share is a warning sign. FFO per share also anchors valuation through the price-to-FFO (P/FFO) multiple, the REIT analog of the P/E ratio. So FFO per share is the practical figure for judging a REIT's performance and value — always prefer it to headline total FFO.

What is P/FFO?

P/FFO is the price-to-FFO multiple — a REIT's share price divided by its FFO per share — and it's the REIT analog of the price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio. Because net income is distorted for REITs, the familiar P/E ratio isn't very useful for valuing a REIT, so investors use P/FFO instead, dividing the share price by FFO per share to gauge how much they're paying for each dollar of the REIT's recurring operating performance. P/FFO is used to compare a REIT's valuation to its peers and to its own history: a higher multiple may reflect stronger expected growth, higher quality, or a premium sector, while a lower multiple may reflect slower growth, higher risk, or being out of favor. As with any valuation multiple, the key is to compare like with like — REITs in the same sector with similar growth and risk profiles — since different sectors (for example, data centers versus net-lease) naturally command different multiples. So P/FFO is the standard REIT valuation multiple, and reading it well means comparing it across similar REITs and against a REIT's own historical range, while accounting for differences in growth, quality, and sector.

Do all REITs report FFO the same way?

Mostly yes for the headline figure, but with important caveats. Nareit defines FFO, so the great majority of REITs report a Nareit-compliant FFO calculated on a broadly consistent basis — net income, plus real estate depreciation and amortization, minus gains on property sales (plus losses) — which is what makes FFO comparable across REITs. So when you're looking at the standard 'FFO' line, you can generally trust that two REITs computed it similarly. The caveat is that many REITs also report a 'core,' 'adjusted,' 'normalized,' or 'recurring' FFO alongside the Nareit figure, adding back items of their own choosing — acquisition costs, one-time charges, certain non-cash items, or other company-specific adjustments. These company-defined versions aren't standardized and aren't directly comparable across REITs, so you have to check which line you're reading. Some REITs also differ in how they treat impairments or particular non-recurring items even within their Nareit FFO. So the practical rule is to confirm you're comparing Nareit FFO to Nareit FFO when you compare REITs, and to read the reconciliation to understand any company-specific adjustments before relying on a 'core' or 'adjusted' figure. Standardization applies to the base FFO, not to every variant a REIT may present.

Why is FFO better than net income for REITs?

FFO is better than net income for evaluating REITs because it corrects two distortions that make net income unrepresentative. First, it adds back real estate depreciation and amortization — the large non-cash charge that GAAP requires but that doesn't reflect economic reality for real estate that often holds or gains value. This add-back reverses the single biggest reason a profitable REIT can show low or negative net income. Second, FFO removes one-time gains and losses on property sales, which otherwise make net income lumpy and hard to compare from period to period. The result is a measure of a REIT's recurring real-estate operating performance — far closer to the cash the REIT actually generates from running its properties. On top of that, because Nareit standardizes the FFO definition, FFO is comparable across REITs, while net income is distorted differently for each REIT depending on its depreciation schedule and sale activity. So FFO is better because it strips out non-cash depreciation and one-time sale gains to reveal recurring performance, and because its standardization enables meaningful comparison — two things net income can't do for a REIT.

Does FFO measure cash flow exactly?

No — FFO is a useful proxy for a REIT's operating cash flow, but it isn't a perfect or exact cash-flow measure. FFO is an accounting-based metric: it starts from GAAP net income and makes specific adjustments (adding back real estate depreciation and amortization, removing property-sale gains and losses), but it still includes some non-cash items and excludes others. For example, FFO doesn't subtract recurring maintenance capital expenditures — the real, ongoing cash a REIT must spend to keep its properties competitive and leased — so it overstates the cash truly available to fund dividends. It also typically includes straight-line rent adjustments, which are non-cash. These gaps are exactly why analysts also use AFFO (Adjusted FFO), which subtracts maintenance capex and straight-line rent to better approximate distributable cash. So FFO should be understood as a standardized measure of recurring operating performance, not as exact cash flow. It's the right starting point for evaluating a REIT, but for questions about cash and dividend coverage you should look to AFFO and the actual cash-flow statement alongside it. Use FFO knowing what it does and doesn't capture.

What are the limitations of FFO?

FFO has several limitations to keep in mind. The most important is that it does not subtract recurring maintenance capital expenditures — the ongoing capital a REIT must spend to keep properties competitive and leased (roofs, HVAC, tenant improvements, leasing commissions). Because these are real, recurring cash costs, FFO overstates the cash truly available to fund dividends; AFFO (Adjusted FFO) is designed to close this gap. A second limitation is definitional drift: while Nareit's FFO is standardized, many REITs also report 'adjusted,' 'core,' or 'normalized' FFO with company-specific adjustments that aren't directly comparable across REITs, so you must check which version you're reading. A third limitation is that FFO isn't a perfect cash-flow measure — it's an accounting proxy that still includes some non-cash items (like straight-line rent) and excludes others. So while FFO is the right primary metric for a REIT's operating performance, it's powerful but incomplete: read it alongside AFFO (for distributable cash and dividend coverage), the payout ratio, and the balance sheet. Knowing FFO's limits keeps you from over-relying on a single number when evaluating a REIT.

What is the difference between FFO and AFFO?

FFO and AFFO are related REIT metrics, with AFFO building on FFO to better approximate distributable cash. FFO (Funds From Operations) starts with net income, adds back real estate depreciation and amortization, and removes property-sale gains, giving a standardized measure of recurring operating performance. AFFO (Adjusted FFO) takes FFO and goes further, subtracting recurring maintenance capital expenditures and straight-line rent adjustments (and sometimes other normalizations), so it more closely reflects the actual cash available to fund the dividend. The key trade-off is standardization versus accuracy: FFO is more standardized (Nareit defines it) and therefore more comparable across REITs, while AFFO is more economically accurate as a cash measure but less standardized, since companies define their maintenance-capex and other adjustments differently. In practice, investors use both — FFO for comparability and valuation (P/FFO), and AFFO for assessing dividend sustainability via the AFFO payout ratio. So the difference is that FFO measures recurring operating performance in a comparable way, while AFFO refines FFO into a closer estimate of distributable cash — and reading both gives a fuller picture of a REIT than either alone.

Should I rely only on FFO to evaluate a REIT?

No — FFO is an essential metric, but you shouldn't rely on it alone. FFO is the right primary measure of a REIT's recurring operating performance and the basis for the P/FFO valuation multiple, but it has limitations: it ignores recurring maintenance capex (so it overstates distributable cash), 'core' versions vary by company, and it isn't a perfect cash measure. A sound REIT analysis uses FFO alongside several other tools. AFFO refines FFO into a closer estimate of distributable cash and supports the dividend-coverage analysis (the AFFO payout ratio). The balance sheet — leverage, debt maturities, and interest coverage — reveals financial risk that FFO doesn't capture. NAV (net asset value) estimates the underlying real estate value per share. And property-level metrics like occupancy, same-store NOI growth, and lease expirations show operational health. So treat FFO as the starting point and the key per-share growth and valuation gauge, but combine it with AFFO, the payout ratio, the balance sheet, NAV, and operating fundamentals for a complete view. Relying on any single metric — even one as useful as FFO — risks missing important parts of the picture.

Is higher FFO always better?

Not necessarily — higher FFO is generally good, but the figure needs context to be meaningful. First, focus on FFO per share rather than total FFO: a REIT can grow total FFO simply by issuing new shares to buy properties, but if FFO per share doesn't grow, existing shareholders aren't better off, so per-share growth is what matters. Second, consider how the FFO was achieved — sustainable, recurring growth from rising rents and good operations is more valuable than a one-time bump, and FFO supported by excessive leverage carries more risk. Third, remember FFO overstates distributable cash because it ignores maintenance capex, so a high FFO doesn't automatically mean a well-covered dividend; check AFFO and the payout ratio for that. Fourth, valuation matters — a REIT with strong FFO growth may already trade at a high P/FFO multiple that prices it in. So higher FFO per share, grown sustainably and at a reasonable valuation, is what to look for, not simply a bigger headline number. Context — per-share basis, quality and sustainability of growth, leverage, dividend coverage, and valuation — determines whether higher FFO is actually better.
