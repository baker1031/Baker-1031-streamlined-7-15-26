The Mineral & Royalty Owner's 1031 Exchange Checklist

By Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 15 min read

A 1031 exchange is a sequence, not a leap. This checklist walks mineral and royalty owners through every step — confirming eligibility, engaging the QI, tracking the deadlines, gathering the documents, and filing — so nothing falls through the cracks.

A mineral 1031 exchange has a lot of moving parts, but it's fundamentally a sequence — a series of steps that, done in order and on time, let you sell your mineral rights and defer the capital gains. The owners who struggle are the ones who treat it as a leap, improvising as they go; the owners who succeed work through it methodically. This checklist lays out that sequence for mineral and royalty owners, from the threshold eligibility question through the final tax filing, with the mineral-specific items (division orders, royalty checks, conveyances) flagged along the way. Use it as a roadmap to keep your exchange on track and to make sure nothing essential falls through the cracks. Each step builds on the one before, and the discipline of working through them in order is most of what separates a smooth exchange from a stressful one.

For the reasoning behind each item on this list, keep our in-depth mineral and royalty exchange guide open alongside it.

Key takeaways
  • Step 1: Confirm the interest qualifies (characterize the conveyance) — eligibility can't be fixed later.
  • Step 2: Engage a vetted, oil-and-gas-experienced QI before closing, and arrange trailing-income handling.
  • Step 3: Track the concurrent 45- and 180-day deadlines, identify a DST backup, and watch the late-year trap.
  • Steps 4-5: Gather deeds, division orders, statements, and title; then file Form 8824 and retain the full file.

Step 1: Confirm your interest qualifies

The first step, before anything else, is confirming that your interest qualifies for a 1031 exchange. Have a tax adviser or oil and gas attorney read the conveyance that created your interest and characterize it: is it a perpetual royalty or fee mineral interest (qualifies cleanly), an override or net profits interest (depends on the facts), a term interest (uncertain), or a production payment (treated as debt, doesn't qualify)? For a working interest, identify the equipment component, which is non-qualifying personal property.

This confirmation is the gating step because eligibility can't be fixed after the sale — exchanging a non-qualifying interest leads to a disallowed exchange and the full tax plus penalties. For a clean perpetual royalty, the authority is settled and the confirmation is quick and reassuring. For borderline interests, get a written opinion that defines whether it qualifies and with what confidence, so you know the risk before committing.

Document the eligibility determination as part of your file. Whether it's a quick confirmation for a clean royalty or a formal opinion for a borderline interest, having the characterization in writing supports the exchange's defensibility if it's ever examined. With eligibility confirmed and documented, you have the foundation for everything that follows; without it, every later step rests on an unverified assumption. This is the step that most determines whether the exchange is even possible, so it comes first.

Step 2: Engage a QI before closing

The second step is engaging a qualified intermediary before your sale closes — and the timing is critical. The QI must be in place, assigned into your purchase and sale contract, with closing instructions directing the proceeds to its segregated account, all before the closing happens. If you close first and the proceeds reach you, you've taken receipt and the exchange is dead with no fix. Engaging the QI early is non-negotiable.

Choose the QI carefully. Confirm fund security — segregated qualified escrow or trust accounts, fidelity bonding, errors-and-omissions insurance, and a clean operating history — since the funds may be held for months and are irreplaceable if mishandled. For minerals, also confirm the QI has oil and gas experience and understands mineral conveyances, division orders, and trailing royalty checks. A QI unfamiliar with minerals may miss the wrinkles that matter.

Sort out the mineral-specific income handling with the QI at this stage. Royalty checks for pre-closing production will arrive after closing and must be routed per the QI's and CPA's instructions so they don't blur into exchange proceeds and create a constructive-receipt problem. Establishing how trailing income and the division-order transition will be handled — before closing — is the mineral-specific part of engaging the QI. With the QI engaged, vetted, and instructed, the custodial foundation of the exchange is in place.

Engage the QI before the sale closes — assigned into the contract, with proceeds directed to its account. Close first, and the exchange is dead with no fix.

Step 3: Track the 45/180-day deadlines

The third step is rigorously tracking the two deadlines that begin the moment your sale closes: 45 days to identify replacement property in writing, and 180 days to close on it. Both clocks start together and run concurrently, are absolute (including weekends and holidays), and can't be extended on request. Mark these dates on every calendar the instant a closing date is set, and set reminders well ahead of each.

Within the 45-day window, identify your replacement property — a signed written notice unambiguously describing each candidate, delivered to the QI by day 45. For minerals, where sourcing is slow, this is the highest-pressure step, which is why you should enter the window with candidates already lined up (from Step 1's planning) and identify a fast-closing DST backup under the 3-property rule. After day 45, nothing can be added, so the identification must be complete and correct.

Watch two timing traps. First, the clocks are concurrent — identifying on day 45 leaves at most 135 days to close, not a fresh 180. Second, a late-year sale's tax-return due date can shorten the 180-day window unless you file an extension, which your CPA should flag. Building a backward-planned timeline with buffer — aiming to identify well before day 45 and to have your primary closing on track by day 120, with the DST backup ready — is how you keep these absolute deadlines manageable.

Step 4: Gather deeds, division orders & statements

The fourth step is gathering the documents the exchange requires, several of which are mineral-specific. For the relinquished interest, you'll need the conveyance that created it (the deed, reservation, or assignment — also used for eligibility in Step 1), the current division orders showing your decimal interest and payment details, and recent royalty or production statements establishing the income and supporting the valuation. These documents underpin the sale, the valuation, and the eligibility analysis.

You'll also need the instruments to convey the relinquished interest to the buyer — a mineral deed or assignment prepared under the relevant state's law — and, if acquiring direct replacement minerals, the conveyances for those. Title documentation is important, since mineral title can be fractured or encumbered; confirming clear title to what you're selling (and buying) may require specialized title work. For a DST replacement, you'll need the offering documents (the private placement memorandum) and subscription paperwork instead.

Organize these documents early, because assembling them under deadline pressure is stressful and error-prone. The conveyance and title work in particular can take time, especially for older or fractured interests. Having the deeds, division orders, statements, valuations, and title documentation in order — alongside the QI agreement, the written identification, and any eligibility opinion — gives you a complete, defensible file. This organized documentation is what supports both a smooth closing and the final tax filing, and it's the kind of preparation that prevents last-minute scrambles.

Step 5: File Form 8824 correctly

The fifth and final step is reporting the exchange to the IRS on Form 8824, Like-Kind Exchanges, filed with your tax return for the year the relinquished property was sold. The form documents the properties exchanged, the dates, the values, any boot, and the calculation of deferred and recognized gain and your new carryover basis. Even a fully deferred exchange must be reported — the deferral is claimed through this filing, not granted automatically.

For minerals, the filing also captures any taxable components: boot you took, the equipment portion if you relinquished a working interest, depletion recapture triggered by any boot, and the carryover of your (often low) basis into the replacement, which governs future depletion or depreciation. Your CPA prepares Form 8824 as part of the return — which is why the CPA should have been involved from Step 1, not introduced at filing time. Errors or omissions here can invite questions even when the exchange itself was valid.

Retain your complete file after filing: the eligibility opinion, the QI agreement, the closing statements for both legs, the written identification, the valuations, the conveyances, and Form 8824. A mineral exchange may attract more scrutiny than a routine real estate swap, and this organized documentation demonstrates a qualifying interest was exchanged through clean mechanics. With Form 8824 filed and the file retained, the exchange is complete — the gain deferred, the sequence finished, and your documentation ready to support it.

The checklist at a glance

Pulled together, the mineral exchange checklist is a clear sequence you can work through and check off. Before listing or selling, the preparatory items: confirm eligibility by characterizing the interest; assemble your team (QI, CPA, advisor, attorney); design your replacement strategy including a fast-closing DST backup; and gather your conveyance, division orders, and statements. These pre-sale steps are where the exchange is won or lost, because they can't be done under deadline pressure later.

Confirm the interest qualifies — have a tax adviser characterize the conveyance (perpetual royalty, fee minerals, override, NPI, term interest, or production payment).

Assemble the team: an oil-and-gas-experienced qualified intermediary, a CPA who understands mineral taxation, an advisor, and an attorney.

Engage the QI before closing, assigned into the contract, with proceeds directed to its segregated account.

Arrange how trailing royalty checks and the division-order transition will be handled so they don't create constructive receipt.

Design your replacement strategy and line up candidates, including a fast-closing royalty-pool or real estate DST backup.

Mark day 45 and day 180 the moment a closing date is set; file a tax-return extension if selling late in the year.

Identify replacement property in writing to the QI by day 45 (3-property rule for a primary plus backups).

Gather deeds, division orders, royalty statements, valuations, and title documentation; confirm clear title.

Close on the identified replacement within 180 days, reinvesting all proceeds to avoid boot.

File Form 8824 with that year's return, and retain the complete documentation file.

The checklist as a timeline

It helps to map the checklist onto a timeline, because the steps aren't evenly spaced — most of the important work happens before the sale, then the deadlines compress everything after. In the pre-sale phase (weeks or months before listing), you confirm eligibility, assemble your team, design your replacement strategy with a backup, and gather your documents. This is the longest and most important phase, and it's entirely under your control, which is why starting early is the single biggest predictor of a smooth exchange.

The sale itself is the pivot point. You ensure the QI is engaged and assigned, the proceeds are directed to its account, and the mineral-specific income handling is arranged — all before closing. The day the sale closes is day zero, starting the 45- and 180-day clocks. From this moment, the timeline tightens dramatically, and the preparation you did beforehand determines how comfortably you navigate it.

The post-sale phase runs on the deadlines: identify by day 45 (ideally earlier, with buffer), close by day 180 (with a checkpoint around day 120 to pivot to the backup if needed), then file Form 8824 with that year's return and retain your file. Seeing the checklist as a timeline — long preparation, a well-managed sale, then disciplined execution against the clocks — clarifies why the pre-sale steps carry so much weight. The owners who work the checklist as an early, deliberate sequence rather than a post-sale scramble are the ones whose exchanges simply work.

Frequently asked questions

What's the first step in a mineral 1031 exchange?

Confirming your interest qualifies. Have a tax adviser or oil and gas attorney characterize the conveyance — perpetual royalty, fee minerals, override, NPI, term interest, or production payment — and confirm it's a qualifying perpetual real-property interest. This is the gating step because eligibility can't be fixed after the sale; everything else assumes a qualifying interest.

When must I engage the qualified intermediary?

Before the sale closes. The QI must be assigned into the contract and the closing instructions must direct the proceeds to its segregated account, all before closing. If you close first and the proceeds reach you, the exchange is dead with no fix. Engaging the QI early is non-negotiable, and for minerals, choose one with oil and gas experience.

What are the deadlines I need to track?

From the day your sale closes, 45 days to identify replacement property in writing and 180 days to close on it. Both clocks start together and run concurrently, are absolute, and can't be extended on request. Mark them the instant a closing date is set, identify a DST backup, and watch the late-year tax-return-date trap (file an extension).

What documents do I need for a mineral exchange?

The conveyance that created your interest, current division orders, recent royalty/production statements, the mineral deed or assignment to convey the interest, title documentation, and any eligibility opinion. For a DST replacement, the offering documents and subscription paperwork. Plus the QI agreement, the written identification, valuations, and ultimately Form 8824.

What is Form 8824?

The IRS form, Like-Kind Exchanges, filed with your return for the year of the relinquished sale. It reports the properties, dates, values, any boot, and the deferred and recognized gain and carryover basis. Even a fully deferred exchange must be reported — the deferral is claimed through this filing, which your CPA prepares.

Why does the order of steps matter?

Because each step builds on the one before, and several can't be done late. Eligibility must be confirmed before committing; the QI must be engaged before closing; identification must happen by day 45. Working the checklist in order, with the preparatory steps done before selling, is what makes the exchange smooth — improvising out of order is how exchanges fail.

How are trailing royalty checks handled?

Royalty checks for pre-closing production arrive after closing and must be routed per the QI's and CPA's instructions so they don't blur into exchange proceeds and create constructive receipt. Arrange this handling with the QI before closing — it's a mineral-specific item on the checklist that a real estate QI might overlook.

What's the most important pre-sale step?

Confirming eligibility, closely followed by engaging the QI and lining up replacements with a backup. The pre-sale steps — eligibility, team, replacement strategy, document gathering — are where the exchange is won or lost, because they can't be done under deadline pressure after the sale. Preparation before listing is the single biggest predictor of success.

Do I need a DST backup on the checklist?

Strongly recommended for minerals. Because sourcing qualifying minerals within 45 days is hard, identifying a fast-closing royalty-pool or real estate DST as a backup under the 3-property rule guarantees you a viable replacement if a direct deal stalls. It's cheap insurance against a failed exchange and is standard practice for oil and gas exchangers.

Why confirm clear title?

Because mineral title can be fractured or encumbered, you may own less than you think, or the interest may have title issues that complicate the sale or the buyer's diligence. Confirming clear title — through specialized title work, especially for older or fractured interests — ensures you're selling (and buying) exactly what you believe, supporting a clean closing.

When should my CPA get involved?

From Step 1, before the sale — not at filing time. The CPA confirms eligibility with you, establishes your basis, estimates the gain and four-layer tax, models the carryover basis, flags the late-year deadline trap, and ultimately prepares Form 8824. Introducing the CPA only at filing time misses the planning where most of the value is, so engage them early.

What should I keep after the exchange?

Your complete file: the eligibility opinion, QI agreement, closing statements for both legs, written identification, valuations, conveyances, and Form 8824. A mineral exchange may attract more scrutiny than a routine real estate swap, and this organized documentation demonstrates a qualifying interest was exchanged through clean mechanics if it's ever examined. Retain it well beyond the filing.

How far in advance should I start the checklist?

Ideally weeks or months before listing. The pre-sale phase — confirming eligibility, assembling the team, designing the replacement strategy, gathering documents — is the longest and most important, and it's entirely under your control. Most of the work that determines success happens before the sale, so the earlier you begin, the more comfortably you navigate the deadlines that follow.

What's the single biggest predictor of a smooth exchange?

Starting the pre-sale steps early. The owners whose exchanges simply work are the ones who confirm eligibility, assemble their team, line up replacements with a backup, and gather documents before listing — not those who scramble after selling. The pre-sale phase is long and under your control; using it well is what makes the compressed post-sale timeline manageable.

Can I use this checklist for exchanging into minerals?

Largely, yes — the sequence (confirm eligibility, engage the QI, track deadlines, gather documents, file) applies whether minerals are relinquished or acquired. When buying minerals, the eligibility confirmation and document gathering focus on the replacement interest, and sourcing under the 45-day clock is the high-pressure step. The framework adapts to either direction of a mineral exchange.

What if I'm missing the original conveyance document?

Locate it before proceeding — through your files or county records — because you can't confirm eligibility or convey the interest without it. For inherited or long-held interests, tracking down the original instrument is sometimes the first real task. An oil and gas attorney can help retrieve and interpret it. Don't skip this; the conveyance underpins both eligibility (Step 1) and the documentation (Step 4).

Who keeps the checklist on track day to day?

Ideally your advisor, coordinating with the QI, CPA, and attorney. While you own the decisions, an experienced advisor manages the sequence — confirming each step is done and on time, tracking the deadlines, and flagging the mineral-specific items. For owners who'd rather not manage the moving parts themselves, having an advisor run the checklist is much of the value of engaging one.

Is the checklist different for a working interest?

The sequence is the same, but a working interest adds steps: identifying and allocating the non-qualifying equipment (taxable personal property), and accounting for its depreciation recapture in the tax filing. Eligibility confirmation also covers the real-property component specifically. The checklist still applies, with the equipment carve-out folded into the eligibility, document, and filing steps — work your CPA and advisor handle.

What happens if I complete the checklist but miss a deadline?

Missing the 45-day or 180-day deadline generally fails the exchange regardless of how well the other steps were done, making the sale taxable. This is why the deadline-tracking step (Step 3) is so critical and why a fast-closing DST backup matters. Completing every other step perfectly doesn't help if a deadline is missed, so the timeline discipline is non-negotiable.
