Net Profits Interest (NPI): Does It Qualify for 1031?

By Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read

A net profits interest sits between a royalty and a working interest, paying a share of profits rather than gross production. Whether it's real property for 1031 purposes depends on how it's structured and documented — here's the analysis, the open questions, and how to approach an NPI exchange carefully.

Of all the oil and gas interests, the net profits interest is among the most fact-dependent for 1031 purposes. An NPI pays its holder a share of the net profits from a mineral property — gross revenue minus specified costs — which gives it a hybrid character: it's cost-free like a royalty in that the holder isn't personally liable for operating expenses, yet its payment depends on the profitability calculation in a way a gross royalty's doesn't. That hybrid nature is exactly what makes its like-kind treatment uncertain. An NPI carved out of a real-property interest can itself be a real-property interest eligible for exchange, but the way it's structured — how the 'net profits' are computed, whether it has a defined term, and how closely it resembles a mere contractual right — can push it toward or away from qualification. This guide explains what an NPI is, how it compares to royalties and working interests, the real-property question at its core, and how to approach an NPI exchange with appropriate caution.

What is a net profits interest?

A net profits interest is a share of the net profits from an oil and gas property, carved out of a working interest or a mineral interest. The NPI holder receives a percentage of the revenue remaining after specified costs are deducted — typically the operating costs, and sometimes a share of development costs, depending on how the instrument defines 'net profits.' Unlike a working-interest owner, the NPI holder is not personally obligated to fund those costs; the costs are simply netted against revenue before the holder's share is calculated.

This makes the NPI a hybrid. Like a royalty, it's cost-free in the sense that the holder never receives a bill or a capital call — the worst case is that there are no net profits in a given period, so no payment, but the holder is never out of pocket. Like a working interest, though, its payment is sensitive to the cost side of the operation, because it's measured on net rather than gross. An NPI holder shares, in effect, in the profitability of the property rather than simply in its production.

NPIs are used in a variety of deal structures — to compensate a party, to share upside without sharing liability, or to monetize part of a property's economics. Their precise terms vary enormously from one instrument to the next: the cost definitions, whether the interest is perpetual or term-limited, and how it's carved out all differ. That variability is the heart of the 1031 challenge, because eligibility depends on these specifics, not on the 'NPI' label.

NPI vs. royalty vs. working interest

Placing the NPI alongside its cousins clarifies its character. A royalty interest pays a share of gross production (or its value) free of all costs — the cleanest, most passive interest, and the one most comfortably treated as real property. A working interest pays the costs and keeps the revenue after royalties — the active, cost-bearing operating interest, part real property and part taxable equipment. The NPI sits between them: cost-free to the holder like a royalty, but cost-sensitive in its calculation like a working interest.

The key practical difference from a royalty is the cost deduction. A gross royalty owner doesn't care what production costs — they get their percentage off the top. An NPI holder's payment shrinks when costs rise and grows when costs fall, because it's measured on the net. This ties the NPI's economics to the property's profitability, making it more volatile and more dependent on operating efficiency than a gross royalty, even though both are cost-free to the holder in the sense of no personal liability.

The difference from a working interest is liability and operations. A working-interest owner can be called for capital and bears operational and environmental liability; an NPI holder bears none of that. So the NPI captures some of the profit-sharing of a working interest without its burdens — an attractive position economically, but one whose hybrid nature is precisely what makes its tax characterization, and therefore its 1031 eligibility, a genuine question rather than a settled answer.

Is an NPI a real-property interest?

The 1031 question for an NPI reduces to whether it's an interest in real property. Oil and gas interests carved out of a mineral or leasehold estate can be real property under state law, and an NPI that functions as a continuing interest in the production of real property may qualify on that basis. The favorable case is an NPI that is perpetual, carved from a real-property interest, and that operates economically like a royalty with a cost adjustment — such an interest looks much like the real-property interests the like-kind rules are comfortable with.

The uncertainty arises when the NPI's structure pushes it toward looking like a contractual or financing arrangement rather than a continuing interest in real property. If the 'net profits' computation is elaborate, if the interest is term-limited or capped, or if it functions in substance like a right to a stream of payments rather than an ownership stake in producing minerals, it can resemble a production payment (treated as debt under Section 636) or a mere contract right — neither of which is like-kind to real estate. The same NPI label can therefore sit on either side of the line depending on its terms.

Because state law characterization and federal tax characterization both matter, and because NPIs vary so widely, there is no blanket answer to 'does an NPI qualify?' The honest answer is that some do and some don't, and the determination requires reading the specific instrument against the authorities. This is a more uncertain area than perpetual royalties (which qualify cleanly) or production payments (which clearly don't), and it's precisely the kind of interest for which a professional opinion is not optional.

1031 treatment and the open questions

Where an NPI is structured as a perpetual interest carved from a real-property mineral or leasehold estate, and functions economically like a cost-adjusted royalty, the better view is generally that it's a real-property interest capable of like-kind exchange — exchangeable for other real property such as royalties, conventional real estate, or a DST. Practitioners often treat well-structured, perpetual NPIs over real-property interests as qualifying, by analogy to the treatment of royalties and overrides.

But several open questions counsel caution. The treatment can hinge on whether the NPI is perpetual or term-limited; on whether the cost computations make it resemble a financing arrangement; on how the relevant state characterizes the interest; and on the absence of direct, on-point IRS guidance for every NPI structure. Unlike the clean authority for royalties or the clear disqualification of production payments, the NPI lives in a zone where reasonable advisers analyze the specific facts rather than apply a settled rule. That uncertainty is itself a risk an owner accepts when exchanging an NPI.

The practical consequence is that an NPI exchange should rest on a tax adviser's written opinion specific to the instrument, and the owner should understand the degree of confidence behind it. A strongly-qualifying NPI may support an exchange with comfort; a borderline one may carry meaningful risk that the IRS could disagree, with the consequence of a disallowed exchange and the full tax plus penalties. Knowing where on that spectrum your NPI falls — before you sell — is the essential planning step.

Documenting the exchange

Given the fact-specific nature of NPI eligibility, documentation is more important here than for almost any other oil and gas interest. Start with the instrument that created the NPI — its definition of net profits, its term, how it was carved out, and the underlying real-property interest it derives from. A tax adviser reads this against the authorities and issues an opinion on whether the NPI qualifies as exchangeable real property, and with what level of confidence. This opinion is the foundation of the exchange's defensibility.

From there, the standard 1031 mechanics apply, executed cleanly: a qualified intermediary engaged before the sale closes so you never take constructive receipt, the QI assigned into the contract, written identification of like-kind replacement property within 45 days, and closing within 180 days with full reinvestment to avoid boot. As with all oil and gas exchanges, identify a fast-closing backup, route any trailing income carefully, and keep a defensible valuation. Each step should be documented thoroughly, because an NPI exchange may attract more scrutiny than a routine one.

The replacement side deserves the same rigor. If you exchange an NPI for another oil and gas interest, that interest must itself qualify — don't assume two profit-linked interests are automatically like-kind. Many NPI holders use the exchange to move into cleaner assets: a perpetual royalty, conventional real estate, or a DST, which removes the characterization uncertainty going forward. Whatever the replacement, the through-line is documentation: the opinion, the instrument, the QI agreement, the identification, and the closing records together demonstrate that a qualifying interest was exchanged through clean mechanics — which is exactly what an NPI exchange needs to stand up.

Alternatives if your NPI doesn't qualify

If a tax adviser concludes your net profits interest doesn't qualify as exchangeable real property — because it's term-limited, structured like a financing arrangement, or characterized as a contract right under the relevant state law — a straight 1031 isn't available for it, but you still have options. The first is simply to recognize that part of your value won't defer and plan the taxable sale deliberately, perhaps timing it to manage your bracket or pairing it with offsetting losses elsewhere in your portfolio with your CPA's guidance.

A second option is restructuring before any sale. In some cases, with careful planning and counsel, an interest can be modified or the transaction structured so that a qualifying real-property interest is what's exchanged. This is delicate work that must be done well in advance and only on sound legal advice — you can't retroactively convert a non-qualifying interest after a sale — but for a borderline NPI it's sometimes possible to put the exchange on firmer footing before committing.

A third option is to consider other deferral tools for the taxable portion. An installment sale can spread the gain over the years you receive payments; a Qualified Opportunity Fund can defer gain from any source by reinvesting the gain (not the full proceeds); and charitable strategies can reduce tax for owners with philanthropic intent. None of these is a 1031, and each has its own rules and trade-offs, but they can soften the tax on an NPI that won't qualify for like-kind treatment. The unifying lesson is that knowing your NPI's status before you sell — qualifying, borderline, or non-qualifying — is what lets you choose the right path rather than discovering the problem on your tax return.

Frequently asked questions

What is a net profits interest (NPI)?

A share of the net profits from an oil and gas property — revenue minus specified costs — carved out of a working or mineral interest. The holder receives a percentage after costs are netted but isn't personally liable for those costs. It's cost-free to the holder like a royalty, but cost-sensitive in its calculation like a working interest.

Does a net profits interest qualify for a 1031 exchange?

It depends on its structure. A perpetual NPI carved from a real-property interest that functions like a cost-adjusted royalty often qualifies as exchangeable real property. A term-limited NPI, or one whose computation makes it resemble a financing arrangement, may not. Because NPIs vary widely, eligibility is fact-specific and requires a tax adviser's opinion.

How is an NPI different from a royalty?

A gross royalty pays a share of production off the top, regardless of costs — the cleanest, most passive interest. An NPI pays a share of net profits, so its payment shrinks when costs rise and grows when they fall. Both are cost-free to the holder (no personal liability), but the NPI's payment depends on the property's profitability, making it more volatile.

How is an NPI different from a working interest?

A working interest bears operating costs and capital calls and carries operational and environmental liability — it's the active operating interest. An NPI holder bears none of that liability; costs are simply netted against revenue before the holder's share is computed. The NPI captures some profit-sharing of a working interest without its burdens.

Why is the NPI's 1031 status uncertain?

Because the NPI is a hybrid. Whether it's an interest in real property — and therefore like-kind to other real estate — depends on its term, how 'net profits' are computed, the relevant state's characterization, and how closely it resembles a contractual or financing arrangement. There isn't blanket guidance for every NPI structure, so the analysis is fact-specific.

When is an NPI most likely to qualify?

When it's perpetual, carved from a real-property mineral or leasehold estate, and functions economically like a cost-adjusted royalty rather than a defined stream of payments. Under those conditions it resembles the real-property interests the like-kind rules accept. A perpetual, royalty-like NPI over real property is the favorable case.

When might an NPI not qualify?

When it's term-limited or capped, when its net-profits computation makes it resemble a production payment (debt under §636) or a mere contract right, or when state law treats it as something other than a real-property interest. In those cases it may not be like-kind to real estate, and an exchange shouldn't proceed without a careful opinion — or may not be available at all.

Do I need a tax opinion to exchange an NPI?

Strongly advisable — arguably essential. Because NPI eligibility is genuinely uncertain and instrument-specific, a written opinion from a tax adviser analyzing your particular NPI is the gating step. It tells you whether the interest qualifies and with what confidence, defining the risk you accept. The cost is trivial next to a disallowed exchange's tax and penalties.

Can I exchange an NPI for real estate or a DST?

If the NPI qualifies as real property, yes — it's like-kind to conventional investment real estate or a DST. Many NPI holders use the exchange to move into a cleaner asset (a perpetual royalty, real estate, or a DST), which removes the characterization uncertainty going forward. The relinquished NPI must qualify, and the replacement must be like-kind real property.

Can I exchange one NPI for another?

Potentially, but both must be analyzed — don't assume two profit-linked interests are automatically like-kind. The relinquished NPI must qualify as real property, and the replacement NPI must too. Given the uncertainty around NPIs, many holders prefer to exchange into a cleaner, clearly-qualifying replacement rather than another NPI.

How should I document an NPI exchange?

Thoroughly. Keep the instrument creating the NPI, the tax adviser's eligibility opinion, the qualified intermediary agreement, the written identification, a defensible valuation, and the closing records. Because an NPI exchange may attract more scrutiny than a routine one, the documentation must clearly show a qualifying interest was exchanged through clean mechanics.

Is an NPI exchange riskier than a royalty exchange?

From an eligibility standpoint, yes. A perpetual royalty qualifies cleanly under well-settled authority, while an NPI's qualification depends on its specific structure and sits in a more uncertain zone. That added risk is why an NPI exchange needs a written opinion and careful documentation, and why some holders choose to exchange into a cleaner asset to remove the uncertainty.

What if my NPI doesn't qualify for a 1031?

You have alternatives: plan a deliberate taxable sale (timing it to manage your bracket or pairing it with offsetting losses), restructure before any sale with counsel so a qualifying interest is exchanged, or use other deferral tools for the taxable portion — an installment sale, a Qualified Opportunity Fund, or charitable strategies. Knowing your NPI's status before you sell lets you choose the right path.

Can I restructure a non-qualifying NPI to qualify?

Sometimes, with careful planning and legal advice done well in advance. In some cases an interest can be modified, or the transaction structured, so that a qualifying real-property interest is what's exchanged. But you can't retroactively convert a non-qualifying interest after a sale — the restructuring must happen before you commit, and only on sound counsel given the eligibility uncertainty.

Does the 'net profits' computation affect eligibility?

It can. An elaborate net-profits computation — especially one that deducts development and capital costs in a way that makes the interest resemble a financing arrangement or a defined payment stream — pushes the NPI away from looking like a continuing real-property interest. A simpler computation that nets only operating costs, leaving the interest functioning like a cost-adjusted royalty, supports the real-property characterization.

Can I exchange an NPI into a royalty-pool DST?

If the NPI qualifies as real property, yes — a royalty-pool DST is like-kind replacement property, and moving into it removes the characterization uncertainty going forward while diversifying across many interests. Many NPI holders prefer to exchange into a cleaner, clearly-qualifying asset like a DST or a perpetual royalty rather than into another NPI.

How does state law affect my NPI's 1031 status?

State law determines whether the NPI is characterized as a real-property interest, which is the starting point for federal like-kind treatment. Because oil and gas property law varies by state, the same NPI structure can be characterized differently depending on where the minerals sit. Your tax adviser's opinion should account for the relevant state's treatment, not just federal principles.

Is there IRS guidance specifically on NPIs and 1031?

There's authority on royalties and production payments, but not clean, on-point guidance covering every NPI structure for like-kind purposes. That gap is precisely why NPI eligibility is analyzed case by case against the existing authorities rather than resolved by a settled rule, and why a written professional opinion specific to your instrument is so important before exchanging.

Should I just sell my NPI and pay the tax?

It depends on the gain at stake and the confidence in your eligibility opinion. If the NPI clearly qualifies and the deferral is meaningful, exchanging makes sense. If it's borderline and the gain is modest, a deliberate taxable sale may be simpler and less risky than an aggressive exchange the IRS could challenge. Model both with your CPA before deciding.
