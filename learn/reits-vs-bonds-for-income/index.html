REITs vs. Bonds for Income

By Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read

REITs and bonds are both used to generate income, but they behave very differently. This guide compares their yield and risk, interest-rate sensitivity, inflation protection, volatility differences, and how balancing REITs and bonds can build a more resilient income portfolio.

Income investors frequently weigh REITs against bonds — and for good reason, since both are cornerstones of income-oriented portfolios. But they're fundamentally different instruments. A bond is a contractual loan that pays a fixed coupon and returns your principal at maturity: lower-yielding, more stable, and predictable, but generally offering no inflation protection (except TIPS) and a fixed payment. A REIT is an equity investment in real estate that offers higher yield potential plus the possibility of growth and an inflation consideration, but with more volatility and real price risk. Both are interest-rate sensitive — bond prices and REIT valuations tend to fall as rates rise, through different channels — yet REITs are generally the more volatile of the two. This guide compares yield and risk, interest-rate sensitivity, inflation protection, and volatility, and explains how balancing REITs and bonds can build a more resilient income portfolio. This is general, educational information, not investment advice; past performance doesn't guarantee future results, and you should verify current market conditions.

Key takeaways
  • REITs offer higher yield potential plus growth and an inflation consideration, while bonds offer lower, stable, contractual income and principal return at maturity.
  • Both are interest-rate sensitive — bond prices fall mechanically as rates rise, while REIT valuations face discount-rate, borrowing-cost, and yield-competition pressure.
  • Most bonds offer no inflation protection (except TIPS), while REITs' rents and values can rise with inflation.
  • REITs are more volatile (equity-like) than bonds — so balancing REITs (growth/inflation) and bonds (stability) can build a more resilient income portfolio.

Yield and Risk Compared

The starting point is the trade-off between yield and risk. REITs generally offer higher yield potential than bonds, plus the possibility of growth and an inflation consideration — because they own real estate whose rents and values can rise over time. The 90% distribution rule pushes REIT yields above the broad market, and unlike a bond's fixed coupon, a REIT's distribution can grow if rents and income rise. But that higher yield comes with equity-like risk: REIT prices fluctuate, distributions can be cut, and there's no guaranteed return of principal.

Bonds sit at the other end of the trade-off. A bond offers lower, more stable, contractual income — a fixed coupon paid on a set schedule — and the return of principal at maturity (subject to the issuer's credit risk). This makes bonds a more defined, predictable income instrument: you generally know what you'll receive and when. The cost of that stability is a lower yield, a fixed payment that doesn't grow, and (for most bonds) no inflation protection. So bonds trade upside and growth for predictability and principal return.

So REITs offer higher, potentially growing yield with equity risk, while bonds offer lower, stable, contractual income with principal return at maturity. So the yield-risk trade-off is the foundation of the comparison. REITs add upside and risk; bonds add stability. Understanding this foundation shapes the rest of the comparison. Interest-Rate Sensitivity

Both REITs and bonds are interest-rate sensitive — when rates rise, both bond prices and REIT valuations tend to fall — but they get there through different channels. For bonds, the mechanism is direct and mathematical: a bond's price moves inversely to interest rates, because a fixed coupon becomes less attractive when newly issued bonds pay more. Longer-duration bonds are more sensitive; their prices fall further when rates rise and rise more when rates fall. This is the textbook interest-rate risk of fixed income.

For REITs, the channels are different and less mechanical. Higher rates raise the discount rate applied to future property cash flows (lowering valuations), increase borrowing costs for REITs that use debt, and make bond yields more competitive with REIT distributions — all of which can pressure REIT prices when rates climb. But REITs also have offsetting dynamics a bond lacks: rising rents and property values, and the potential to grow income, can partly counter rate pressure, so the relationship isn't as fixed as a bond's. So both fall when rates rise, but bonds through a direct price-yield link and REITs through valuation, borrowing-cost, and competition channels with offsets.

So both REITs and bonds are rate-sensitive, but bonds move mechanically with rates while REITs respond through valuation, borrowing costs, and yield competition, with growth offsets. So understanding the channels refines the comparison. Bonds move mechanically; REITs respond with offsets. Understanding the channels clarifies how each behaves when rates move. Rising rates pressure both REITs and bonds, but a bond can only sit there as its price falls, while a REIT can fight back by raising rents — an offset no fixed coupon enjoys.

Inflation Protection

Inflation is where REITs and bonds diverge most sharply. A conventional bond offers no inflation protection: its coupon is fixed in nominal terms, so as inflation rises, the real (inflation-adjusted) value of those payments — and of the principal returned at maturity — erodes. Rising inflation is a classic headwind for fixed-rate bonds, both because it erodes purchasing power and because it often pushes rates up (pressuring prices). The main exception is Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), whose principal adjusts with inflation.

REITs, by contrast, carry a meaningful inflation consideration. Because they own real estate, their rents often rise with inflation — through contractual escalations, market rent growth, and lease resets — and property values tend to climb as replacement costs increase. This gives REITs the potential to grow income and value during inflationary periods, helping preserve purchasing power in a way a fixed coupon cannot. The hedge isn't perfect or guaranteed (it varies by sector and lease structure, and rising rates can still pressure prices), but it's a real distinction from conventional bonds. So REITs offer inflation-linked income potential that most bonds lack.

So most bonds offer no inflation protection (except TIPS), while REITs' rents and values can rise with inflation, giving them a real, if imperfect, inflation consideration. So inflation is a key dividing line. Inflation protection — conventional bonds offering none (fixed nominal coupons whose real value erodes with inflation, except TIPS), versus REITs whose rents (via escalations and resets) and property values often rise with inflation, providing an imperfect but real inflation consideration — sharply distinguishes the two. Bonds lose to inflation; REITs can keep pace. Understanding this dividing line is central to choosing between them in different environments. Volatility Differences

Volatility is a major practical difference, and here REITs are clearly higher. REITs are equities — publicly traded REIT prices move continuously with the market, reflecting investor sentiment, rate expectations, and real-estate conditions, and they can swing sharply, including falling during market sell-offs even when the underlying properties and rents are stable. This equity-like volatility means a REIT allocation can experience meaningful drawdowns, which is the price of its higher yield and growth potential.

Bonds, particularly high-quality bonds, are generally less volatile. A bond's value is anchored by its contractual coupon and the return of principal at maturity, so high-quality bonds tend to hold their value more steadily and often act as a stabilizer in a portfolio, especially during equity downturns (though they're not immune — rising rates and credit risk can move bond prices too, and long-duration bonds can be quite volatile). Overall, however, the income stream and principal repayment make bonds the more stable of the two. So REITs bring higher volatility and bonds bring relative stability.

So REITs are more volatile (equity-like, with real drawdowns) while bonds are generally more stable (anchored by coupons and principal return) — an important difference for income planning. So volatility tolerance shapes the choice. Volatility differences — REITs being more volatile (equity-like, swinging with markets and capable of meaningful drawdowns), versus bonds being generally more stable (anchored by contractual coupons and principal return, often a portfolio stabilizer, though not immune to rate and credit moves) — are a key practical contrast. REITs are higher-volatility; bonds steadier. Understanding this matters for how much price swing you can tolerate in an income holding. Credit, Default, and Principal Risk

The nature of the downside risk also differs between bonds and REITs. A bond's central risk (beyond rate risk) is credit risk — the chance the issuer fails to make interest payments or repay principal at maturity. High-quality government and investment-grade bonds carry low default risk, while high-yield ('junk') bonds pay more precisely because their default risk is higher. But absent a default, a bondholder receives the contractual coupons and the return of principal, giving bonds a defined, contractual downside.

A REIT has no maturity date and no promise to return your principal — your outcome depends on the value of the real estate and the REIT's distributions, both of which can fall. The downside isn't a discrete 'default' event but rather declining property income, falling share prices, and potential distribution cuts. So a bond's risk is concentrated in the issuer's ability to pay (with principal returned if it can), while a REIT's risk is the ongoing performance of the underlying real estate, with no principal guarantee at all. This is a fundamental structural difference: contractual repayment versus equity ownership.

So bonds carry credit/default risk but a contractual return of principal, while REITs carry property-performance risk and no principal guarantee — different shapes of downside. So understanding the risk structure completes the comparison. Credit, default, and principal risk — bonds facing issuer credit risk but offering contractual coupons and principal return if no default occurs, versus REITs facing ongoing property-performance and distribution risk with no maturity and no principal guarantee — distinguish the shape of each instrument's downside. Bonds promise repayment; REITs don't. Understanding this structural difference clarifies what you're actually taking on with each. Bonds carry credit/default risk but a contractual return of principal, while REITs carry property-performance risk and offer no principal guarantee — fundamentally different downsides.

A bond makes you a lender with a contract; a REIT makes you an owner with no maturity date — which is exactly why one is steadier and the other can grow.

Balancing REITs and Bonds

The practical conclusion is that REITs and bonds aren't competitors so much as complements in an income portfolio. Bonds provide stability, contractual income, and principal return — a defensive anchor that can steady a portfolio and supply defined cash flow. REITs provide higher yield potential, the possibility of growing income, and an inflation consideration that bonds lack — a more offensive, inflation-aware income source with equity characteristics. Holding both lets an income portfolio capture stability and growth, defined income and inflation resilience.

Balancing the two depends on your goals, time horizon, income needs, and risk tolerance. An investor prioritizing capital preservation and predictable income may lean more toward bonds, while one seeking higher income and inflation protection (and able to tolerate volatility) may include more REITs. Many income investors hold a mix — bonds for the stable core, REITs for growth and inflation resilience — sized and diversified appropriately. In inflationary or rising-rate environments, the REIT sleeve's inflation linkage can be especially valuable, while bonds anchor the portfolio in calmer or deflationary periods. This is a general framework, not a recommendation of any specific allocation.

So balancing REITs (for growth and inflation resilience) and bonds (for stability and defined income) can build a more resilient income portfolio than either alone. Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between REITs and bonds for income?

The main difference is the nature of the income and the risk behind it. A bond is a contractual loan: it pays a fixed coupon on a set schedule and returns your principal at maturity (subject to the issuer's credit risk), making it a lower-yielding but stable, predictable income instrument. A REIT is an equity investment in real estate: it tends to pay a higher yield, its distributions can grow as rents rise, and it carries an inflation consideration — but it has no maturity, no guaranteed return of principal, and equity-like price volatility. So a bond offers defined, stable income with principal return, while a REIT offers higher, potentially growing income with more risk and inflation resilience. Both are interest-rate sensitive, but REITs are the more volatile of the two. Neither is universally better — they serve complementary roles, and many income investors hold both. This is general information, not a recommendation; verify current conditions with your advisor.

Do REITs yield more than bonds?

As a general tendency, REITs offer higher yield potential than high-quality bonds, because the REIT structure requires distributing at least 90% of taxable income, pushing yields above the broad market, and because REITs carry equity risk that demands a higher return. Unlike a bond's fixed coupon, a REIT's distribution can also grow over time as rents and income rise. However, the comparison depends heavily on the specific bond and REIT and on market conditions: high-yield ('junk') bonds can offer yields competitive with or above some REITs (with commensurate risk), while the gap between REIT yields and high-quality bond yields varies with the interest-rate environment. So REITs generally yield more than investment-grade bonds, but the difference isn't fixed, and a higher yield always reflects higher risk. Importantly, REITs' higher yield comes with volatility and no principal guarantee, while bonds' lower yield comes with stability and contractual repayment. Evaluate yield alongside risk, not in isolation, and verify current conditions, as yields change.

Are REITs and bonds both affected by interest rates?

Yes — both are interest-rate sensitive, but through different channels. For bonds, the link is direct and mathematical: a bond's price moves inversely to interest rates, because its fixed coupon becomes less attractive when new bonds pay more; longer-duration bonds are more sensitive. For REITs, rising rates exert pressure through several channels — higher discount rates lower property valuations, higher borrowing costs squeeze REITs that use debt, and higher bond yields compete with REIT distributions. But REITs also have offsets a bond lacks: rising rents and property values, and the potential to grow income, can partly counter rate pressure. So both tend to fall when rates rise, but bonds move mechanically while REITs respond through valuation, borrowing-cost, and competition channels with growth offsets. This shared rate sensitivity is one reason some investors are surprised that REITs and bonds can move together; understanding the different mechanisms helps set realistic expectations. Verify current conditions, as the rate environment changes.

Which is better for inflation, REITs or bonds?

REITs generally offer better inflation protection than conventional bonds. A standard bond pays a fixed nominal coupon, so as inflation rises, the real value of those payments — and of the principal returned at maturity — erodes; conventional bonds offer no inflation protection. The main exception is Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), whose principal adjusts with inflation. REITs, by contrast, own real estate whose rents often rise with inflation (through contractual escalations, market rent growth, and lease resets) and whose property values tend to climb as replacement costs increase, giving REITs a real, if imperfect, inflation consideration that fixed coupons lack. The REIT hedge isn't guaranteed — it varies by sector and lease structure, and rising rates can still pressure prices — but it's a genuine distinction from conventional bonds. So for inflation resilience, REITs (especially in short-lease, pricing-power sectors) and TIPS tend to fare better than conventional fixed-rate bonds. Many investors combine inflation-aware REITs with bonds for balance. Verify current conditions.

Are REITs more volatile than bonds?

Yes — REITs are generally more volatile than bonds. REITs are equities, so publicly traded REIT prices move continuously with the market, reflecting sentiment, rate expectations, and real-estate conditions, and they can swing sharply, including meaningful drawdowns during market sell-offs even when the underlying properties are stable. This equity-like volatility is the price of REITs' higher yield and growth potential. High-quality bonds, by contrast, are anchored by their contractual coupon and the return of principal at maturity, so they tend to hold their value more steadily and often act as a portfolio stabilizer, especially in equity downturns — though they aren't immune, since rising rates and credit risk can move bond prices, and long-duration bonds can be quite volatile. Overall, however, bonds are the steadier of the two. So if minimizing price volatility is a priority, bonds have the edge; REITs offer higher potential income and growth in exchange for accepting more volatility. Balancing both can moderate overall portfolio swings.

Do bonds guarantee return of principal but REITs don't?

Essentially, yes — with an important caveat about credit risk. A bond is a contractual obligation: the issuer promises to pay periodic coupons and to return your principal at maturity. As long as the issuer doesn't default, you get your principal back, which gives bonds a defined, contractual downside. The caveat is credit risk — if the issuer defaults, you may not receive full repayment, which is why high-quality bonds (low default risk) yield less and high-yield bonds (higher default risk) yield more. A REIT, by contrast, has no maturity and makes no promise to return your principal — your outcome depends entirely on the value of the underlying real estate and the REIT's distributions, both of which can fall, so there's no principal guarantee at all. So bonds offer a contractual return of principal (subject to credit risk), while REITs, as equity, offer no such guarantee. This is a fundamental structural difference between lending (bonds) and owning (REITs), and it shapes the risk you take on with each.

Can REITs replace bonds in a portfolio?

No — REITs shouldn't be treated as a replacement for bonds, because they serve a different role. Bonds provide stability, contractual income, and a return of principal — a defensive anchor that can steady a portfolio and supply defined cash flow, particularly valuable during equity downturns. REITs provide higher yield potential, the possibility of growing income, and inflation resilience, but with equity-like volatility and no principal guarantee. Swapping bonds for REITs would raise a portfolio's yield and inflation resilience but also its volatility and risk, removing the stabilizing role bonds play. Conversely, ignoring REITs would leave out their growth and inflation benefits. So rather than replacing bonds with REITs, most income investors are better served by balancing the two — bonds for the stable core, REITs for growth and inflation resilience — sized to their goals and risk tolerance. So REITs complement bonds rather than replace them. This is general information, not a recommendation; the right balance depends on your situation, so consult your advisor and verify current conditions.

How should I balance REITs and bonds for income?

There's no universal formula — the right balance depends on your goals, time horizon, income needs, and risk tolerance. Bonds provide stability, contractual income, and principal return, while REITs provide higher yield potential, growing-income potential, and an inflation consideration with more volatility. An investor prioritizing capital preservation and predictable income may lean more toward bonds; one seeking higher income and inflation protection, and able to tolerate volatility, may include more REITs. Many income investors hold a mix — bonds as a stable core, REITs for growth and inflation resilience — sized and diversified appropriately. The environment matters too: in inflationary or rising-rate periods, the REIT sleeve's inflation linkage can be especially useful, while bonds anchor the portfolio in calmer times. So balance the two to capture both stability and growth, weighting toward your priorities. This is a general framework, not a recommendation of any specific allocation — confirm what suits your situation with your advisor, and verify current market conditions, since the right mix can shift as conditions change.

Why do REITs and bonds sometimes move together?

REITs and bonds can move together because both are interest-rate sensitive, even though their other characteristics differ. When interest rates rise, bond prices fall mechanically (a fixed coupon is worth less when new bonds pay more), and REIT valuations also tend to come under pressure — higher discount rates lower property values, higher borrowing costs squeeze leveraged REITs, and higher bond yields compete with REIT distributions for income investors' dollars. So a rate shock can push both down at once, which surprises investors who think of REITs purely as equities. That said, the relationship isn't constant: REITs also respond to real-estate fundamentals and can grow income to offset rate pressure, so over time REITs behave more like equities than bonds, and the two don't always move together. So the shared interest-rate sensitivity explains the occasional joint moves, while REITs' equity nature and growth offsets explain why they diverge over longer periods. Understanding both helps set realistic expectations for how a REIT-and-bond portfolio behaves. Verify current conditions.

Are REIT distributions as safe as bond coupons?

No — REIT distributions are generally less safe than the coupons on high-quality bonds, because of the difference between equity and debt. A high-quality bond's coupon is a contractual obligation: the issuer must pay it (absent default), giving bond income a defined, contractual character. A REIT's distribution, by contrast, isn't contractual — it depends on the REIT's underlying property income, which can decline due to rising vacancies, tenant defaults, falling rents, higher costs, or rising rates. If income falls, a REIT can reduce or suspend its distribution, cutting your cash flow. So a REIT distribution is variable, equity-like income, not a guaranteed payment like a high-quality bond coupon. This is the trade-off for REITs' higher yield potential and growth: you accept more income uncertainty. So if certainty of income is paramount, high-quality bonds offer more safety; REITs offer higher potential income with more risk to that income. Diversifying across REITs and sectors and focusing on sustainable distributions helps, but doesn't make REIT income contractually safe.

What role do bonds play that REITs can't?

Bonds play a stabilizing, defensive role that REITs generally can't replicate. Because a high-quality bond pays a contractual coupon and returns principal at maturity, it provides defined, predictable income and a return of capital that anchors a portfolio — and high-quality bonds often hold their value (or even rise) during equity downturns, helping cushion losses elsewhere. This defensive ballast is valuable for capital preservation and for investors who need reliable, defined cash flow. REITs, as equities, can't provide this: they have no maturity, no principal guarantee, variable distributions, and equity-like volatility, so they don't serve as a stabilizing anchor — they're a growth-and-income holding, not a defensive one. So bonds offer principal protection (subject to credit risk), defined income, and downturn stability that REITs lack. This is precisely why the two complement each other: bonds anchor and stabilize, while REITs add yield, growth, and inflation resilience. So even an income investor who likes REITs typically still wants bonds for the role only they can play. Balance the two to your goals.

Do REITs offer growth that bonds don't?

Yes — one of REITs' key advantages over bonds is the potential for growth, both in income and in value. A conventional bond pays a fixed coupon that never grows and returns a fixed principal at maturity, so its nominal payments are static. A REIT, by owning real estate, can grow its distributions over time as rents rise — through contractual escalations, market rent growth, and re-leasing — and the underlying property values can appreciate, potentially lifting the share price as well. This growth potential gives REITs an inflation-hedging quality (rents often rise with inflation) and the ability to increase an investor's income over time, neither of which a fixed-rate bond provides. That said, REIT growth isn't guaranteed — distributions can be flat or cut, and values can fall, depending on the real estate cycle. So REITs offer growth potential that bonds lack, at the cost of more volatility and no principal guarantee. This growth-versus-stability contrast is central to why investors balance the two rather than choosing only one.

Are REITs or bonds better for a retiree?

Both can serve a retiree, and many retirees use a blend, because each fills a different need. Bonds offer stable, contractual income and a return of principal, which supports predictable cash flow and capital preservation — valuable for retirees who need reliable income and can't easily replace lost capital. REITs offer higher yield potential, growing-income potential, and inflation resilience, which helps a retiree's income keep pace with rising costs over a long retirement — but with more volatility and no principal guarantee. So a retiree prioritizing stability and predictable income may lean toward bonds, while one concerned about inflation eroding purchasing power over decades may want some REIT exposure for growth and inflation protection. A common approach is to hold bonds as a stable income core and REITs as a smaller, inflation-aware growth-and-income sleeve, sized so that REIT volatility doesn't threaten essential income. So neither is categorically better for retirees — the right balance depends on income needs, time horizon, and risk tolerance. This is general information, not advice; consult your advisor and verify current conditions.

Do REITs and bonds belong in different accounts?

Tax-aware investors often place REITs and bonds with an eye to account type, because both tend to generate ordinary-income-taxed cash flow. REIT distributions are largely ordinary income (softened by the 20% Section 199A deduction), and most bond interest is also taxed as ordinary income, so some investors hold both in tax-advantaged accounts (like IRAs) where that ordinary-income treatment doesn't reduce after-tax returns, reserving taxable accounts for more tax-efficient holdings like qualified-dividend stocks. There are nuances: municipal-bond interest is often federally tax-exempt and may suit a taxable account, and the 199A deduction on REIT dividends applies in taxable accounts too, so the right placement depends on your full situation. So both REITs and bonds frequently fit well in tax-advantaged accounts because of their ordinary-income character, but the specifics — bond type, REIT distribution mix, account limits, and income needs — vary by investor. So account placement is one more dimension to consider when balancing REITs and bonds. This is general information, not tax advice — confirm the right placement with your tax advisor, as the details can be technical.
