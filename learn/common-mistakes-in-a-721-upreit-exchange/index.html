Common Mistakes in a 721 / UPREIT Exchange

By Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read

A 721 / UPREIT exchange is powerful but has pitfalls that can lead to regret or poor outcomes. This guide covers the common mistakes — not understanding the one-way nature, failing to evaluate the REIT, overlooking the conversion tax, neglecting tax protection, ignoring suitability, and inadequate professional guidance — and how to avoid them.

The 721 / UPREIT exchange offers powerful benefits, but it's a sophisticated, generally irreversible strategy where mistakes can lead to regret or poor outcomes. Because the move is largely permanent (you commit to REIT ownership), errors are hard to undo — making it especially important to avoid the common pitfalls. The frequent mistakes include not fully understanding the one-way nature (and committing without certainty), failing to adequately evaluate the REIT (you're tying your outcome to it), overlooking the conversion tax (being surprised by the tax on accessing liquidity), neglecting tax protection (leaving your deferral vulnerable), ignoring whether the strategy truly fits you, and proceeding without adequate professional guidance. Understanding these mistakes — and how to avoid them — helps you execute a 721 exchange soundly. This guide covers the common mistakes and how to avoid each.

Key takeaways
  • Not understanding the one-way nature (committing without certainty) is the most consequential mistake — be certain before committing.
  • Failing to evaluate the REIT (you're tying your outcome to it) and overlooking the conversion tax (being surprised by it) are common errors.
  • Neglecting tax protection (leaving your deferral vulnerable) and ignoring suitability (a poor-fit commitment) are mistakes to avoid.
  • Proceeding without adequate professional guidance compounds all the other mistakes — work with an advisor, CPA, and attorney.

Not understanding the one-way nature

The most consequential mistake is not fully understanding the 721 exchange 's one-way nature before committing. Because the move into REIT ownership is generally irreversible (you lose 1031 eligibility and can't potentially tax-free return to direct real estate), committing without grasping this can lead to regret if your circumstances or preferences change. Investors who treat the 721 as a reversible or tentative move are setting themselves up for potential regret.

This mistake happens when investors focus on the benefits (deferral, diversification, etc.) without fully internalizing the irreversible commitment. They may not realize they're giving up the ability to keep exchanging real property or to return to direct ownership potentially tax-free. So they commit, then later wish they'd retained that flexibility — but can't get it back potentially tax-free.

To avoid this mistake, be certain before committing — fully understand that the 721 is a generally permanent transition into REIT ownership, and confirm that's what you want (not a tentative move). Consider keeping some assets in direct real estate if you want to retain some flexibility. Not understanding the one-way nature — committing to the irreversible 721 exchange without grasping its permanence, risking regret if circumstances change — is the most consequential mistake. Avoid it by being certain before committing and fully understanding the one-way commitment. Understanding this mistake underscores the importance of certainty. The one-way nature is the defining feature to internalize before a 721 exchange, and failing to do so is the costliest mistake.

Failing to evaluate the REIT

A second common mistake is failing to adequately evaluate the REIT you're transitioning into. Because the 721 exchange ties your outcome to the REIT (its performance, management, and financial health determine your returns and your holding's value), the REIT's quality matters enormously — yet some investors don't evaluate it thoroughly, focusing on the strategy's benefits rather than the specific REIT's merits.

This mistake can lead to a poor outcome if you transition into a weak, poorly-managed, or unsuitable REIT. Since you're committing (largely irreversibly) to this REIT, a bad choice is hard to undo, and your returns and value will suffer. So failing to evaluate the REIT — its portfolio quality, diversification, management, track record, financial health, fees, and (for liquidity) whether it's traded or non-traded — is a serious mistake.

To avoid it, evaluate the REIT thoroughly before committing — assess its quality, management, financials, fees, and structure, since you're tying your outcome to it. Choose a strong, well-managed REIT that fits your goals. Overlooking the conversion tax

A third common mistake is overlooking the conversion tax — being surprised by the tax triggered when you convert OP units to REIT shares for liquidity. Some investors assume the 721's liquidity is freely accessible, not realizing that converting units to shares triggers the deferred gain (taxable). So when they convert, they're surprised by the tax bill.

This mistake can lead to poor liquidity planning — converting too much at once (triggering a large tax), or not anticipating the tax when planning to access liquidity. Because the conversion triggers the deferred four-layer tax (including recapture), the tax can be substantial, and overlooking it can cause unwelcome surprises and poor decisions.

To avoid this mistake, understand upfront that converting units to shares is taxable (triggering the deferred gain), and plan conversions accordingly — converting gradually to spread the tax, timing conversions well, and holding units you don't need toward the step-up. So anticipate and plan for the conversion tax rather than being surprised by it. Avoid it by understanding the conversion is taxable and planning conversions to manage the tax. Understanding this mistake ensures you plan your liquidity (and its tax) realistically. The conversion tax is a key consideration to anticipate, not overlook, so your liquidity access is planned with the tax in mind.

Neglecting tax protection

A fourth common mistake is neglecting tax protection — failing to negotiate or understand the tax protection agreement, leaving your deferral vulnerable. Recall that if the operating partnership sells your contributed property in a taxable way, your deferred (built-in) gain could be triggered unexpectedly. A tax protection agreement (the REIT agreeing not to sell your property taxably for a period) protects against this — but neglecting it leaves you exposed.

This mistake happens when investors don't focus on the tax protection during the contribution negotiation, or don't understand its importance. Without adequate tax protection, a partnership sale of your property could trigger your deferred tax before you intended, undermining the deferral you sought. So neglecting tax protection is a meaningful mistake for protecting your deferral.

To avoid it, negotiate appropriate tax protection as part of the contribution (with your attorney) — understanding its scope and duration, and ensuring it adequately protects your deferred gain for a reasonable period. So make tax protection a priority in the negotiation. Avoid it by prioritizing and negotiating appropriate tax protection. Understanding this mistake emphasizes the importance of the tax protection agreement. Ignoring suitability and fit

A fifth common mistake is ignoring whether the 721 exchange truly fits you — proceeding because the benefits sound appealing, without honestly assessing whether your goals and profile align with the strategy. The 721 exchange suits specific profiles (owners transitioning into passive REIT ownership); for others (active, control-valuing investors), it's a poor fit. Ignoring this fit can lead to a regrettable commitment.

This mistake happens when investors are drawn to the benefits (deferral, diversification) without considering whether the trade-offs (loss of control, the one-way nature) are acceptable to them. An active investor who values control might do a 721 exchange (attracted by the deferral) and then regret giving up control — a poor fit they should have recognized.

To avoid this mistake, honestly assess your fit before committing — consider whether your goals align with the 721's benefits and whether you're comfortable with the trade-offs. If you're an active, control-valuing investor, recognize the 721 may not fit (a 1031 might suit better). So match the strategy to your goals honestly. Inadequate professional guidance

A sixth mistake, which compounds the others, is proceeding without adequate professional guidance. The 721 exchange is complex (securities, tax mechanics, documentation, the partnership structure) and significant (generally irreversible), so it requires expertise — a financial advisor (suitability and fit), a CPA (the tax), and an attorney (the structuring, documentation, and tax protection). Proceeding without this guidance risks all the other mistakes.

So inadequate guidance compounds the risks.

Frequently asked questions

What's the biggest mistake in a 721 exchange?

Not understanding the one-way nature — committing to the generally irreversible 721 exchange without fully grasping its permanence. Because the move into REIT ownership is largely irreversible (you lose 1031 eligibility and can't potentially tax-free return to direct real estate), committing without certainty can lead to regret if your circumstances or preferences change. Avoid it by being certain before committing and fully understanding the one-way commitment. So failing to evaluate the REIT thoroughly (its portfolio quality, diversification, management, track record, financials, fees, and structure) is a serious mistake. Evaluate the REIT carefully before committing, since you're investing in that specific REIT, not just doing a 721 exchange in the abstract.

What is the conversion tax mistake?

This leads to poor liquidity planning (converting too much at once, or not anticipating the tax). Anticipate the conversion tax rather than being surprised by it, so your liquidity access is planned realistically.

What is tax protection, and why not neglect it?

Tax protection is an agreement by the REIT not to sell your contributed property in a taxable way for a period (protecting your deferred gain from being triggered by a partnership sale). Neglecting it leaves your deferral vulnerable — a partnership sale of your property could trigger your deferred tax unexpectedly, undermining the deferral you sought. So failing to negotiate or understand the tax protection is a mistake. Avoid it by prioritizing and negotiating appropriate tax protection (with your attorney) — understanding its scope and duration to adequately protect your deferred gain for a reasonable period.

How do I know if the 721 exchange fits me?

Honestly assess whether your goals align with the 721's benefits and whether you're comfortable with the trade-offs. It suits owners transitioning into passive REIT ownership (tired landlords, estate planners, concentrated owners, liquidity seekers); it doesn't suit active investors who value control and flexibility. Ignoring this fit — being drawn by the benefits without considering whether the trade-offs (loss of control, one-way nature) are acceptable — is a mistake. Avoid it by honestly assessing your fit before committing. If you're an active, control-valuing investor, recognize the 721 may not fit (a 1031 might suit better).

Do I really need professional guidance?

Can I undo a 721 exchange if I make a mistake?

Generally not potentially tax-free — the 721 exchange is largely irreversible (you can't potentially tax-free return to direct real estate). So mistakes are hard to undo, which is why avoiding them upfront is so important. To exit REIT ownership, you'd have to convert your units (triggering the deferred gain) and reinvest the after-tax proceeds — not potentially tax-free. So the irreversibility means you should avoid mistakes by careful upfront diligence (understanding the one-way nature, evaluating the REIT, assessing your fit) rather than relying on undoing errors. Prevention is key, since the move is generally permanent.

How can I avoid overlooking the conversion tax?

Understand from the start that converting OP units to REIT shares triggers the deferred gain (the four-layer tax, including recapture), so accessing liquidity has a tax cost. Plan your conversions accordingly — convert gradually (spreading the tax over years), time conversions in favorable years, and hold units you don't need toward the step-up (eliminating the gain on those). Coordinate with your CPA to model the conversion tax. So anticipate and plan for the tax rather than being surprised by it, ensuring your liquidity access is planned with the tax in mind.

What happens if I transition into a weak REIT?

Your returns and your holding's value would suffer, and because the 721 exchange is largely irreversible, the poor choice is hard to undo — you'd be committed to the weak REIT. This is why evaluating the REIT thoroughly before committing is essential. A weak, poorly-managed REIT directly harms you, with little recourse (you're a passive investor). So failing to evaluate the REIT and ending up in a weak one is a serious mistake with lasting consequences. Choose a strong, well-managed REIT that fits your goals, since you're tying your outcome to it largely permanently.

Is rushing a 721 exchange a mistake?

Yes — rushing can lead to the other mistakes (not understanding the one-way nature, inadequately evaluating the REIT, overlooking the conversion tax, neglecting tax protection, ignoring fit). Because the 721 exchange is significant and largely irreversible, it warrants careful, unhurried diligence. Unlike a 1031 (with its deadlines), a standalone 721 exchange has no statutory deadlines, so there's no need to rush — take the time to do the diligence properly. Rushing a generally permanent decision is unwise; deliberate carefully to avoid the common mistakes. The lack of deadlines allows (and the stakes demand) careful diligence.

How do I avoid these mistakes?

Be certain before committing (understanding the one-way nature), evaluate the REIT thoroughly (you're tying your outcome to it), anticipate the conversion tax (and plan conversions), negotiate appropriate tax protection (protecting your deferral), honestly assess your fit (matching the strategy to your goals), and assemble a qualified professional team (advisor, CPA, attorney). Is choosing a non-traded REIT without understanding it a mistake?

It can be — transitioning into a non-traded REIT without understanding its specific characteristics (limited, program-based liquidity, valuation uncertainty, often higher fees) is a mistake, because these features significantly affect your liquidity and outcome. FINRA has specifically cautioned investors to review non-traded REITs carefully. So if you're considering a non-traded REIT, understand its liquidity limitations, valuation approach, and fees before committing — don't assume it behaves like a traded REIT. Failing to understand the non-traded structure's specifics is a version of the REIT-evaluation mistake, with particular importance given non-traded REITs' distinct features.

Can poor timing of the conversion be a mistake?

Yes — converting OP units to shares at a poor time (e.g., converting a large amount in a high-income year, triggering the gain at higher tax rates, or converting all at once instead of gradually) is a mistake that increases your tax unnecessarily. Good conversion timing (spreading conversions over years, converting in lower-income years, coordinating with your CPA) manages the tax efficiently. So poor conversion timing — not planning when and how much to convert — is a tax mistake to avoid. Plan your conversions thoughtfully with your CPA to avoid triggering more tax than necessary at unfavorable times.

Is not having an exit or liquidity plan a mistake?

It can be — entering a 721 exchange without thinking through how (and when) you'll eventually access liquidity or what your long-term plan is (hold for income and the step-up, convert gradually for liquidity, etc.) can lead to poor decisions later. Having a plan for your OP units — whether to hold toward the step-up, convert gradually for income, or some combination — helps you manage the holding and the tax effectively. So failing to plan your long-term approach to the units is a mistake. Think through your liquidity and holding plan as part of the decision, with your advisor and CPA, rather than leaving it unconsidered.

Is overlooking fees a mistake in a 721 exchange?

Yes — failing to understand the fees involved (the REIT's ongoing management fees and expenses, plus any transaction costs) is a mistake, because fees reduce your net returns over time. As with any managed investment, the fee structure matters to your outcome, and it varies by REIT (non-traded REITs often have higher fees). So understanding the specific REIT's fees before committing is part of sound diligence. Overlooking the fees can lead to disappointment if the net returns are lower than expected. Ask about and understand the fee structure as part of evaluating the REIT, so you have realistic expectations about your net returns after fees.
