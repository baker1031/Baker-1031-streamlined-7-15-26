Evaluating a REIT's Dividend Yield

By Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read

A high REIT yield can be a great income opportunity — or a warning sign. This guide explains why a high yield can be a trap, how to judge yield against payout sustainability, how to compare yields by sector, why yield plus growth often beats a high static yield, and how to build a yield watchlist.

For income investors, a REIT's dividend yield is the headline number — but it's also the most easily misread. Yield is simply the annual dividend divided by the share price, so it rises when the dividend goes up or, just as often, when the price falls. That second case is the catch: a yield that looks unusually high frequently reflects a price the market has marked down because it fears a dividend cut, not a generous, sustainable payout. Evaluating a yield well means looking past the number — judging it against payout sustainability (AFFO coverage), comparing it by sector rather than across the whole market, weighing yield together with growth for total return, and tracking candidates on a watchlist with real sustainability checks. This is an income-investor skill, and it's the difference between collecting durable cash flow and walking into a yield trap. This guide explains why a high yield can be a trap, how to test sustainability, how to compare yields by sector, why yield plus growth matters, and how to build a yield watchlist. This is educational information, not investment advice — and never a promise of any particular yield.

Reading a yield well starts with knowing how different REIT structures generate and report their distributions, a foundation our comprehensive REIT primer walks through.

Key takeaways
  • Yield is dividend divided by price, so a high yield often reflects a falling price and an at-risk dividend — a potential trap, not a gift.
  • Judge a yield against payout sustainability (the AFFO payout ratio and coverage), not in isolation — coverage determines whether the income is durable.
  • Compare yields within a sector, since mortgage and net-lease REITs run higher and growth sectors lower — a yield far above peers warrants scrutiny.
  • Weigh yield plus growth for total return, and maintain a yield watchlist of quality REITs pre-vetted for sustainability rather than chasing the highest number.

Why a High Yield Can Be a Trap

The first thing to understand about dividend yield is the math that makes a high yield seductive — and sometimes dangerous. Yield equals the annual dividend divided by the share price. That means a yield can rise two ways: the dividend can increase (good), or the share price can fall (often not good). When trouble hits a REIT — declining occupancy, a stretched balance sheet, a struggling sector — investors sell, the price drops, and the yield mechanically climbs. So a strikingly high yield is frequently a symptom of a falling price, not a sign of generosity.

This is the 'yield trap': an investor sees a double-digit yield, buys for the income, and then the REIT cuts its dividend because the payout was unsustainable — at which point both the income and, often, the price fall further. The high yield was the market's warning that a cut was coming, not a free lunch. Not every high yield is a trap — some sectors legitimately pay high yields — but a yield that's high relative to a REIT's peers, or that has spiked as the price fell, should prompt suspicion rather than excitement until you've checked whether the payout can actually be sustained.

So a high yield can be a trap because yield rises as price falls on trouble, and an unsustainable payout may be cut — leaving you worse off. Yield vs. Payout Sustainability

The antidote to the yield trap is to judge a yield against payout sustainability — and the key tool is AFFO coverage. A REIT's dividend is sustainable only if it's covered by the cash the REIT actually generates, which adjusted funds from operations (AFFO) approximates better than net income or even FFO. Because a REIT must pay out at least 90% of its taxable income as dividends, the cushion behind that payout is what really matters. The AFFO payout ratio (dividends divided by AFFO) tells you how much cushion sits behind the dividend: a payout comfortably below 100% of AFFO suggests the dividend is covered with room to spare, while a payout near or above 100% suggests it's stretched.

So before you're impressed by a yield, check whether the payout funding it is sustainable. A high yield backed by a well-covered payout (low AFFO payout ratio, stable or improving coverage, distributions funded from operating cash flow) may be a genuine income opportunity. A high yield backed by a stretched payout (AFFO payout near or above 100%, declining coverage, distributions funded by debt or return of capital) is a yield trap waiting to spring. The yield itself is meaningless without this check — two REITs with identical yields can have completely different risk profiles depending on how well their dividends are covered.

So a yield is only as good as the payout behind it — judge it against AFFO coverage, not in isolation, to separate durable income from a looming cut. Comparing Yields by Sector

A yield only has meaning in context, and the most important context is sector. Different REIT sectors carry structurally different yields. Mortgage REITs (mREITs) and net-lease REITs tend to offer higher yields — mREITs because they're leveraged and rate-sensitive, net-lease REITs because they have stable, bond-like cash flows and high payouts. Growth-oriented sectors (like certain industrial or data center REITs) tend to offer lower yields, because they retain more cash to fund expansion and investors expect growth rather than high current income.

Because of this, a yield that looks high or low only tells you something when you compare it within its sector, not across the whole REIT market. A 4% yield might be high for a fast-growing data center REIT but low for a mortgage REIT; an 8% yield might be normal for an mREIT but a glaring red flag for an apartment REIT. The useful question is: how does this REIT's yield compare to its sector peers? A yield well above the sector norm is the one that warrants scrutiny — it may reflect higher risk or an impending cut, while a yield in line with peers is less likely to be a trap (though it still needs the sustainability check).

So compare yields within a sector, not across the whole market — a yield high relative to its peers is the one to scrutinize. Yield Plus Growth

Chasing the highest current yield can cost you on total return, because what ultimately matters is yield plus growth. A REIT's total return comes from its dividend income plus any appreciation, and appreciation is driven largely by growth in cash flow (FFO/AFFO) and the dividend over time. A lower-yielding REIT that reliably grows its FFO and raises its dividend year after year can deliver a higher total return — and a rising income stream — than a higher-yielding REIT whose dividend is static or at risk of being cut.

This reframes the income question: instead of asking only 'what's the yield today?', ask 'what's the yield, and how is the dividend likely to grow?' A 4% yield growing at a healthy clip can, over years, surpass a flat 7% yield in both total income collected and ending value — and with less risk of a cut. High-growth REITs in strong sectors often pair modest yields with durable dividend growth, while the highest static yields often come with little or no growth (and more risk). So the goal isn't to maximize starting yield but to optimize the combination of starting yield and sustainable growth for total return.

So the best income outcome usually comes from balancing yield and growth, not from maximizing yield alone — a lower yield that grows reliably can beat a high static one. Building a Yield Watchlist

The practical way to put all of this together is to build a yield watchlist — a curated list of REITs you'd consider for income, each screened and monitored with real sustainability checks rather than chosen on yield alone — the kind of roster that underpins durable income-focused REIT distribution strategies. Rather than reacting to whatever shows the highest yield today, you maintain a roster of candidates across sectors, each evaluated for the things that actually matter: AFFO payout coverage, the trend in that coverage, balance-sheet strength (leverage, coverage, maturities), dividend-growth history, and how the yield compares to sector peers.

A watchlist lets you act with discipline. You note each REIT's current yield and its sustainability profile, and you wait for opportunities where a quality REIT's yield rises for the right reasons — a broad market sell-off or rate scare rather than a company-specific problem — so you can add income at a more attractive yield without taking on a likely cut. The watchlist approach also helps you avoid the trap of buying the highest yield reflexively; instead, each candidate has already passed your sustainability and quality filters, so a high yield prompts a check against your notes rather than an impulse purchase. So a yield watchlist turns yield evaluation into an ongoing, disciplined process.

So building a yield watchlist — a curated, continually monitored set of quality REITs vetted for sustainability — turns the principles of yield evaluation into a repeatable, disciplined practice. Yield and the Rate Environment

You can't evaluate a REIT's yield in isolation from the interest-rate environment, because rates shape both the level of REIT yields and what those yields mean. When rates rise, REIT share prices often fall — their dividends compete with now-higher-yielding bonds, higher borrowing costs pressure cash flow, and rising cap rates can lower property values — which mechanically pushes REIT yields up across the board. So in a rising-rate period, broadly higher REIT yields can reflect genuine value (quality REITs marked down by macro fears) or real strain (REITs whose fundamentals are pressured by higher rates), and telling the two apart requires the sustainability and balance-sheet checks.

The rate backdrop also reframes the income comparison. A REIT yield should be judged not just against other REITs but against the broader yield landscape — what investors can earn on bonds and cash — because that opportunity cost shifts as rates move. A 5% REIT yield looks different when safe bonds yield 2% than when they yield 5%; in the latter case, an income investor rightly demands more from a REIT (growth, quality, or a higher yield) to compensate for its added risk. So the rate environment determines both why yields are where they are and how attractive a given yield really is relative to the alternatives.

So the interest-rate environment drives REIT yields and sets the bar a yield must clear versus bonds and cash — context that's essential to evaluating any yield. Frequently asked questions

What is a REIT's dividend yield?

A REIT's dividend yield is the annual dividend divided by the current share price, expressed as a percentage. If a REIT pays $2 in annual dividends and trades at $40, its yield is 5%. Yield tells you the income return you'd receive at today's price — a quick gauge of how much current income the REIT offers relative to its cost. For income investors, it's the headline number. But it's important to understand the math: because yield is the dividend over the price, it moves with both. If the dividend rises, yield rises; if the price falls, yield also rises — even though a falling price often signals trouble. So a high yield can reflect either a generous, growing dividend or a marked-down price that warns of a coming cut. So while dividend yield is a useful starting point for evaluating REIT income, it's only a starting point. So always look past the yield itself to whether the underlying dividend is sustainable, how it compares to sector peers, and whether it's likely to grow — the yield number alone doesn't tell you any of that.

Why can a high REIT yield be a trap?

A high REIT yield can be a trap because of the math: yield equals the dividend divided by the share price, so a yield can spike not because the dividend is generous but because the price has fallen. When a REIT runs into trouble — declining occupancy, a stretched balance sheet, a struggling sector, looming debt maturities — investors sell, the price drops, and the yield mechanically climbs. An investor who buys purely for that high yield may then see the REIT cut its dividend (because the payout was unsustainable), at which point both the income and, often, the price fall further. The high yield was the market's warning that a cut was coming, not a free lunch. This is the classic 'yield trap.' Not every high yield is a trap — some sectors legitimately pay high yields — but a yield that's high relative to peers or that spiked as the price fell deserves suspicion, not excitement, until you've checked sustainability. So treat an unusually high yield as a question to investigate, not a bargain to grab.

How do I tell if a REIT's dividend is sustainable?

You test a REIT's dividend sustainability primarily through AFFO coverage. A dividend is sustainable only if it's covered by the cash the REIT actually generates, and adjusted funds from operations (AFFO) approximates that distributable cash better than net income or even FFO. Calculate (or find) the AFFO payout ratio — dividends divided by AFFO. A payout comfortably below 100% of AFFO suggests the dividend is covered with cushion to spare; a payout near or above 100% suggests it's stretched and at risk. Beyond the level, watch the trend: a rising payout ratio signals declining coverage. Also check how the distribution is funded — if much of it comes from return of capital, asset sales, or borrowing rather than operating cash flow, the dividend may be unsustainable even if the headline ratio looks acceptable. So a sustainable dividend shows a low and stable AFFO payout, solid coverage, and funding from operating cash flow. So before trusting any yield, confirm the payout behind it passes these checks — verifying current figures from the REIT's own reporting, since AFFO involves some estimation.

Should I compare REIT yields across different sectors?

No — comparing REIT yields across different sectors is misleading, because sectors carry structurally different yields. Mortgage REITs (mREITs) tend to offer high yields because they're leveraged and rate-sensitive; net-lease REITs offer high yields because they have stable, bond-like cash flows and high payout ratios. Growth-oriented sectors, like certain industrial or data center REITs, offer lower yields because they retain cash to fund expansion and investors expect growth rather than high current income. So a 4% yield might be high for a fast-growing data center REIT but low for a mortgage REIT, and an 8% yield might be normal for an mREIT but a glaring warning for an apartment REIT. Comparing a yield to the wrong peer group will either falsely alarm you or falsely reassure you. So compare a REIT's yield within its own sector, asking how it stacks up against similar REITs. So a yield well above the sector norm is the one warranting scrutiny, while a yield in line with peers is less likely to be a trap — though it still needs a sustainability check.

What is a yield trap?

A yield trap is a stock or REIT whose unusually high dividend yield lures income investors in, but whose dividend is unsustainable and likely to be cut — so the 'high yield' turns out to be a warning rather than an opportunity. The mechanism: yield equals dividend over price, so when a REIT's price falls on real problems (weak fundamentals, a stretched payout, balance-sheet strain), the yield rises mechanically. An investor who buys for that high yield then suffers a dividend cut, after which the income drops and the price often falls further, producing a loss on both fronts. The market's elevated yield was effectively pricing in the coming cut. You avoid yield traps by judging the yield against payout sustainability (AFFO coverage and its trend), comparing it to sector peers rather than the whole market, and checking how the distribution is funded. So a yield trap is a deceptively high yield backed by an at-risk dividend. So the defense is always to verify that the payout behind a high yield can actually be sustained before being drawn in by the number.

Is a lower-yielding REIT ever a better investment than a higher-yielding one?

Yes — frequently. What ultimately matters for an income investor is total return and the durability and growth of the income, not just the starting yield. A lower-yielding REIT that reliably grows its funds from operations (FFO/AFFO) and raises its dividend year after year can deliver a higher total return — and a rising income stream — than a higher-yielding REIT whose dividend is static or at risk of being cut. For example, a 4% yield growing steadily can, over a number of years, surpass a flat 7% yield in both cumulative income and ending value, often with less risk of a cut. High-growth REITs in strong sectors commonly pair modest yields with durable dividend growth, while the highest static yields frequently come with little growth and more risk. So a lower yield isn't a disadvantage if it comes with sustainable growth and lower cut risk. So evaluate yield plus growth together, focusing on total return and a growing income stream, rather than reaching for the highest current number — the highest yield is rarely the best long-term outcome.

What does yield plus growth mean for REITs?

Yield plus growth is the idea that a REIT's total return comes from its current dividend income plus the appreciation driven by growth in its cash flow and dividend over time — so you should evaluate both together, not yield alone. A REIT's price tends to rise as its funds from operations (FFO/AFFO) and dividend grow, so a REIT that reliably increases its cash flow and raises its distribution offers a rising income stream and capital appreciation, not just a static payout. This means a lower starting yield that grows can outperform a higher static yield over time, in both total income collected and ending value — and usually with less risk of a cut, since growth signals a healthy, well-covered business. The highest current yields, by contrast, often come with little or no growth and greater risk. So instead of asking only 'what's the yield today?', ask 'what's the yield, and how sustainably is the dividend likely to grow?' So optimizing the combination of a reasonable starting yield and durable dividend growth, rather than maximizing yield, is the path to better long-term income and total return.

How do I build a REIT yield watchlist?

You build a REIT yield watchlist by curating a roster of quality REITs you'd consider for income, each screened and monitored with real sustainability checks rather than chosen on yield alone. Start by selecting candidates across sectors and evaluating each on the things that matter: AFFO payout coverage and its trend, balance-sheet strength (leverage, coverage, debt maturities, fixed-versus-floating mix), dividend-growth history, and how the yield compares to sector peers. Record each REIT's current yield alongside its sustainability profile. Then monitor the list over time, watching for opportunities where a quality REIT's yield rises for the right reasons — a broad market sell-off or rate scare rather than a company-specific problem — so you can add income at a more attractive yield without buying a likely cut. The watchlist replaces impulsive high-yield buying with discipline: each candidate has already passed your filters, so a high yield triggers a check against your notes, not a reflexive purchase. So a yield watchlist turns yield evaluation into an ongoing, repeatable process. So maintain and update it, and act only when a vetted REIT's yield becomes attractive for sound reasons.

Why does yield rise when a REIT's price falls?

Yield rises when a REIT's price falls because of the simple arithmetic of how yield is defined: yield equals the annual dividend divided by the share price. If the dividend stays the same but the price drops, the same dollar payout now represents a larger percentage of a smaller price — so the yield goes up. For example, a $2 dividend on a $40 price is a 5% yield; if the price falls to $25 while the dividend holds, the yield jumps to 8%. This is why a falling price mechanically inflates yield. The catch is why the price fell: often it's because the market anticipates problems — weak fundamentals, a stretched payout, balance-sheet strain — and is pricing in the risk of a dividend cut. So a rising yield driven by a falling price can be a warning, not a windfall: the market may be telling you the dividend is at risk. So when you see a yield that has climbed sharply, check whether it rose because the dividend grew (a good sign) or because the price fell (a sign to investigate the cause and the dividend's sustainability).

Do mortgage REITs have higher yields than equity REITs?

Generally, yes — mortgage REITs (mREITs) tend to carry higher dividend yields than most equity REITs, but the higher yield comes with different and often greater risks. Mortgage REITs don't own property; they finance real estate by holding mortgages and mortgage-backed securities, earning the interest-rate spread between what those assets pay and their own borrowing cost. They typically use significant leverage and are highly sensitive to interest rates and to changes in that spread, which supports high yields but also makes their income and book value more volatile — and their dividends more prone to cuts when rates or spreads move against them. Equity REITs, which own and operate income-producing properties, generally offer more moderate yields tied to rents, with the yield level varying widely by sector. So an mREIT's higher yield isn't free — it reflects higher leverage and rate sensitivity. So when comparing yields, recognize that a mortgage REIT's elevated yield should be judged against its particular risks and within the mREIT peer group, not held up as superior to a lower-yielding but more stable equity REIT.

Can a high yield ever be safe?

Yes — a high yield can be safe, but only when the payout behind it is well-covered, so you have to verify rather than assume. Some REITs and sectors legitimately pay high yields from durable cash flows: stable net-lease REITs with long leases and high (but covered) payout ratios, for instance, can offer elevated yields that are sustainable. The way to tell a safe high yield from a yield trap is the sustainability check: a high yield with a low and stable AFFO payout ratio, solid and improving coverage, distributions funded from operating cash flow, a sound balance sheet, and a yield in line with sector peers may well be safe. The same yield with a stretched AFFO payout, declining coverage, return-of-capital-heavy funding, or a level far above sector peers is the dangerous kind. So a high yield isn't inherently unsafe — it's unverified until you confirm coverage and quality. So judge each high yield on its payout sustainability and sector context, and don't dismiss all high yields, but don't trust any of them on the headline number alone.

How does interest rate environment affect REIT yields?

The interest-rate environment strongly influences REIT yields. When interest rates rise, REIT share prices often fall (because their dividends compete with now-higher-yielding bonds, and because higher rates raise borrowing costs and can lower property values), which mechanically pushes REIT yields up. So in rising-rate periods, you'll often see broadly higher REIT yields — sometimes reflecting genuine value, sometimes reflecting real pressure on cash flow and dividends, especially for rate-sensitive REITs like mortgage REITs or those with heavy floating-rate debt. When rates fall, REIT prices often rise and yields compress. This rate sensitivity is why a high yield during a rate spike can sometimes be an opportunity (quality REITs marked down by macro fears) and sometimes a warning (REITs whose fundamentals are genuinely strained by higher rates). So you can't read REIT yields without considering the rate backdrop. So in a rising-rate environment, scrutinize whether a higher yield reflects a market-wide repricing of a sound REIT or genuine stress on a vulnerable one — the sustainability and balance-sheet checks become especially important when rates are moving.

Should income investors hold a diversified set of REITs?

Yes — diversifying across REITs is generally wise for income investors, because it reduces the impact of any single dividend cut, sector downturn, or company-specific problem on your overall income. Holding REITs across different sectors (for example, residential, industrial, healthcare, net-lease) and avoiding over-concentration in one name or one rate-sensitive corner of the market means that if one REIT cuts its dividend or one sector struggles, your total income and capital are less affected. A yield watchlist approach naturally supports this, since you're evaluating candidates across sectors rather than piling into the highest single yield. Diversification doesn't eliminate risk — REITs as a group can fall together in a downturn or rate shock — but it smooths the income stream and lowers the damage from individual mistakes or surprises. So sizing each REIT position appropriately and spreading exposure across sectors is a sound discipline for income-focused REIT investing. So combine diversification with the per-REIT sustainability checks, rather than relying on either alone, to build a more resilient income stream — and size the overall REIT allocation to fit your broader plan and risk tolerance.

How are REIT dividends taxed, and does that affect yield?

REIT dividend taxation affects the after-tax income you actually keep from a yield, so it's worth understanding — though Baker 1031 doesn't provide tax advice. Most REIT ordinary dividends are taxed as ordinary income rather than at the lower qualified-dividend rates, because the REIT paid no corporate tax. However, a 20% deduction under Section 199A applies to qualified REIT dividends, lowering the effective top federal rate on those dividends; the 199A deduction was made permanent by the 2025 OBBBA legislation. Some distributions are classified as return of capital (which reduces your cost basis rather than being currently taxed) or as capital-gain distributions (taxed at capital-gains rates), and the REIT reports the breakdown on Form 1099-DIV. Because taxes reduce the income you keep, a headline yield isn't the same as your after-tax yield — and holding REITs in tax-advantaged accounts can change the calculus. So factor taxation into how you compare yields, and verify your specific treatment with your tax advisor, since the details are technical and depend on your situation and account type.
