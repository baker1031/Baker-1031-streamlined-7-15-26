Can You 1031 Into a REIT? The DST + 721 Path

By Gerald F. “Jerry” Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read

Can you 1031 exchange into a REIT? Not directly — REIT shares are securities, not like-kind real property. But a compliant two-step path exists. This guide explains why you can't 1031 directly into a REIT, how the DST + 721 path works, how it maintains your tax deferral, and whether it's right for you.

It's one of the most common questions real estate investors ask: can I sell my property in a 1031 exchange and reinvest into a REIT to get out of active landlording while deferring my capital-gains tax? The direct answer is no — Section 1031 requires like-kind real property, and REIT shares are securities, which the tax code specifically excludes. But that isn't the end of the story. There's a well-established, compliant two-step route that ultimately reaches a REIT while preserving your deferral: first you 1031 into a Delaware Statutory Trust (DST), which is 1031-eligible real property, and later the DST's property is acquired by the sponsor's REIT through a 721 (UPREIT) exchange, converting your interest into REIT operating-partnership units tax-deferred. This guide explains why a direct 1031 into a REIT isn't allowed, how the DST + 721 path works step by step, how it maintains deferral, and whether it's right for you. Baker 1031 does not provide tax or legal advice — verify the rules and your specific situation with your tax advisor; this is educational information.

The DST + 721 path is one route into REIT ownership; for the bigger picture on how private and non-traded REITs work alongside it, see our complete 2026 guide to private and non-traded REITs and the 721 exchange.

Why You Can't 1031 Directly Into a REIT

The reason you can't 1031 directly into a REIT comes down to what Section 1031 requires and what a REIT share legally is. A 1031 exchange lets you defer capital-gains tax when you exchange like-kind real property held for investment or business use for other like-kind real property. The key phrase is 'like-kind real property' — the replacement must be real estate, not a security or other type of asset. The tax code is explicit that interests in entities (stocks, bonds, partnership interests, and shares of a corporation or trust treated as one) do not qualify for like-kind exchange treatment.

A REIT share is precisely such an excluded interest. When you buy a REIT share, you don't own the underlying real estate directly — you own a piece of a company (or trust treated as a corporation) that owns the real estate. Legally, your interest is a security, not a direct interest in real property. So even though a REIT clearly involves real estate, exchanging your property for REIT shares is, in the eyes of the tax code, exchanging real property for a security — which doesn't qualify as like-kind. That's why a sale of property followed by a purchase of REIT shares is a taxable event, not a deferred exchange.

So you can't 1031 directly into a REIT because a REIT share is a security, not the like-kind real property a 1031 requires. So this rule sets up the need for an indirect path. Why you can't 1031 directly into a REIT — because Section 1031 requires like-kind real property, the tax code specifically excludes interests in entities (including REIT shares, which are securities representing ownership of a company that owns real estate rather than direct ownership of the real estate itself), and so exchanging property for REIT shares is a taxable purchase of a security, not a deferred like-kind exchange — explains a hard rule. A REIT share's legal character disqualifies it. Understanding this sets up the compliant workaround. Step 1: 1031 Into a DST

The compliant route begins by doing something the tax code does allow: a 1031 exchange into a Delaware Statutory Trust (DST). A DST is a trust that holds one or more specific real estate properties, and investors own fractional beneficial interests in it. Critically, under IRS Revenue Ruling 2004-86, a DST interest is treated as a direct, undivided interest in the underlying real property — so it qualifies as like-kind replacement property for a 1031 exchange. This means you can sell your investment property and reinvest the proceeds into a DST while fully deferring your capital-gains tax, just as you could with a like-kind replacement building.

Practically, the DST step gives you a passive replacement: the DST's sponsor manages the property, and you receive your share of the rental income without landlord responsibilities. You complete the exchange within the usual 1031 timelines (identify replacement property within 45 days, close within 180 days), and a DST can be a convenient identification because it's a defined, available investment. At this stage, you've achieved the immediate goal — deferred your gain and moved into passive real estate — but you're in a DST, not yet a REIT. The bridge to the REIT comes in step two.

So step one is a straightforward 1031 exchange into a DST, which is 1031-eligible real property, deferring your gain and moving you into passive ownership. So the DST is the compliant entry point. The DST is the linchpin: it's the one passive, fractional vehicle that the tax code treats as like-kind real property, so it can legally receive your 1031 exchange where a REIT share cannot.

Step 2: 721 Roll-Up Into the REIT

The second step is where REIT exposure enters the picture, through a 721 exchange (also called an UPREIT transaction). After you've held your DST interest, the DST's sponsor — often affiliated with a REIT — may have the DST's property acquired by the REIT's operating partnership. Under Section 721 of the tax code, contributing property to a partnership in exchange for partnership interests is generally a potentially tax-free event, so when the REIT's operating partnership acquires the DST's property in exchange for operating-partnership (OP) units, your interest converts into OP units without triggering the gain you deferred.

After the 721 roll-up, you hold operating-partnership units in the REIT's operating partnership rather than a DST interest. These OP units typically entitle you to distributions comparable to the REIT's dividends and represent an economic interest tied to the REIT's diversified portfolio — so you've effectively moved from a single-property DST into a diversified REIT structure, while your deferral remains intact. OP units can usually be converted into actual REIT shares over time, at your election. So the 721 step is the bridge that carries you from the DST into the REIT's structure tax-deferred, completing the two-step path that a direct 1031 into a REIT couldn't accomplish.

Maintaining Tax Deferral

The whole point of the DST + 721 path is to maintain tax deferral throughout, so it's worth tracing how the deferral survives each step. When you 1031 into the DST, your original capital gain is deferred under Section 1031, and your basis carries over into the DST interest. When the DST's property is rolled into the REIT's operating partnership via a 721 exchange, Section 721 treats the contribution as non-taxable, so no gain is recognized — your deferred gain and carryover basis simply continue into the OP units. At no point in property → DST → OP units is the deferred tax triggered.

There is, however, an important point at which deferral ends: when you convert OP units into REIT shares and sell, or otherwise dispose of the units in a taxable transaction. Converting OP units to REIT shares is generally a taxable event that triggers the deferred gain, because you're moving from a partnership interest into a security. So the deferral chain runs property → DST → OP units and pauses there; the eventual conversion to shares (and sale) is the taxable moment. Many investors hold OP units for income and diversification and defer that conversion — and if they hold until death, heirs may receive a step-up in basis that can eliminate the deferred gain entirely.

So deferral is maintained through the 1031 into the DST and the 721 into OP units, and ends when OP units convert to REIT shares and are sold. So understanding where deferral pauses is essential. The deferral runs all the way from your property to your operating-partnership units — but converting those units into REIT shares and selling is the moment the deferred tax finally comes due, so timing that conversion is its own decision.

Is This Path Right for You?

Whether the DST + 721 path is right for you depends on your goals and your willingness to accept its trade-offs. On the benefit side, it lets you exit active real estate management, defer your capital-gains tax, and ultimately gain a diversified, professionally-managed REIT interest with potential liquidity (OP units can convert to shares you could sell) — and if you hold until death, the step-up can eliminate the deferred gain for your heirs. For an investor tired of landlording but facing a large tax bill on a sale, this path can be compelling.

But the trade-offs are real and worth weighing carefully. The path is largely one-way: once you're in OP units, they're no longer 1031-eligible like-kind real property, so you generally can't 1031 back out of them into another property — your future flexibility narrows. The eventual conversion of OP units to REIT shares (and sale) is taxable, ending the deferral. You give up control over specific properties (you're in a diversified portfolio, not a building you chose), and you're dependent on the sponsor and REIT's management and the timing of the roll-up. The structure also has to exist — not every DST offers a 721 path, and the timing of the REIT's acquisition isn't fully in your control. So this path suits investors who value diversification, passivity, and the deferral-to-step-up potential, and accept giving up control and future 1031 flexibility.

So whether the path fits depends on weighing diversification, passivity, deferral, and potential liquidity and step-up against the one-way nature, eventual taxable conversion, and loss of control. So the decision is personal and worth careful thought. How the Process Works in Practice

In practice, the DST + 721 path unfolds over years and requires coordination, so it helps to understand the sequence and what to confirm at each stage. It begins when you sell your property and engage a qualified intermediary to hold the proceeds, then identify and close into a DST within the 1031 timelines (45 days to identify, 180 days to close). At selection, it's important to choose a DST that offers — or is sponsored by a REIT that contemplates — a future 721/UPREIT roll-up, since not every DST leads to a REIT; the offering documents and sponsor disclosures address whether and how a roll-up is anticipated.

After the DST holds the property for a period, the sponsor's REIT may execute the 721 exchange, converting your DST interest into OP units — a step whose timing depends on the sponsor and isn't fully in your control. From there, you hold OP units, receive distributions, and decide if and when to convert to REIT shares (a taxable step) based on your liquidity needs and tax planning. Throughout, work closely with your CPA and attorney: the 1031, the 721, and the eventual conversion each have technical requirements and tax consequences, and the rules and your circumstances should be verified at each stage. The path is well-established but document-intensive and time-sensitive.

So in practice the path runs sale → qualified intermediary → DST (within 1031 timelines) → 721 roll-up into OP units → eventual optional conversion to shares, with professional coordination throughout. Frequently asked questions

Can you 1031 directly into a REIT?

No — you cannot complete a 1031 exchange directly into a REIT, because REIT shares are securities, not like-kind real property. A 1031 exchange requires you to exchange like-kind real property held for investment or business for other like-kind real property, and the tax code specifically excludes interests in entities (stocks, bonds, partnership interests, and corporate or trust shares) from like-kind treatment. A REIT share is exactly such an excluded interest: when you own a REIT share, you own a piece of a company that owns real estate, not the real estate itself, so your interest is a security. That means selling your property and buying REIT shares is a taxable event, not a deferred exchange. There is, however, a compliant indirect route: you 1031 into a Delaware Statutory Trust (DST), which is 1031-eligible real property, and the DST's property is later rolled into a REIT through a 721 exchange, converting your interest into REIT operating-partnership units tax-deferred. So you can't 1031 directly into a REIT, but the DST + 721 path can reach one while preserving deferral.

What is the DST + 721 path to a REIT?

The DST + 721 path is a compliant, two-step route that lets a 1031 exchanger ultimately reach a REIT while deferring capital-gains tax. Step one: you complete a 1031 exchange into a Delaware Statutory Trust (DST), which under IRS Revenue Ruling 2004-86 is treated as a direct interest in real property and therefore qualifies as like-kind replacement property — deferring your gain and moving you into passive, professionally-managed real estate. Step two: after a holding period, the DST's sponsor — often affiliated with a REIT — has the DST's property acquired by the REIT's operating partnership through a 721 (UPREIT) exchange. Under Section 721, this contribution is potentially tax-free, so your DST interest converts into operating-partnership (OP) units without triggering the deferred gain, moving you into a diversified REIT structure. OP units can later be converted to REIT shares (a taxable step). So the DST + 721 path uses two non-taxable steps — a 1031 into a DST, then a 721 into OP units — to reach a REIT tax-deferred, which a direct 1031 into REIT shares cannot accomplish.

Why isn't a REIT share considered like-kind property?

Because a REIT share is, legally, an interest in a corporation (or a trust treated as one) — a security — not a direct interest in real estate. A 1031 exchange requires the exchange of like-kind real property held for investment or business, and the tax code specifically excludes interests in entities — including stocks, bonds, partnership interests, and shares of a corporation or trust — from like-kind exchange treatment. When you own a REIT share, you own a piece of a company that owns and operates real estate; you don't own the underlying buildings directly. So your interest is a security representing indirect ownership, not the like-kind real property a 1031 requires. By contrast, a DST interest is structured under IRS Revenue Ruling 2004-86 so that each investor is treated as owning a direct, undivided interest in the underlying real property — which is exactly why a DST qualifies for a 1031 and a REIT share doesn't. So it's the legal character of the interest — a security versus a direct real-property interest — that determines eligibility, not the fact that both ultimately involve real estate. Confirm specifics with your tax advisor.

What is a 721 exchange?

A 721 exchange — named for Section 721 of the tax code and also called an UPREIT transaction — is a generally potentially tax-free contribution of property to a partnership in exchange for partnership interests. In the REIT context, it works like this: a REIT typically operates through an 'umbrella partnership' (the operating partnership, hence 'UPREIT'). When you contribute real property — or, in the DST + 721 path, when the DST's property is acquired by that operating partnership — Section 721 treats the exchange of property for operating-partnership (OP) units as a non-taxable event, so no gain is recognized at that step. You receive OP units representing an economic interest in the REIT's portfolio, typically entitling you to distributions comparable to the REIT's dividends. Those OP units can usually be converted into actual REIT shares over time, though that conversion is generally a taxable event. So a 721 exchange is the mechanism that lets property (or a DST interest) move into a REIT's operating partnership tax-deferred, and it's the second step that completes the DST + 721 path to a REIT. It preserves the deferral established in the original 1031.

Does the 721 roll-up trigger my deferred tax?

No — the 721 roll-up itself does not trigger your deferred capital-gains tax. Under Section 721 of the tax code, contributing property to a partnership in exchange for partnership interests is generally a non-taxable event, so when the REIT's operating partnership acquires the DST's property and issues you operating-partnership (OP) units in exchange for your interest, no gain is recognized at that step. Your deferred gain and carryover basis simply continue into the OP units. So the deferral you established with your original 1031 into the DST survives the 721 roll-up intact. There is, however, a later point where deferral ends: when you convert your OP units into REIT shares and sell them (or otherwise dispose of the units in a taxable transaction), the deferred gain is generally triggered, because you're moving from a partnership interest into a security and realizing the gain. Many investors hold their OP units to defer that moment, and holding until death may allow heirs a step-up in basis that eliminates the deferred tax. So the 721 step preserves deferral; the eventual conversion to shares is the taxable moment. Confirm the timing with your tax advisor.

When does the tax deferral end in the DST + 721 path?

The deferral runs through several non-taxable steps and ends at conversion-and-sale. When you 1031 into the DST, your gain is deferred under Section 1031. When the DST's property is rolled into the REIT's operating partnership via a 721 exchange, Section 721 makes that step non-taxable, so the deferral continues into your operating-partnership (OP) units. Throughout property → DST → OP units, no deferred tax is triggered. The deferral ends when you convert your OP units into REIT shares and sell them (or dispose of the units in a taxable transaction): converting from a partnership interest into a security and realizing the gain is generally a taxable event that triggers the deferred tax. Many investors hold their OP units for income and diversification, deferring that conversion as long as they wish — and if they hold until death, their heirs may receive a step-up in basis to fair market value, which can eliminate the deferred capital-gains tax entirely. So deferral is maintained from the property through the DST and into OP units, and ends only when OP units are converted to REIT shares and sold, unless held until death for a potential step-up. Plan the timing with your advisor.

What are operating-partnership (OP) units?

Operating-partnership (OP) units are the partnership interests you receive in a 721/UPREIT exchange. A REIT typically operates through an umbrella operating partnership that holds its real estate; when property (or a DST interest) is contributed to that operating partnership under Section 721, you receive OP units representing an economic interest in the partnership — and indirectly in the REIT's diversified portfolio. OP units generally entitle you to distributions comparable to the REIT's dividends, so you receive ongoing income tied to the REIT's performance. Importantly, the contribution that creates OP units is tax-deferred under Section 721, so your deferred gain carries into the units without being triggered. Over time, OP units can usually be converted into actual REIT shares (often on a one-for-one or formula basis), at your election — but that conversion is generally a taxable event that triggers the deferred gain. So OP units are the tax-deferred bridge between a contributed property (or DST interest) and full REIT-share ownership: you hold them for income and diversification while deferral continues, and convert to shares when you're ready to accept the tax consequence or need the liquidity. They're central to how the DST + 721 path works.

Can I 1031 out of OP units later?

Generally no — once your interest has been converted into operating-partnership (OP) units through a 721 exchange, those units are partnership interests, not like-kind real property, so you typically cannot 1031 exchange out of them into another property. This is one of the most important trade-offs of the DST + 721 path: it's largely a one-way road. While you could 1031 between DSTs or other real property before the 721 step, the 721 roll-up into OP units narrows your future flexibility, because the tax code excludes partnership interests from like-kind exchange treatment. From the OP-unit stage, your main options are to hold the units (continuing to defer and collect distributions), convert them to REIT shares (a taxable event), or hold until death for a potential step-up. You generally can't move back into direct real estate via a 1031. So before electing the 721 path, it's important to understand that you're giving up the ability to 1031 back out — your future choices become hold, convert-and-sell, or pass to heirs. This loss of 1031 flexibility is a key reason the path suits investors ready to commit to a REIT structure long-term. Confirm the specifics with your tax advisor.

What are the benefits of the DST + 721 path?

The DST + 721 path offers several benefits for the right investor. First, it lets you exit active real estate management — no more tenants, maintenance, or financing to handle — moving from direct ownership into passive, professionally-managed real estate. Second, it defers your capital-gains tax: the 1031 into the DST and the 721 into OP units are both non-taxable, so you postpone the tax you'd owe on a straight sale. Third, it ultimately gives you a diversified REIT interest — instead of a single property, you hold an economic stake in a portfolio of properties, reducing concentration risk. Fourth, it offers potential liquidity: OP units can convert to REIT shares that you could sell (subject to the tax on conversion), unlike a single DST or direct property. Fifth, it carries estate-planning potential — if you hold the OP units until death, your heirs may receive a step-up in basis that eliminates the deferred capital-gains tax entirely. So the path combines passivity, deferral, diversification, potential liquidity, and a possible step-up — a compelling package for an investor tired of landlording but facing a large tax bill. The benefits must be weighed against the trade-offs.

What are the drawbacks of the DST + 721 path?

The DST + 721 path has real trade-offs to weigh. First, it's largely one-way: once you're in operating-partnership (OP) units, they're no longer 1031-eligible like-kind real property, so you generally can't 1031 back out into another property — your future flexibility narrows. Second, the eventual conversion of OP units to REIT shares (and their sale) is a taxable event that triggers the deferred gain, ending the deferral. Third, you give up control over specific properties — you move from a building you chose into a diversified portfolio managed by the REIT, so you no longer pick or control the assets. Fourth, you're dependent on the sponsor and the REIT's management, and on the timing of the 721 roll-up, which isn't fully in your control. Fifth, the structure has to exist — not every DST contemplates a 721 path, so you must select one that does. And as with any REIT, market, interest-rate, and management risks apply, and distributions aren't guaranteed. So the drawbacks center on the one-way nature, the eventual taxable conversion, loss of control, and dependence on the sponsor and timing. Weigh these carefully against the benefits before pursuing the path.

Is the DST + 721 path right for me?

It depends on your goals and your willingness to accept the trade-offs. The path tends to suit an investor who wants to exit active real estate management, defer a significant capital-gains tax, and ultimately hold a diversified, professionally-managed REIT interest with potential liquidity — and who may value the estate-planning potential of a step-up at death. If you're tired of landlording, facing a large tax bill on a sale, and comfortable in a diversified REIT structure for the long term, the path can be compelling. It tends not to suit an investor who wants to retain control over specific properties, preserve the ability to 1031 into future real estate, or avoid an eventual taxable conversion — because OP units are one-way and the conversion to shares is taxable. It also requires that a suitable DST with a contemplated 721 roll-up be available, and the timing depends on the sponsor. So the path is right for you if you prioritize passivity, deferral, diversification, and potential liquidity, and accept giving up control and future 1031 flexibility. It's a personal decision best made with your CPA, attorney, and a broker-dealer after a suitability review. Weigh the benefits and trade-offs against your specific situation.

Does every DST offer a 721 path to a REIT?

No — not every DST offers or contemplates a 721 roll-up into a REIT. Some DSTs are structured as standalone investments where the sponsor simply holds the property for a period and then sells it, returning proceeds to investors (who can take the cash, paying any deferred tax, or 1031 into another property). Others are sponsored by firms affiliated with a REIT and are specifically structured to anticipate a future 721/UPREIT roll-up, in which the DST's property is acquired by the sponsor's REIT and investors' interests convert to OP units. If reaching a REIT via the 721 path is your goal, it's essential to select a DST that contemplates such a roll-up — the offering documents and sponsor disclosures address whether and how a 721 exchange is anticipated, though even then the timing isn't guaranteed and depends on the sponsor's decision. So before investing, confirm that the specific DST is designed to lead to a REIT if that's your objective; don't assume any DST will. A broker-dealer can help you identify DSTs structured for a 721 path. So choose deliberately, and verify the structure and the sponsor's intentions in the offering documents before committing to the path.

How long does the DST + 721 path take?

The DST + 721 path typically unfolds over a multi-year period, and the exact timing depends on the sponsor and the structure. The first step — the 1031 exchange into the DST — follows the standard 1031 timelines: you have 45 days from your property sale to identify replacement property and 180 days to close into the DST. After that, you hold the DST interest for a holding period that varies by offering, and the second step — the 721 roll-up into the REIT's operating partnership — occurs when the sponsor's REIT decides to acquire the DST's property, which isn't fully within your control and can take a few years. Once you hold OP units, you decide if and when to convert to REIT shares, which can be years later or at your discretion (subject to the tax consequence). So the path can span several years from the initial sale to OP-unit ownership, and longer if you hold the OP units before converting. Because the timing of the 721 roll-up depends on the sponsor, it's important to understand the anticipated timeline in the offering documents and to plan with your CPA. So expect a multi-year horizon, with the 1031 step on a fixed timeline and the 721 step on the sponsor's timing.

Will my heirs owe the deferred tax in the DST + 721 path?

Not necessarily — and this is one of the more powerful estate-planning features of the path. As long as you hold your interest (the DST, and then the operating-partnership units) and don't convert OP units to shares and sell, your capital-gains tax remains deferred. If you continue to hold the OP units until your death, your heirs may receive a step-up in basis to the fair market value as of your death — which can eliminate the deferred capital-gains tax entirely, so your heirs inherit the interest without owing the tax you deferred. This 'defer until death, then step up' dynamic is the same principle that makes 1031 exchanges generally powerful for legacy planning, extended through the 721 structure. That said, estate tax, the specifics of how the interest is held, and the prevailing rules all matter, and the step-up rules can change, so this is an area to plan carefully with your estate and tax advisors. So your heirs may not owe the deferred capital-gains tax if you hold the OP units until death and they receive a step-up — but confirm the current rules and your specific situation with your tax and estate professionals, because the details are technical and consequential.
