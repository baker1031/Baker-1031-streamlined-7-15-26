1031 Exchange Deadline Calculator (45/180 Days)

Your whole exchange hinges on two dates. A deadline calculator pins them down from your closing date — and flags the tax-return-date trap. This complete guide covers how it works, counting the days, the variants, and using the result.
In a 1031 exchange, two dates matter more than any others, and missing either ends the exchange. A deadline calculator takes your closing date and returns your exact day-45 identification deadline and day-180 closing deadline — so you can plan backward with no ambiguity. Because timing is what sinks most failed exchanges, knowing these dates precisely, building in buffer, and avoiding the tax-return-date trap is among the most important things you can do. This guide explains how the deadline calculator works, how the days are counted, the variants for reverse and improvement exchanges, and how to use the result. Your 45-Day Identification Deadline
The calculator adds 45 calendar days to your closing date to give your identification deadline — the date by which your written, signed identification of replacement property must reach your qualified intermediary. This is the deadline most exchanges fail.
Forty-five days is short for sourcing and committing to replacement property, so knowing the exact date lets you work backward and aim to identify well before it (often day 30–40) rather than at the wire.
The 45-day deadline is firm — no grace period, no extension on request. The calculator's date is the hard line by which you must have delivered a valid identification, so treat it as immovable.
Your 180-Day Closing Deadline
The calculator then adds 180 calendar days for your closing deadline — the date by which you must acquire the replacement property. Remember the tax-return-date caveat: if your return is due sooner, that date controls unless you extend.
The 180-day deadline gives you the remaining 135 days after identification to negotiate, finance, and close. Knowing the exact date lets you set a target closing well before it (often day 160–165) to build buffer for slippage.
Like the 45-day deadline, the 180-day deadline is firm. The calculator's date is the last day you can close and still complete the exchange, so plan to finish ahead of it.
Calendar-Day Counting Notes
Days are counted as calendar days, including weekends and holidays, beginning the day after closing. There's no grace period and no automatic roll to the next business day if a deadline falls on a weekend or holiday — unlike some tax deadlines.
This catches people off guard, so the calculator's precise dates are valuable. If day 45 or day 180 lands on a Saturday, it still counts as the deadline; you can't wait until Monday.
Count carefully and conservatively. The calculator does the arithmetic, but you should understand that the dates are firm calendar days, which is why building buffer before each is wise.
The Tax-Return-Date Trap
The calculator may flag, or you should remember, that your actual closing deadline is the earlier of 180 days or your tax-return due date (including extensions) for the year of the sale. A late-year sale can make your return due before day 180, shortening your window.
If you sell in, say, November or December, your individual return is due the following April 15 — which can arrive before your 180 days run out. The fix is to file an extension for that year's return, restoring the full 180 days.
A deadline calculator that accounts for this, or a note to check it, prevents year-end sellers from losing weeks of their window. If you sell late in the year, plan to extend so the calendar, not the filing date, governs.
Key Takeaways
Both deadlines count in calendar days from your closing date — day 45 to identify, day 180 to close.
Days include weekends and holidays with no grace period; build buffer before each.
A late-year sale can trigger the tax-return-date trap — file an extension to keep all 180 days.
Deadlines for Reverse and Improvement Exchanges
The standard 45/180-day deadlines apply to a forward (delayed) exchange. In a reverse exchange , where you buy first, the deadlines apply to the parking arrangement: from when the exchange accommodation titleholder parks a property, you generally have 45 days to identify the relinquished property to sell and 180 days to complete.
In an improvement exchange , all value-adding construction must be completed and paid for within the same 180-day window. The deadline calculator's 180-day date is the limit for finishing improvements, not just acquiring the property.
For these variants, the calculator's dates still anchor the timeline, but the activities within the window differ. Understanding which deadline governs which activity is important for the more complex structures.
Planning Backward From the Deadlines
With the exact dates in hand, plan backward to set milestones. A practical schedule might target identification by day 35–40, a replacement under contract by day 70–90, financing approved by day 130, and closing by day 160–165 — each leaving buffer before the next.
Planning backward turns the two deadline dates into a concrete project plan, rather than a vague sense of "I have six months." The investors who finish cleanly are the ones who map the timeline from the deadlines, not toward them.
Put the Dates to Work: Reminders and Your Team
Once you have the dates, set reminders well ahead of each — a couple of weeks before each deadline gives buffer if a closing slips. Then share the dates with everyone involved: your qualified intermediary, CPA, advisor, and any lender.
Each has a role tied to the timeline. Your QI tracks the dates and holds the funds; your CPA watches for the tax-return-date interaction and handles any extension; your advisor coordinates the replacement-property search against the dates; and your lender works to have financing ready before the closing deadline. When everyone works from the same two dates, you avoid the misaligned assumptions that quietly derail exchanges.
Why Deadlines Cause Most Failed Exchanges
Almost every failed 1031 exchange dies on the calendar, not on the law. The substantive rules — like-kind, same taxpayer, value — are usually satisfied by careful planning. The deadlines, by contrast, are a relentless clock that runs whether or not the market cooperates or your financing comes together.
The 45-day identification deadline causes the most failures, because committing to replacement property in just over six weeks is genuinely hard. Investors who start searching only after they sell often haven't lined up enough viable options by day 45, and after that they can't add any.
This is why a deadline calculator — and the precise dates it provides — matters so much. Knowing exactly when each deadline falls, from the moment you close, lets you work backward and prepare, rather than discovering too late that time has run out. The calculator is a small tool that addresses the single biggest cause of failed exchanges.
The Deadlines and Your DST Backup
The deadline calculator's dates and a DST backup can work together to protect your exchange. A DST is a passive replacement-property option that — subject to offering availability, completion of accreditation and suitability review, and the sponsor's acceptance of your subscription — may be able to close relatively quickly, so identifying one within your 45-day window can give you an additional route to close inside the 180-day deadline if a direct deal stalls.
When you know your exact deadlines and have a DST identified as a backup, the timeline is easier to manage. If your primary deal is at risk as a deadline approaches, the calculator tells you how much time remains, and a suitable DST may give you a way to use it.
DSTs are illiquid securities offered only to verified accredited investors through a sponsor's private placement memorandum (PPM), which should be read in full for risks, fees, and expenses before investing; they involve substantial risk, including possible loss of principal and the risk that an intended 1031 exchange fails to qualify for tax deferral. Knowing your precise dates and lining up a suitable backup early are complementary parts of a prepared process.
Using the Deadline Calculator Effectively
The deadline calculator is most useful when run at (or even before) closing, so you know your dates from day one. Pair it with the front-loaded preparation the rest of this guide series describes — starting your replacement search before you sell, lining up financing, and identifying a DST backup.
Knowing the dates is necessary but not sufficient; you also need to act on them with a prepared process. The calculator gives you the deadlines; your preparation determines whether you meet them comfortably.
If a deadline is approaching and a deal is at risk, the dates also tell you how much time remains to pivot to a backup such as a DST — provided you have one identified and it remains available and suitable. Combined with that backup and a prepared team, the calculator helps turn the unforgiving 1031 timeline into a manageable one.
Frequently Asked Questions
How do I calculate my 1031 exchange deadlines?
Add 45 calendar days to your relinquished sale's closing date for the identification deadline, and 180 calendar days for the closing deadline. A deadline calculator does this from your closing date, returning both exact dates.
Do weekends and holidays count toward the deadlines?
Yes. The 45- and 180-day periods are counted in calendar days, including weekends and holidays, with no grace period. If a deadline falls on a weekend or holiday, it still counts — there's no automatic roll to the next business day.
Can my tax-return date shorten the 180 days?
Yes. The closing deadline is the earlier of 180 days or your tax-return due date (with extensions) for the year of the sale. A late-year sale can make your return due before day 180. File an extension to preserve the full 180 days.
When do the deadlines start?
On the date your relinquished property's sale closes — when title transfers and the qualified intermediary receives the proceeds. Day one is the day after closing, and both the 45-day and 180-day periods run concurrently from that date.
When should I set reminders?
Well before each deadline — for example two weeks before day 45 and two weeks before day 180 — to give buffer if a closing slips. Aim to identify by day 35–40 and close by day 160–165, working backward from the calculator's dates.
Are the deadlines ever extended?
Not on request. The IRS occasionally postpones deadlines for taxpayers affected by federally declared disasters via specific notices, but otherwise they're fixed. Plan as though no extension exists; the calculator's dates are firm.
How do the deadlines work in a reverse exchange?
The deadlines apply to the parking arrangement: from when the exchange accommodation titleholder parks a property, you generally have 45 days to identify the relinquished property to sell and 180 days to complete the transaction. The same calendar-day counting applies.
How do the deadlines work in an improvement exchange?
All value-adding construction must be completed and paid for within the 180-day window, not just the acquisition. The calculator's 180-day date is the limit for finishing improvements, which is the binding constraint in a construction exchange.
What if I miss a deadline?
The exchange generally fails — the sale becomes taxable and the qualified intermediary returns the funds. There's no grace period, which is why knowing the exact dates and building buffer (and a DST backup) matters so much.
Should I share the dates with my team?
Yes. Share the calculator's dates with your qualified intermediary, CPA, advisor, and lender so everyone works to the same timeline. Misalignment on the dates among your team is a common, avoidable source of delay.
When should I run the deadline calculator?
At or even before closing, so you know your dates from day one and can plan backward. Knowing the deadlines early lets you set milestones (identification, contract, financing, closing) with buffer and coordinate your team.
How do I plan backward from the deadlines?

Should I aim to finish exactly on the deadline?
No — aim to finish ahead of it. Target identification by day 35–40 and closing by day 160–165, using the calculator's dates as the absolute limits. Building buffer before each firm date protects against the slippage that affects nearly every real estate transaction.

What's the single most important date in my exchange?
Day 45 — the identification deadline — because it fails the most exchanges and, once passed, you can only acquire what you identified. The calculator pins down this date precisely, letting you work backward and identify confidently (ideally with a DST backup) well before it.

What time of day is the deadline?
The identification must be delivered to your qualified intermediary by midnight at the end of the 45th day, and the replacement acquired by the end of the 180th day. Don't cut it to the last hours — deliver identifications and close well before the date to allow for any delivery or closing delays.
How does the tax-return-date trap change my deadline?

What if a deadline falls on a weekend or holiday?
It still counts — the 1031 deadlines don't roll to the next business day like some tax deadlines. If day 45 or day 180 lands on a Saturday, Sunday, or holiday, that's still the deadline, which is another reason to finish ahead of the date rather than on it.
How much buffer should I build in?
Aim to identify by day 35–40 (versus the day-45 deadline) and close by day 160–165 (versus day 180). That buffer absorbs the slippage common in real estate — late appraisals, title issues, financing delays — so a small slip doesn't become a missed deadline.
Can I rely solely on the calculator for my timeline?
The calculator gives you accurate dates, but you still need a prepared process to meet them — starting your search before you sell, lining up financing, and identifying a DST backup. Knowing the dates is necessary but not sufficient; preparation is what lets you meet them comfortably.
Does the calculator account for the year-end timing issue?
A good calculator flags it or reminds you that a late-year sale can trigger the tax-return-date trap. The 45- and 180-day arithmetic is straightforward; the year-end caveat is the one nuance, so if you sell in the last few months of the year, plan to file an extension and confirm with your CPA.
Is the deadline calculator useful for an improvement exchange?
Yes — it gives you the 180-day date by which all value-adding construction must be completed and paid for, which is the binding constraint in an improvement exchange.
