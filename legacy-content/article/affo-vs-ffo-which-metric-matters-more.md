AFFO vs. FFO: Which Metric Matters More?
Back to All Insights
REIT Gerald F. "Jerry" Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read
AFFO and FFO are the two key REIT cash-flow metrics — but which matters more? This guide explains what AFFO adjusts for, the role of maintenance capex and straight-line rent, why AFFO tracks dividends, how FFO and AFFO differ in practice, and how to use both metrics together.
FFO (Funds From Operations) is the standard REIT operating metric, but it has a known blind spot: it ignores the recurring capital a REIT must spend to keep its properties competitive. AFFO (Adjusted FFO) closes that gap, refining FFO into a closer estimate of the cash that actually funds the dividend. So which matters more — FFO or AFFO? The honest answer is that they serve different purposes, and good REIT analysis uses both. FFO is more standardized and comparable across REITs, making it the workhorse for valuation; AFFO is more economically accurate as a cash measure, making it the better gauge of dividend sustainability — but its definitions vary by company, so it's less standardized. This guide explains what AFFO adjusts for, the role of maintenance capex and straight-line rent, why AFFO tracks dividends, how FFO and AFFO differ in practice, and how to use both together. This is educational information, not investment advice — verify current details and a REIT's own definitions before relying on them.
What AFFO Adjusts For
AFFO — Adjusted Funds From Operations — starts where FFO leaves off and adjusts it to better reflect the cash a REIT actually has available. FFO already corrects net income for non-cash real estate depreciation and one-time property-sale gains, giving a measure of recurring operating performance. But FFO still overstates the cash truly available to shareholders because it doesn't account for the ongoing capital a REIT must reinvest in its properties, and because it includes certain non-cash income items. AFFO adjusts for exactly these things.
Specifically, AFFO takes FFO and subtracts recurring maintenance capital expenditures (the capital needed to keep properties competitive and leased) and straight-line rent adjustments (a non-cash accounting smoothing of rent), along with other normalizations a given REIT applies — such as adjusting for non-cash compensation, leasing costs, or amortization of financing items. The goal is to strip out the remaining non-cash and recurring-reinvestment items so that what's left approximates the genuine, distributable cash flow the REIT generates. So AFFO is FFO refined: FFO minus maintenance capex, minus straight-line rent, minus other normalizations, yielding a closer estimate of real cash.

Maintenance Capex & Straight-Line Rent
The two most important AFFO adjustments deserve a closer look, because they explain why AFFO is lower than FFO and closer to real cash. Recurring maintenance capital expenditures are the ongoing capital a REIT must spend to keep its existing properties competitive and leased — replacing roofs and HVAC systems, funding tenant improvements, and paying leasing commissions to re-lease space. These are real, recurring cash outflows, but FFO ignores them entirely; AFFO subtracts them, which is the single biggest reason AFFO comes in below FFO.
Straight-line rent is an accounting convention: when a lease has scheduled rent increases over its term, GAAP requires the REIT to recognize the average rent evenly over the lease rather than the actual cash rent collected each period. In early years this records more rent than cash received (a non-cash addition to income); in later years, less. Because FFO includes this non-cash straight-line rent, AFFO removes the adjustment to reflect actual cash rent. Together, subtracting maintenance capex and removing the straight-line-rent adjustment moves AFFO from an accounting-based operating figure toward a genuine cash figure. So these two adjustments are what make AFFO a better cash proxy than FFO.

Why AFFO Tracks Dividends
AFFO matters most for one question above all: can the REIT sustain its dividend? Because AFFO subtracts the recurring maintenance capex and non-cash items that FFO ignores, it better approximates the distributable cash a REIT actually has available to pay shareholders. That makes the AFFO payout ratio — dividends paid divided by AFFO — a more meaningful gauge of dividend sustainability than the FFO payout ratio, which can look comfortable even when real cash is tighter.
The logic is direct: a REIT pays its dividend out of cash, not out of an accounting figure, so the metric that best estimates available cash is the one that best signals whether the dividend is covered. If a REIT's AFFO payout ratio is well below 100% (say, comfortably in the 70s or 80s as a percentage), the dividend appears well-covered by distributable cash, with a cushion. If the AFFO payout ratio approaches or exceeds 100%, the REIT is paying out most or all of its distributable cash — or more — which raises the risk of a dividend cut or reliance on borrowing or asset sales to fund the payout. FFO vs. AFFO in Practice
In practice, FFO and AFFO have a clear division of labor rooted in a trade-off between standardization and accuracy. FFO is more standardized and comparable: because Nareit defines it, two REITs' FFO are calculated on a broadly consistent basis, which makes FFO the workhorse for cross-REIT comparison and valuation (the P/FFO multiple). When you want to compare REITs' growth and valuation on a level playing field, FFO is the reliable common denominator.
AFFO is more economically accurate as a cash measure but less standardized. There's no single mandated AFFO definition, so companies differ in how they calculate maintenance capex (which is partly judgment — what counts as 'maintenance' versus 'growth' capital), straight-line rent, and other normalizations. This means one REIT's AFFO isn't always strictly comparable to another's, and a REIT could, in principle, define AFFO favorably. So in practice you use FFO for standardized comparison and valuation, and AFFO for assessing a given REIT's dividend coverage and true cash generation — while scrutinizing how each REIT defines its AFFO adjustments. So FFO is the comparable measure; AFFO is the accurate-but-variable one.

AFFO's Limitations & Scrutiny
AFFO's greater accuracy comes with a catch: because it isn't standardized, it requires more scrutiny than FFO. The biggest source of variability is maintenance capex, which involves judgment — the line between 'maintenance' capital (keeping a property competitive, which AFFO subtracts) and 'growth' or 'value-add' capital (expanding or improving a property, which is often not subtracted) is genuinely blurry, and REITs can classify spending in ways that flatter AFFO. A REIT that under-counts maintenance capex will report a higher AFFO and a more comfortable-looking payout ratio than reality warrants.
Other AFFO adjustments — straight-line rent, non-cash compensation, capitalized interest, leasing costs — also vary by company, and there's no enforcement body mandating consistency. This means you can't take AFFO at face value across REITs without understanding each one's definition. The practical defense is to read the REIT's reconciliation, see exactly which items it adjusts and how it defines maintenance capex, compare its capex intensity to sector norms, and be skeptical of an AFFO that looks too generous. So AFFO is the better cash measure, but only if you scrutinize how it's built — its lack of standardization is the price of its accuracy.

What is maintenance capex?
Maintenance capital expenditures (maintenance capex) are the recurring capital a REIT must spend to keep its existing properties competitive, functional, and leased — as opposed to growth or value-add capital spent to expand, develop, or substantially improve properties. Maintenance capex includes things like replacing roofs and HVAC systems, repaving parking lots, funding tenant improvements when re-leasing space, and paying leasing commissions to brokers. These are real, ongoing cash outflows that a REIT can't avoid if it wants to maintain its rental income over time. FFO ignores maintenance capex entirely, which is why FFO overstates the cash truly available to fund dividends; AFFO subtracts it, which is the single biggest reason AFFO is lower than FFO and closer to real cash. The tricky part is that classifying spending as 'maintenance' versus 'growth' capital involves judgment, and REITs can differ in how they draw the line — which is a key reason AFFO is less standardized than FFO and requires scrutiny. So maintenance capex is the recurring property-reinvestment cost that AFFO captures and FFO misses, and understanding how a REIT defines it is essential to trusting its AFFO figure.
What is straight-line rent?
Straight-line rent is an accounting convention required by GAAP for leases that have scheduled rent increases over their term. Rather than recording the actual cash rent collected each period, GAAP requires the REIT to recognize the average (straight-lined) rent evenly over the entire lease term. In the early years of such a lease, the straight-line rent recorded is higher than the cash rent actually received, so the difference is a non-cash addition to reported income; in the later years, the recorded rent is lower than the cash collected. The effect is to smooth reported rental income over the lease, but it means reported rent diverges from actual cash rent in any given period. Because FFO includes this non-cash straight-line rent, it overstates current cash income when leases are in their early years. AFFO removes the straight-line-rent adjustment to reflect the actual cash rent collected, which is one of the two core adjustments (alongside maintenance capex) that make AFFO a better cash proxy than FFO. So straight-line rent is a non-cash rent-smoothing convention that AFFO strips out to get closer to the real cash a REIT collects from its leases.
Why does AFFO track dividends better than FFO?
AFFO tracks dividends better than FFO because it more closely measures the cash a REIT actually has available to pay them. A REIT funds its dividend out of real cash, not out of an accounting figure — and AFFO, by subtracting recurring maintenance capex and non-cash items like straight-line rent that FFO ignores, better approximates that distributable cash. FFO, by contrast, overstates available cash because it pretends maintenance capital and non-cash income don't reduce what's distributable, so an FFO payout ratio can look comfortable even when the REIT's real cash is tighter. The AFFO payout ratio — dividends paid divided by AFFO — is therefore a more meaningful gauge of dividend sustainability. So AFFO tracks dividends better because it measures the very cash dividends come from — making it the key metric for income-focused REIT investors assessing whether a payout is sustainable.
What is the AFFO payout ratio?
The AFFO payout ratio is a REIT's dividends paid divided by its AFFO (Adjusted Funds From Operations), expressed as a percentage — a measure of how much of its distributable cash the REIT is paying out as dividends. It's considered a more meaningful gauge of dividend sustainability than the FFO payout ratio, because AFFO better approximates the actual cash available to fund the dividend (it subtracts maintenance capex and non-cash items that FFO ignores). Reading it is straightforward: an AFFO payout ratio comfortably below 100% — say, in the 70s or 80s as a percentage — suggests the dividend is well-covered by distributable cash, with a cushion that allows for reinvestment and protects against a downturn. A ratio approaching or exceeding 100% suggests the REIT is paying out most or all of its distributable cash (or more), which raises the risk of a dividend cut or reliance on borrowing or asset sales to maintain the payout. So the AFFO payout ratio is the key dividend-coverage metric for a REIT. Just remember that because AFFO definitions vary by company, you should check how the REIT defines its AFFO before drawing firm conclusions from the ratio.
Why isn't AFFO standardized?
AFFO isn't standardized because, unlike FFO, there's no single authoritative definition that all REITs must follow. Nareit defines FFO, so REITs calculate it on a broadly consistent basis, but AFFO is a refinement that each company computes using its own methodology. The biggest source of variability is maintenance capex: the line between 'maintenance' capital (keeping a property competitive, which AFFO subtracts) and 'growth' or 'value-add' capital (expanding or improving a property, often not subtracted) involves genuine judgment, and REITs can classify spending differently — a REIT that counts less spending as maintenance will report a higher AFFO. Other AFFO adjustments — straight-line rent, non-cash compensation, capitalized interest, leasing costs — also vary by company, with no enforcement body mandating consistency. This lack of standardization is the price of AFFO's greater economic accuracy: it captures real cash costs FFO misses, but at the cost of comparability. So you can't take AFFO at face value across REITs without reading each one's definition and reconciliation. The practical defense is to scrutinize how each REIT defines maintenance capex and its other adjustments, compare capex intensity to sector norms, and be skeptical of an AFFO that looks unusually generous.
How do I use FFO and AFFO together?
Use each metric for what it does best, in a complementary workflow. Start with FFO for standardized comparison and valuation: because Nareit defines it, FFO is the reliable basis for comparing REITs' growth (FFO per share) and valuation (the P/FFO multiple) across the sector on a level playing field, so it's your common denominator when sizing up multiple REITs against each other. Then drill into AFFO for dividend sustainability and true cash generation: because AFFO subtracts maintenance capex and non-cash items, it's the better estimate of the distributable cash that funds the dividend, and the AFFO payout ratio is the key coverage gauge — reading the REIT's reconciliation to check how it defines its adjustments. The practical sequence is: compare REITs with FFO per share and P/FFO, then evaluate a candidate's AFFO and AFFO payout ratio to judge dividend safety, scrutinizing the maintenance-capex definition. Finally, pair both with the balance sheet (leverage, debt maturities), NAV, and operating metrics (occupancy, same-store growth) for a complete view. So FFO and AFFO aren't rivals — using both, each for its purpose, gives a fuller and more reliable picture of a REIT than either alone.
Is AFFO always lower than FFO?
AFFO is almost always lower than FFO, and for a clear reason: AFFO subtracts items that FFO ignores. The largest of these is recurring maintenance capital expenditures — the ongoing cash a REIT must spend to keep its properties competitive and leased — which FFO doesn't account for but AFFO subtracts, pulling AFFO below FFO. AFFO also removes the non-cash straight-line rent adjustment that FFO includes, and other normalizations, most of which reduce the figure. Because these are genuine cash costs and non-cash income items, stripping them out leaves a smaller number that more closely reflects distributable cash. So in the vast majority of cases, AFFO comes in below FFO, and the gap between them is informative — a large gap suggests a capital-intensive REIT (or property type) that must reinvest heavily to maintain its income, while a small gap suggests a less capital-intensive one. There can be unusual periods or definitions where adjustments net differently, but as a rule AFFO is the lower, more conservative, more cash-focused figure. So expect AFFO below FFO, and pay attention to the size of the gap as a signal of capital intensity.
Can a REIT manipulate its AFFO?
A REIT can present its AFFO favorably because AFFO isn't standardized, which is why scrutiny matters. The main lever is maintenance capex classification: since the line between 'maintenance' capital (which AFFO subtracts) and 'growth' or 'value-add' capital (often not subtracted) involves genuine judgment, a REIT can classify more spending as growth and less as maintenance, which lowers the capex subtracted and raises reported AFFO — making the AFFO payout ratio look more comfortable than reality warrants. Other adjustments (straight-line rent, non-cash items, capitalized costs) can also be defined in ways that flatter the figure. This isn't necessarily fraud — it often reflects legitimate judgment differences — but it means you can't take AFFO at face value across REITs. The practical defense is to read the REIT's reconciliation to see exactly which items it adjusts and how it defines maintenance capex, compare its capex intensity to sector peers (a REIT spending far less on maintenance than similar REITs deserves a closer look), and be skeptical of an AFFO that looks unusually generous. So while a REIT can present AFFO in a flattering light, informed scrutiny of its definitions and comparison to peers helps you judge whether the figure is realistic.
Do non-traded REITs report FFO and AFFO too?
Yes — non-traded REITs report FFO and AFFO just as publicly traded REITs do, because both are REITs that prepare financial statements and want to communicate their operating performance and cash generation to investors. A non-traded REIT will typically present FFO and AFFO (and their per-share figures) in its periodic reports and offering materials, along with a reconciliation from net income, so you can evaluate its operating performance and dividend coverage using the same metrics you'd apply to a traded REIT. In fact, because non-traded REITs are illiquid and priced at periodic NAV rather than continuously by the market, metrics like AFFO and the AFFO payout ratio can be especially important for assessing whether the distribution is supported by genuine cash flow rather than by return of capital or borrowing. The same cautions apply — AFFO definitions vary, so scrutinize how the non-traded REIT defines maintenance capex and other adjustments. So FFO and AFFO are relevant for both traded and non-traded REITs, and for a non-traded REIT in particular, checking that distributions are covered by AFFO is a valuable part of due diligence, alongside reviewing fees, redemption terms, and the underlying real estate.
What does the gap between FFO and AFFO tell me?
The gap between a REIT's FFO and its AFFO is informative because it reflects how capital-intensive the REIT's real estate is — how much recurring capital the REIT must reinvest to keep its properties competitive and leased. Since AFFO equals FFO minus maintenance capex (and straight-line rent and other normalizations), a large gap means the REIT is spending heavily on maintenance capital and tenant improvements relative to its operating cash, while a small gap means it reinvests comparatively little to sustain its income. Property type drives much of this: net-lease REITs, where tenants often cover maintenance and improvements, tend to have a small FFO-to-AFFO gap, whereas office REITs, which fund substantial tenant improvements and leasing commissions to retain and attract tenants, tend to have a larger gap. So comparing the gap across REITs helps you see which ones generate more of their reported FFO as genuinely distributable cash. A REIT with a high FFO but a much lower AFFO is converting less of its headline performance into spendable cash than a REIT with FFO and AFFO close together. So read the gap as a signal of capital intensity and cash conversion, and factor it into how much weight you give FFO versus AFFO when judging dividend coverage and value.

Glossary
AFFO Adjusted Funds From Operations — FFO refined into a closer estimate of distributable cash.
FFO Funds From Operations — the standardized REIT operating measure AFFO builds on.
Maintenance Capex Recurring property reinvestment FFO ignores but AFFO subtracts.
Growth Capex Capital to expand or improve property, often not subtracted in AFFO.
Straight-Line Rent A non-cash GAAP convention averaging scheduled rent, removed in AFFO.
Distributable Cash The genuine cash a REIT can pay out, which AFFO approximates.
AFFO Payout Ratio Dividends divided by AFFO — the key dividend-sustainability gauge.
FFO Payout Ratio Dividends divided by FFO — looser than the AFFO version.
Nareit The body that standardizes FFO (but not AFFO).
P/FFO Price-to-FFO multiple — the standardized REIT valuation gauge.
Standardization FFO's consistent definition, which AFFO lacks.
Normalization Company-specific AFFO adjustments beyond capex and rent.
Reconciliation The disclosure showing how a REIT derives FFO and AFFO.
Dividend Coverage Whether distributable cash (AFFO) supports the dividend.
Capex Intensity How much a REIT must reinvest, signaled by the FFO-to-AFFO gap.
Non-Cash Items Accounting entries (like straight-line rent) AFFO removes for cash accuracy.

Which should you choose?
Use FFO for fast, apples-to-apples comparisons across REITs, since it follows a common Nareit definition and is reported consistently.
Lean on AFFO when you are judging whether a dividend is truly covered, because it subtracts the recurring maintenance capex and normalizes accounting items that FFO leaves in — just remember AFFO is not standardized, so confirm how each REIT calculates it before comparing.
