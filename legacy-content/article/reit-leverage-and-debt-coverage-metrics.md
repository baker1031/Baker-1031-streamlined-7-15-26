REIT Leverage & Debt Coverage Metrics
Back to All Insights
REIT Gerald F. "Jerry" Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read
How much debt is too much for a REIT, and how do you tell a sturdy balance sheet from a fragile one? This guide explains debt-to-EBITDA leverage, interest and fixed-charge coverage, debt maturity ladders, floating-versus-fixed debt, and how to assess a REIT's balance-sheet risk.
REITs are built on real estate, and real estate is bought with debt — so a REIT's balance sheet is central to its risk . Two REITs can own similar properties yet have very different risk profiles depending on how much they've borrowed, how well their cash flow covers their obligations, when their debt comes due, and whether that debt carries fixed or floating rates. Credit-minded investors gauge leverage with debt-to-EBITDA (and net debt to EBITDA), measure the ability to service obligations with interest and fixed-charge coverage, study the debt maturity ladder for refinancing risk, and assess exposure to rising rates through the fixed-versus-floating mix. Put together, these metrics separate a conservatively financed REIT — low leverage, strong coverage, laddered and mostly-fixed debt — from a fragile one. This guide walks through debt-to-EBITDA, coverage ratios, maturity ladders, floating-versus-fixed debt, and how to assess overall balance-sheet risk. Debt-to-EBITDA for REITs
The most common gauge of a REIT's leverage is debt-to-EBITDA — total debt divided by EBITDA (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization). Because EBITDA approximates a REIT's recurring operating cash flow before financing costs, the ratio expresses leverage as a multiple of cash flow: how many years of EBITDA it would take to repay the debt. A lower multiple means less leverage and more balance-sheet flexibility; a higher multiple means the REIT carries more debt relative to its earnings power.
Analysts often refine this to net debt-to-EBITDA, which subtracts the REIT's cash and equivalents from total debt before dividing — recognizing that cash on hand could be used to pay down debt. Net debt-to-EBITDA is generally the preferred leverage measure because it reflects the REIT's debt burden net of liquidity. What counts as conservative versus aggressive varies by property sector and strategy, so the ratio is best read against sector norms and a REIT's own history rather than a universal cutoff — but, broadly, lower net-debt-to-EBITDA multiples indicate a more conservatively financed REIT.
So debt-to-EBITDA (and net debt-to-EBITDA) expresses a REIT's leverage as a multiple of its cash flow — the headline gauge of how much it has borrowed relative to earnings. Interest & Fixed-Charge Coverage
Where debt-to-EBITDA measures how much a REIT owes, coverage ratios measure its ability to service what it owes. The interest coverage ratio divides EBITDA by interest expense, showing how many times over the REIT's cash flow covers its interest bill. A higher ratio means a thicker buffer — the REIT could absorb a meaningful drop in earnings or a rise in rates and still pay its interest; a low ratio means little margin between cash flow and the interest due.
The fixed-charge coverage ratio is a stricter, more complete version: it divides EBITDA by the sum of interest expense and preferred dividends (and sometimes other fixed obligations). Because many REITs issue preferred stock whose dividends must be paid before common dividends, fixed-charge coverage captures obligations that interest coverage alone misses, giving a fuller picture of how well cash flow covers all of the REIT's fixed financing commitments. Strong, stable coverage ratios are a hallmark of a financially sound REIT; weak or declining coverage is an early warning that the balance sheet is under strain.
So coverage ratios — interest coverage and the stricter fixed-charge coverage — measure how comfortably a REIT's cash flow covers its interest and other fixed financing obligations. Leverage tells you how much a REIT owes; coverage tells you whether it can comfortably pay — and a sturdy balance sheet needs both a sensible debt load and strong, stable coverage of every fixed charge.
Debt Maturity Ladders
Beyond how much a REIT owes and how well it covers it, when the debt comes due is critical. A debt maturity ladder shows the schedule of when a REIT's various loans and bonds mature. A well-laddered profile spreads maturities evenly across many years, so only a small portion comes due in any single year — which limits how much debt the REIT must refinance at once and reduces its exposure to any one bad moment in the credit markets.
The danger is a concentrated maturity wall — a large slug of debt all coming due in the same year or two. If those maturities arrive when interest rates are high or credit is tight, the REIT may be forced to refinance at much higher rates, issue equity at depressed prices, or sell assets under pressure — all of which can damage value. This refinancing risk is especially acute in a high-rate environment, where rolling over maturing debt means replacing cheap old debt with expensive new debt. So a laddered maturity schedule with no large near-term concentrations is a sign of prudent balance-sheet management, while a looming maturity wall is a clear risk to scrutinize.

Floating vs. Fixed Debt
The composition of a REIT's debt — how much carries a fixed interest rate versus a floating (variable) rate — determines how exposed it is to rising rates. Fixed-rate debt locks in the interest cost for the life of the loan, so the REIT's interest expense on that debt doesn't change when market rates rise. Floating-rate debt, by contrast, resets periodically with a benchmark rate, so its interest cost climbs as rates rise (and falls as rates fall).
A REIT with a high proportion of floating-rate debt is therefore more exposed to rising rates: as benchmark rates climb, its interest expense increases, squeezing cash flow, eroding coverage ratios, and pressuring the dividend — even if nothing about its properties has changed. A REIT that funds itself mostly with fixed-rate debt is insulated from that near-term rate risk, with its interest costs locked in. Some REITs also use interest-rate hedges (swaps or caps) to convert floating exposure to effectively fixed. So a predominantly fixed-rate, hedged debt structure is generally lower-risk in a rising-rate world, while heavy floating-rate exposure amplifies it.
So the fixed-versus-floating mix shapes a REIT's interest-rate risk — more floating means more exposure to rising rates, while mostly-fixed (or hedged) debt locks in costs. Assessing Balance-Sheet Risk
No single metric defines a REIT's balance-sheet risk; you assess it by reading the metrics together. A lower-risk balance sheet generally combines moderate net debt-to-EBITDA, strong and stable interest and fixed-charge coverage, a well-laddered maturity schedule with no large near-term concentrations, and predominantly fixed-rate (or hedged) debt. A higher-risk balance sheet shows the opposite — high leverage, thin or declining coverage, a looming maturity wall, and heavy floating-rate exposure. Loan-to-value (LTV) — debt as a share of property value — is another useful gauge, though it depends on property valuations that can move.
Context matters as much as the raw numbers. Sector and strategy shape what's normal: some property types and business models support more leverage than others, so compare a REIT's metrics to its sector peers and its own history. Liquidity (cash and undrawn credit lines), credit ratings, and access to capital also factor in — a REIT with strong liquidity and investment-grade access can weather stress that would strain a weaker-rated peer. So assessing balance-sheet risk means weighing leverage, coverage, maturities, rate mix, LTV, and liquidity together, in context, rather than fixating on any one figure.

Liquidity and Access to Capital
Leverage, coverage, maturities, and rate mix describe the debt a REIT already carries, but liquidity and access to capital describe its ability to handle what comes next — and they're often what separates a REIT that survives a stress period from one that doesn't. Liquidity is the cash and undrawn credit (typically a revolving credit facility) a REIT can tap immediately to meet maturities, fund commitments, or ride out a downturn without selling assets at the wrong time. Ample liquidity is a buffer that turns a potential crisis into a manageable inconvenience.
Access to capital is the broader, forward-looking version of the same idea: can the REIT raise debt and equity on reasonable terms when it needs to? A REIT with an investment-grade credit rating, a strong relationship with lenders, and the ability to issue equity at a fair price has many options for refinancing maturities or funding growth, even in tight markets. A REIT with weak liquidity, a low credit rating, and shares trading at a deep discount (making equity issuance dilutive) has far fewer — and may be forced into distressed asset sales or punitive financing. So liquidity and capital access are the shock absorbers of the balance sheet.
So liquidity and access to capital determine how well a REIT can absorb stress and refinance on good terms — the shock absorbers behind the leverage and coverage metrics. Frequently Asked Questions
What is debt-to-EBITDA for a REIT?
Debt-to-EBITDA is the most common measure of a REIT's leverage. It divides total debt by EBITDA — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization, which approximates the REIT's recurring operating cash flow before financing costs. The result expresses leverage as a multiple: roughly how many years of EBITDA it would take to repay the debt. A lower multiple means less leverage and more balance-sheet flexibility, while a higher multiple means the REIT carries more debt relative to its earnings power. Analysts often refine this to net debt-to-EBITDA, which subtracts the REIT's cash and equivalents from total debt first, recognizing that cash could be used to pay down debt — net debt-to-EBITDA is generally the preferred leverage gauge. What counts as conservative versus aggressive varies by sector and strategy, so read the multiple against sector norms and the REIT's own history rather than a universal cutoff. So debt-to-EBITDA expresses a REIT's borrowing as a multiple of its cash flow, and lower multiples generally signal a more conservatively financed REIT.
Why use net debt-to-EBITDA instead of total debt-to-EBITDA?
Net debt-to-EBITDA is generally preferred because it reflects a REIT's debt burden net of the cash it holds. Total debt-to-EBITDA divides all of a REIT's debt by its EBITDA, but it ignores the fact that the REIT may hold cash and equivalents that could be used to repay debt. Net debt-to-EBITDA subtracts that cash from total debt before dividing, giving a truer picture of the REIT's effective leverage — the debt that would remain if available cash were applied to it. For a REIT sitting on substantial liquidity, total debt-to-EBITDA can overstate leverage, while net debt-to-EBITDA captures the real position. That said, both are useful, and in stressed conditions you may care about gross debt too (cash can be spent or restricted). So net debt-to-EBITDA is the more refined leverage measure because it nets out liquidity, while total debt-to-EBITDA is a more conservative gross view. So look at net debt-to-EBITDA as the primary leverage gauge, and keep gross debt in mind as a backstop, comparing both to sector norms.
What is interest coverage for a REIT?
Interest coverage measures how comfortably a REIT's cash flow covers its interest expense. The ratio divides EBITDA by interest expense, showing how many times over the REIT's operating cash flow covers its interest bill. A higher interest coverage ratio means a thicker buffer — the REIT could absorb a meaningful drop in earnings, or a rise in interest costs, and still pay its interest. A low ratio means there's little margin between the cash the REIT generates and the interest it owes, so even a modest setback could leave it struggling to service its debt. Interest coverage is a core indicator of financial health: strong, stable coverage is a hallmark of a sound REIT, while weak or declining coverage is an early warning that the balance sheet is under strain. So interest coverage gauges a REIT's ability to pay its interest from cash flow, with higher being safer. So pair it with the leverage ratio: debt-to-EBITDA tells you how much a REIT owes, while interest coverage tells you how easily it can service that debt — both matter for balance-sheet risk.
What is fixed-charge coverage, and how does it differ from interest coverage?
Fixed-charge coverage is a stricter, more complete version of interest coverage. Interest coverage divides EBITDA by interest expense alone. Fixed-charge coverage divides EBITDA by the sum of interest expense plus preferred dividends (and sometimes other fixed obligations like certain lease payments). The reason this matters for REITs is that many issue preferred stock, whose dividends must be paid before any common dividend. Those preferred dividends are a real, recurring fixed obligation that interest coverage ignores — so a REIT could show comfortable interest coverage yet have thinner coverage once preferred dividends are included. Fixed-charge coverage captures the fuller set of fixed financing commitments, giving a more complete read on how well cash flow covers everything the REIT must pay before common shareholders. So fixed-charge coverage is the more comprehensive gauge, and it's especially important for REITs with significant preferred stock. So when assessing whether a REIT can service all its fixed obligations, weight fixed-charge coverage over interest coverage alone — a strong fixed-charge ratio is a better sign of balance-sheet resilience.
What is a debt maturity ladder?
A debt maturity ladder is the schedule showing when a REIT's various loans and bonds come due. A well-laddered profile spreads maturities relatively evenly across many years, so only a small portion of the REIT's total debt matures in any single year. This matters because it limits how much debt the REIT has to refinance at one time, reducing its exposure to any single bad moment in the credit markets. The opposite — a concentrated maturity wall, where a large slug of debt all comes due in the same year or two — is a risk: if those maturities arrive when rates are high or credit is tight, the REIT may be forced to refinance at much higher rates, issue equity at depressed prices, or sell assets under pressure. So a laddered maturity schedule with no large near-term concentrations signals prudent balance-sheet management, while a looming maturity wall is a clear vulnerability to scrutinize. So when assessing a REIT, look at not just how much it owes but when that debt comes due, and how evenly those maturities are spread over time.
Why does a debt maturity wall matter, especially in a high-rate environment?
A debt maturity wall — a large concentration of debt coming due in a short window — matters because it forces a REIT to refinance a big chunk of its borrowings at once, exposing it to whatever conditions prevail at that moment. In a high-rate environment, that's particularly dangerous: maturing debt that was issued cheaply must be replaced with new debt at today's higher rates, sharply raising the REIT's interest expense and squeezing its cash flow and coverage ratios. If credit is also tight, the REIT may struggle to refinance at all, and could be forced to issue equity at depressed prices (diluting shareholders) or sell properties under pressure (at unfavorable prices) to meet the maturities. By contrast, a REIT with laddered maturities refinances only a little at a time, so it's far less exposed to any single rate spike or credit crunch. So a maturity wall concentrates refinancing risk, and high rates magnify the cost of rolling that debt over. So scrutinize near-term maturities closely — a large wall in a high-rate setting is one of the clearer balance-sheet warning signs.
What's the difference between floating-rate and fixed-rate REIT debt?
The difference is whether the interest rate can change over the life of the debt. Fixed-rate debt locks in an interest rate for the term of the loan or bond, so the REIT's interest cost on that debt stays the same regardless of what happens to market rates — predictable and insulated from rate increases. Floating-rate (variable-rate) debt resets periodically with a benchmark rate, so its interest cost rises when market rates rise and falls when they fall. The composition matters for risk: a REIT with a high proportion of floating-rate debt is more exposed to rising rates, because as benchmark rates climb, its interest expense increases, squeezing cash flow, eroding coverage, and pressuring the dividend — even if its properties are performing fine. A REIT funded mostly with fixed-rate debt is insulated from that near-term rate risk. Some REITs also hedge floating exposure with swaps or caps to make it effectively fixed. So fixed-rate debt locks in costs while floating-rate debt moves with rates, making the fixed-versus-floating mix a key driver of a REIT's interest-rate sensitivity.
How does floating-rate debt increase a REIT's risk?
Floating-rate debt increases risk because its interest cost rises with market rates, directly pressuring a REIT's finances when rates climb. When a large share of a REIT's debt floats, an increase in benchmark rates flows straight through to higher interest expense. That higher expense reduces the cash flow available after debt service, which erodes coverage ratios (interest and fixed-charge coverage) and can squeeze the cushion behind the dividend — all without any change in the underlying properties, occupancy, or rents. In a sharply rising-rate environment, heavy floating-rate exposure can turn a comfortable balance sheet into a strained one quickly. A REIT funded predominantly with fixed-rate debt avoids this near-term shock because its interest costs are locked in, and a REIT that hedges its floating exposure (with interest-rate swaps or caps) can convert it to effectively fixed. So heavy floating-rate exposure amplifies a REIT's sensitivity to rising rates, while a mostly-fixed or hedged structure dampens it. So check the share of floating-rate debt and any hedges when judging a REIT's rate risk — it can materially change the risk picture.
What is loan-to-value (LTV) for a REIT?
Loan-to-value (LTV) measures a REIT's debt as a percentage of the value of its properties. If a REIT has debt equal to 40% of the estimated value of its real estate, its LTV is 40%. LTV is another useful leverage gauge alongside debt-to-EBITDA: a lower LTV means more equity cushion beneath the debt (so property values would have to fall further before the debt is underwater), while a higher LTV means thinner cushion and greater sensitivity to declining property values. LTV has one important caveat: it depends on property valuations, which are estimates that can move with markets and cap rates. In a downturn, falling property values can push LTV up even if the debt itself hasn't changed — so an LTV that looked comfortable can deteriorate. For that reason, LTV is best used alongside cash-flow-based measures like debt-to-EBITDA and coverage ratios, which don't depend as directly on appraised values. So LTV gauges leverage relative to property value, providing an equity-cushion view that complements the cash-flow-based leverage and coverage metrics.
How do I assess a REIT's overall balance-sheet risk?
You assess balance-sheet risk by reading the metrics together rather than relying on any single one. A lower-risk balance sheet generally combines moderate net debt-to-EBITDA (sensible leverage), strong and stable interest and fixed-charge coverage (ample ability to service obligations), a well-laddered maturity schedule with no large near-term concentrations (limited refinancing risk), and predominantly fixed-rate or hedged debt (limited rate exposure). A higher-risk profile shows the opposite — high leverage, thin or declining coverage, a looming maturity wall, and heavy floating-rate debt. Loan-to-value adds an equity-cushion view, and liquidity (cash plus undrawn credit lines), credit ratings, and access to capital determine how well the REIT can weather stress. Context matters: compare the metrics to sector peers and the REIT's own history, since some property types support more leverage than others. So combine leverage, coverage, maturities, rate mix, LTV, and liquidity, read in context, to form a judgment about resilience. So no single number defines balance-sheet risk — the picture emerges from how these metrics line up together.
What leverage level is safe for a REIT?
There's no universal 'safe' leverage level, because what's prudent depends on the sector, the stability of the cash flows, and the structure of the debt. A REIT with very stable, long-lease cash flows (such as net-lease) can typically support more leverage safely than a REIT with more volatile, shorter-lease income, because its cash flow is more predictable. Rather than a fixed cutoff, judge leverage relative to sector norms and the REIT's own history, and — crucially — in combination with the other metrics. The same net debt-to-EBITDA multiple is much safer when paired with strong fixed-charge coverage, a laddered maturity schedule, and mostly fixed-rate debt than when paired with thin coverage, a near-term maturity wall, and heavy floating-rate exposure. Liquidity and capital access also factor in. So 'safe' leverage isn't a single number but a level that the REIT's cash-flow stability, coverage, maturity profile, and rate mix can comfortably support. So evaluate leverage in context and alongside coverage and debt structure, not against a one-size-fits-all threshold — and compare to similar REITs in the same sector.
How do interest rates affect a REIT's balance sheet?
Interest rates affect a REIT's balance sheet through several channels. Most directly, rising rates increase the cost of any floating-rate debt immediately, and they raise the cost of refinancing fixed-rate debt as it matures — so a REIT with heavy floating exposure or a near-term maturity wall feels higher rates quickly through rising interest expense, which squeezes cash flow and erodes coverage ratios. Higher rates can also push cap rates up, lowering property values and thereby increasing loan-to-value even if the debt is unchanged. And higher rates raise the bar for new investments, since borrowing costs more. A REIT insulates itself against these effects with a conservative structure: mostly fixed-rate (or hedged) debt, a well-laddered maturity schedule that avoids concentrated near-term refinancing, lower leverage, and strong coverage that can absorb higher costs. So interest rates pressure a REIT's balance sheet through floating-rate costs, refinancing costs, and property values — and a sturdy debt structure cushions those effects. So the fixed-versus-floating mix and the maturity ladder are central to how rate-resilient a REIT's balance sheet is.
Do credit ratings matter for REITs?
Yes — credit ratings matter for REITs because they affect both the cost and availability of debt, and they offer a third-party read on balance-sheet risk. Rating agencies evaluate a REIT's leverage, coverage, asset quality, liquidity, and access to capital, then assign a rating. An investment-grade rating generally lets a REIT borrow more cheaply and access the unsecured bond market, giving it more financing flexibility — including the ability to refinance maturing debt on better terms and to weather stress. A lower or non-investment-grade rating typically means higher borrowing costs and more reliance on secured or private debt, which can be a disadvantage, especially when credit tightens. So while a credit rating is just one input (and ratings can lag events), it provides a useful external assessment of a REIT's creditworthiness and influences the very refinancing risk you're trying to gauge. So consider a REIT's credit rating and access to capital alongside the quantitative metrics — leverage, coverage, maturities, and rate mix — when judging its balance-sheet strength and its ability to manage debt through a cycle.
Can a REIT with low leverage still be risky?
Yes — low leverage reduces balance-sheet risk but doesn't eliminate other risks. A REIT can carry modest debt yet still face meaningful challenges: its properties or sector could underperform (declining occupancy, falling rents, structural headwinds), its dividend could be stretched relative to AFFO, or its growth prospects could be weak. Even on the balance sheet itself, low headline leverage can mask issues like a near-term maturity wall, heavy floating-rate exposure, thin coverage relative to obligations, or weak liquidity — so leverage alone doesn't tell the whole story. Conversely, a moderately leveraged REIT with strong coverage, laddered fixed-rate debt, ample liquidity, and excellent assets can be quite sound. So low leverage is a favorable factor but only one piece of the picture; you still have to assess coverage, maturities, rate mix, liquidity, and — beyond the balance sheet — the quality of the real estate, the dividend's sustainability, and the sector. So don't equate low leverage with low overall risk. So evaluate the full balance sheet and the underlying business together, because a single favorable metric can coexist with other vulnerabilities.
