Oil & Gas DSTs: Royalty-Pool Replacement Property for Your 1031
Back to All Insights
Mineral & Royalty Gerald F. "Jerry" Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read
A royalty-pool DST turns hard-to-source minerals into turnkey, fast-closing 1031 replacement property — diversified across wells, operators, and basins, professionally managed, and structured to qualify under Section 1031. Here's how the structure works, how it's taxed, and where it fits.
One of the hardest parts of a mineral or royalty 1031 is the replacement side: finding suitable mineral interests to buy, vetting them, and closing inside 45 and 180 days is genuinely difficult, because the market is fragmented, opaque, and slow. The oil and gas DST — a royalty-pool DST — is the structure built to solve that problem. Instead of hunting for individual mineral interests, an exchanger places proceeds into a single, professionally assembled trust that holds a diversified pool of royalty interests across many wells, operators, and basins. It's turnkey, it closes fast, and it's structured to qualify as like-kind real property. For a royalty owner who wants to stay in minerals but escape the difficulty and concentration of direct ownership, the royalty-pool DST is often the cleanest replacement available. This guide explains how it satisfies Section 1031, how it diversifies risk, how the income and depletion are reported, and how it compares to owning minerals directly.
If you're new to exchanging minerals, our comprehensive mineral rights and royalty 1031 guide covers the fundamentals this replacement strategy builds on.
What is an oil & gas / royalty DST?
A Delaware Statutory Trust is a legal entity that holds title to property and sells fractional beneficial interests to investors. Most DSTs hold institutional real estate — apartments, industrial, retail — but a royalty-pool DST holds a portfolio of oil and gas royalty interests instead. A sponsor assembles a diversified set of mineral and royalty interests into the trust, and accredited investor s buy fractional interests, each owning a slice of the entire pool's income.
The appeal is that it packages a notoriously hard-to-access asset class into a turnkey, institutional form. Sourcing, vetting, and aggregating individual royalty interests is the sponsor's job, done at scale with professional reserve evaluation and negotiation. The investor simply buys into the finished pool, gaining exposure to many royalty streams at once without the time, expertise, or capital required to assemble such a portfolio independently. For a 1031 exchanger facing a 45-day clock, that turnkey quality is invaluable.
Like all DSTs, a royalty-pool DST is a passive, securitized investment. The investor has no management role and no operating obligations — they receive distributions from the pool's royalty income and rely on the sponsor's management. Because it's a security, it's offered through a broker-dealer under a private placement memorandum to accredited investors, and any purchase follows a suitability review. The structure trades the control of direct ownership for diversification, passivity, and ease of execution.
How royalty-pool DSTs satisfy 1031
The DST structure's eligibility for 1031 rests on Revenue Ruling 2004-86, in which the IRS held that a beneficial interest in a properly structured DST is treated as a direct interest in the trust's underlying real property for tax purposes — making it like-kind to other real property and therefore valid 1031 replacement property. For a real estate DST, the underlying property is buildings and land; for a royalty-pool DST, it's the mineral and royalty interests, which are themselves real property under state law and longstanding IRS rulings and tax-court authority .
So the eligibility chain is: the underlying royalty interests are real property; the DST is structured so that each investor is treated as owning a direct undivided interest in that real property; and a direct interest in real property is like-kind to the relinquished mineral, royalty, or other real-property interest being exchanged. The result is that an exchanger can roll proceeds from selling a mineral or royalty interest — or even conventional real estate — into a royalty-pool DST and defer the gain, just as they could into a real estate DST.
Because the structure must satisfy the specific requirements of Revenue Ruling 2004-86 (the 'seven deadly sins' that limit the trustee's powers, for example), the sponsor bears responsibility for keeping the DST compliant, and the investor relies on that compliance. This is one reason sponsor quality and the offering documents matter so much: a properly structured royalty-pool DST delivers clean 1031 treatment, while a poorly structured one could jeopardize it. Diligence on the sponsor and a review of the PPM, ideally with your tax adviser, confirm the eligibility you're counting on.
Under Rev. Rul. 2004-86, a DST interest is treated as direct ownership of the underlying real property — and royalty interests are real property, so the pool qualifies as 1031 replacement.
Diversification across wells, operators, and basins
The central investment advantage of a royalty-pool DST is diversification. Direct ownership of a single mineral interest concentrates an investor's fortunes in one tract, often one operator, and one geology — if that well declines faster than expected, that operator underperforms, or that basin's economics sour, the income suffers with no offset. A royalty-pool DST spreads capital across many interests, diversifying away much of that single-asset risk in a way an individual investor would struggle to replicate.
Good royalty-pool DSTs diversify along several axes at once: across many individual wells (so no single well's decline dominates), across multiple operators (so no single operator's performance is decisive), and across different basins and geologies (so regional and play-specific risks are blended). Some also diversify across commodity mix (oil-weighted versus gas-weighted) and across the maturity of the wells. The result is a smoother, more resilient royalty income stream than a concentrated direct interest typically provides.
This diversification is especially valuable for an exchanger consolidating out of a concentrated position — say, a single inherited royalty or one large interest. Rather than trade one concentrated mineral asset for another directly held replacement (with all the sourcing difficulty that entails), they can exchange into a professionally diversified pool, immediately reducing concentration risk while staying within the minerals asset class. For owners who like mineral income but have learned the hazards of putting it all in one well or basin, the pool is a structural solution to a problem direct ownership can't easily fix.
Income, depletion, and tax reporting
A royalty-pool DST distributes the net royalty income from the underlying interests to investors, typically on a monthly or quarterly basis, in proportion to their fractional ownership. Because investors are treated as owning direct interests in the underlying real property, the income retains its character as royalty income, and the favorable tax features of mineral ownership generally flow through to the investor rather than being lost in a corporate wrapper.
Most importantly, depletion typically passes through. Royalty owners benefit from depletion deductions — frequently percentage depletion at 15% of gross royalty income for qualifying owners — which shelter a portion of the income from tax. In a properly structured royalty-pool DST, investors generally receive their share of depletion, so a meaningful part of their distributions can be tax-advantaged. This pass-through of depletion is a key reason royalty-pool DSTs appeal to income-focused investors and is a feature real estate DSTs (which pass through depreciation instead) handle analogously.
Reporting is handled through the documentation the sponsor provides — generally a grantor-trust style reporting package that tells each investor their share of income, depletion, and other items to report on their return. As with any oil and gas investment, the interaction with the net investment income tax , state taxes in producing states, and the investor's overall situation should be reviewed with a CPA. The headline, though, is that the royalty character and depletion benefits of direct mineral ownership are largely preserved inside the DST, which is much of its appeal as a replacement for directly held minerals.
Fast closing and the ideal backup
Beyond diversification and tax features, the royalty-pool DST's operational advantage is speed. Because the trust is already assembled and the interests are already acquired, an exchanger can typically close into a DST in days rather than the weeks or months it can take to source, negotiate, and close a direct mineral acquisition. In a process governed by a hard 45-day identification deadline and a 180-day closing deadline, that speed is not a minor convenience — it can be the difference between completing the exchange and failing it.
This makes the royalty-pool DST ideal in two roles. As a primary replacement, it lets an exchanger deploy proceeds quickly and confidently into a diversified, qualifying asset. As a backup, it's the safety net that rescues an exchange when a direct deal stalls: an exchanger can identify a fast-closing DST alongside their primary target under the 3-property rule, and pivot to it if the primary collapses after day 45, when nothing new can be added. For oil and gas exchangers — who face thinner, slower markets than real estate buyers — this backup function is close to essential.
The combination of turnkey assembly, fast closing, and diversification is why royalty-pool DSTs have become a workhorse of mineral and royalty exchanges. They convert the hardest part of the exchange — finding and closing suitable replacement minerals in 45 to 180 days — into a straightforward purchase of a finished, diversified, qualifying product. For many royalty owners, the existence of this structure is what makes a tax-deferred exit from a concentrated mineral position practical at all.
Royalty DST vs. direct mineral ownership
Choosing between a royalty-pool DST and direct mineral ownership is a trade-off between control and convenience. Direct ownership gives you full control — you choose the interests, negotiate the deals, and capture all the upside (and downside) of specific wells. It has no sponsor fees or load, and you can hold indefinitely and manage actively. The costs are the sourcing difficulty, the concentration risk, the time and expertise required, and the slow, uncertain closings that fit poorly with exchange deadlines.
A royalty-pool DST inverts that profile. You give up control and pay the sponsor's fees and load, and you accept a passive, illiquid, multi-year holding governed by the offering documents. In exchange, you get instant diversification, professional management, depletion pass-through, fast and certain closing, and freedom from the sourcing burden. For an exchanger under a deadline, or an owner who wants mineral income without the work and concentration of direct ownership, that's often a favorable trade.
Neither is universally better; the right choice depends on the investor. An experienced, hands-on mineral investor with deal flow may prefer direct ownership and its control and cost savings. A retiree, an heir who inherited minerals, or anyone exchanging out of a concentrated position under time pressure often finds the DST's diversification, passivity, and speed decisive. Many investors even blend the two — keeping some direct interests while placing exchange proceeds into a pool as part of a diversified DST portfolio spread across asset classes for diversification and ease. Because the DST is a security with fees and risks, the decision should be made with an advisor and a clear-eyed read of the offering documents, weighing the convenience against the cost and loss of control.
Key Takeaways
A royalty-pool DST holds a diversified portfolio of mineral royalty interests as turnkey 1031 replacement property.
It qualifies under Rev. Rul. 2004-86: a DST interest is treated as direct ownership of the underlying real property.
Depletion and royalty character generally pass through, preserving the tax features of direct mineral ownership.
Fast closing makes it an ideal primary or backup replacement; the trade-off is fees, passivity, and loss of control.
Fees, risks, and what to scrutinize
Like any securitized product, a royalty-pool DST carries fees and a load — the combination of selling costs, sponsor acquisition and management fees, and reserves that consume part of your investment before money reaches the underlying royalties. A portion of every invested dollar goes to assembling and running the pool rather than into producing minerals, and that load directly affects your net return. Reading the offering's fee disclosure in the private placement memorandum, and comparing it to alternatives, is essential diligence; a high load can meaningfully erode the income advantage of the underlying interests.
The risks are those of the underlying minerals plus the structure. Royalty income declines as wells deplete and swings with commodity prices, so distributions are not guaranteed and can fall — diversification smooths this but doesn't eliminate it. The investment is illiquid and typically multi-year, with no public market to sell into, so you should expect to hold for the duration. And because the structure is passive, you depend entirely on the sponsor's competence in selecting interests, managing the pool, and reporting accurately. Sponsor risk is real and is why track record matters so much.
What to scrutinize, then, is a short list: the sponsor's history and full-cycle results, the genuine diversification of the pool (not just a count of wells but real spread across operators, basins, and maturities), the fee load relative to peers, the conservativeness of the income projections and reserve assumptions, and the clarity of the depletion and income reporting. Review the PPM's risk factors in full, ideally with your tax adviser, and confirm the 1031 structure is sound. A well-run royalty-pool DST from a reputable sponsor can be an excellent replacement; a high-fee pool of marginal interests from an unproven sponsor is a different proposition entirely, and the diligence is what tells them apart.

Frequently Asked Questions
What is an oil and gas DST?
A Delaware Statutory Trust that holds a diversified pool of oil and gas royalty interests and sells fractional beneficial interests to accredited investors. It packages hard-to-source minerals into a turnkey, professionally managed, 1031-eligible form, letting an exchanger gain exposure to many royalty streams at once without assembling them individually.
How does a royalty-pool DST qualify for a 1031 exchange?
Under Revenue Ruling 2004-86, a beneficial interest in a properly structured DST is treated as a direct interest in the trust's underlying real property. The underlying royalty interests are real property, so a DST interest in them is like-kind to other real property — making the pool valid 1031 replacement property for a mineral, royalty, or real estate exchange.
Can I exchange my minerals into a royalty-pool DST?
Yes. Proceeds from selling a qualifying mineral or royalty interest can be exchanged into a royalty-pool DST, deferring the gain. It's a popular way to consolidate out of a concentrated direct interest into a diversified, professionally managed pool while staying in the minerals asset class — and to close quickly within the exchange deadlines.
How does a royalty-pool DST diversify risk?
By spreading capital across many individual wells, multiple operators, and different basins and geologies — sometimes also across commodity mix and well maturity. This blends away much of the single-asset risk of direct ownership, producing a smoother, more resilient royalty income stream than one concentrated interest typically provides.
Does depletion pass through a royalty DST?
Generally yes. Because investors are treated as owning direct interests in the underlying real property, depletion deductions — often percentage depletion at 15% of gross royalty income for qualifying owners — typically pass through, sheltering part of the distributions. This preservation of depletion is a key reason royalty-pool DSTs appeal to income-focused investors.
How is income from a royalty DST taxed?
The DST distributes net royalty income to investors in proportion to their interests, and the income generally retains its royalty character with depletion passing through. Reporting is handled via the sponsor's grantor-trust style package. Interactions with the net investment income tax and state taxes in producing states should be reviewed with your CPA.
How fast can a royalty-pool DST close?
Often within days, because the trust is already assembled and the interests already acquired. That speed is a major advantage under the 45-day identification and 180-day closing deadlines, and it's why royalty-pool DSTs work well both as a primary replacement and as a fast-closing backup if a direct deal stalls.
Why use a royalty DST as a backup?
Because sourcing and closing direct mineral interests is slow and uncertain, and after day 45 you can't identify new property. Identifying a fast-closing royalty-pool DST alongside your primary target under the 3-property rule lets you pivot to it if the primary collapses, completing the exchange. For oil and gas exchangers facing thin markets, this backup is close to essential.
What are the downsides of a royalty-pool DST?
You give up control over the specific interests, pay the sponsor's fees and load, and accept a passive, illiquid, multi-year holding governed by the offering documents. Returns depend on the sponsor's management and the underlying minerals. These trade-offs are the price of the diversification, passivity, and fast closing the structure provides.
How does a royalty DST compare to owning minerals directly?
Direct ownership offers full control, no fees, and indefinite holding, but with sourcing difficulty, concentration risk, and slow closings. A royalty-pool DST offers diversification, professional management, depletion pass-through, and fast closing, at the cost of fees, passivity, and loss of control. The right choice depends on your expertise, goals, and whether you're under exchange deadlines.
Who can invest in a royalty-pool DST?
Accredited investors, since DSTs are securities offered under a private placement memorandum through a broker-dealer. Accreditation generally means meeting SEC income or net-worth thresholds. Any purchase follows a suitability review confirming the investment fits your financial situation and objectives.
How do I evaluate a royalty-pool DST?
Assess the sponsor's track record and full-cycle results, the diversification of the underlying pool (wells, operators, basins), the fee load, the depletion and income reporting, and the underlying reserve quality. Review the PPM's risk factors, ideally with your tax adviser, and confirm the structure delivers the 1031 treatment you're relying on.
What fees does a royalty-pool DST charge?
A load made up of selling costs, sponsor acquisition and management fees, and reserves, disclosed in the private placement memorandum. A portion of each invested dollar goes to assembling and running the pool before money reaches the royalties, which affects your net return. Compare the load to alternatives — a high load can erode the income advantage of the underlying interests.
Are royalty-pool DST distributions guaranteed?
No. Distributions come from the underlying royalty income, which declines as wells deplete and fluctuates with commodity prices. Diversification across many wells and basins smooths the income but doesn't guarantee it. Like any investment in producing minerals, returns are variable and depend on production, prices, and the sponsor's management.
How liquid is a royalty-pool DST?
Not very. DST interests are illiquid, typically multi-year holdings with no public market to sell into, so you should plan to hold for the offering's duration. This illiquidity is a key trade-off for the diversification and passivity the structure provides, and it's why suitability review considers whether you can commit the capital for the full term.
What is sponsor risk in a royalty-pool DST?
Because the structure is passive, you depend entirely on the sponsor to select good interests, manage the pool competently, and report accurately. A weak or inexperienced sponsor can underperform or mishandle the structure, including the 1031 compliance. This is why the sponsor's track record and full-cycle results are among the most important things to scrutinize before investing.
Can a royalty-pool DST go full-cycle like a real estate DST?
Yes — at some point the sponsor may sell the underlying interests and return capital and gain to investors, completing the cycle. At full cycle, investors typically face a taxable event unless they do another 1031, or in some structures may have other options. The expected hold and exit strategy are described in the offering documents and should factor into your decision.
Should I put my whole exchange into one royalty-pool DST?
Not necessarily. Many investors diversify across multiple DSTs or blend a royalty pool with real estate DSTs or direct property, rather than concentrating in a single offering. Spreading proceeds reduces reliance on one sponsor and one pool. An advisor can help you structure a diversified set of replacements that fits your goals and the identification rules.
Glossary
Oil & Gas DST / Royalty-Pool DST A Delaware Statutory Trust holding a diversified pool of mineral royalty interests as 1031 replacement property.
Delaware Statutory Trust (DST) A legal entity holding title to property and selling fractional beneficial interests to investors.
Revenue Ruling 2004-86 The IRS ruling treating a DST beneficial interest as direct ownership of the underlying real property for 1031.
Beneficial Interest An investor's fractional ownership share of a DST and its underlying assets.
Royalty Interest A cost-free right to a share of production; real property underlying a royalty-pool DST.
Percentage Depletion A deduction (commonly 15% of gross royalty income) that shelters part of royalty income and passes through a DST.
Sponsor The firm that assembles, structures, and manages the DST and its underlying interests.
Private Placement Memorandum (PPM) The disclosure document governing a DST offering, including risks and fees.
Load The total of selling costs, sponsor fees, and reserves consumed before invested dollars reach the assets.
Accredited Investor An investor meeting SEC income or net-worth thresholds, eligible to buy private placements like DSTs.
Grantor Trust Reporting The reporting style that passes the DST's income, depletion, and other items through to investors.
Diversification Spreading capital across many wells, operators, and basins to reduce single-asset risk.
Suitability Review The assessment that a securities product like a DST is appropriate for a particular investor.
Full-Cycle A DST that has completed acquisition through disposition, providing realized returns.
Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) A 3.8% tax on investment income for higher-income taxpayers, potentially applying to DST income.
3-Property Rule An identification method allowing up to three replacement properties of any value, useful for naming a DST backup.
