1031 Exchange Fraud & Qualified Intermediary Safety
Back to All Insights
1031 Exchange Gerald F. "Jerry" Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read
The qualified intermediary holds your exchange proceeds — sometimes a large sum — yet QIs aren't federally regulated like banks, and there have been notable failures and frauds. This guide explains the risk, how QI fraud happens, how to vet a QI's fund security, the red flags to watch, and how to protect your funds.
A qualified intermediary is essential to a 1031 exchange — it holds your sale proceeds between the relinquished and replacement closings, preventing you from constructively receiving the funds (which would disqualify the exchange). But this means the QI is holding your money, sometimes a substantial sum, for up to 180 days. And here's the sobering reality: qualified intermediaries aren't federally regulated or insured the way banks are. There's no federal licensing requirement, no FDIC insurance on the funds, and limited oversight in most states. This has led to notable cases where QIs failed or misappropriated client funds, leaving investors with losses. So while the QI is necessary, choosing a financially sound, secure QI is one of the most important risk-management decisions in an exchange. This guide explains the QI safety risk, how fraud happens, vetting a QI's fund security, red flags, and protecting your funds.
The risk: QIs aren't regulated like banks
The fundamental QI safety risk stems from a regulatory gap: qualified intermediaries aren't regulated like banks or other financial institutions. There's no federal licensing requirement to be a QI, no federal oversight of how QIs hold client funds, and no FDIC insurance protecting the funds the way bank deposits are insured. Most states have little or no specific regulation of QIs either (though a few have enacted some requirements). This means a QI holding your funds operates with far less oversight than a bank.
The practical implication is that your exchange funds, while held by the QI, aren't protected by the safeguards you'd expect for money at a bank. If the QI fails financially, misappropriates the funds, or makes risky investments that lose the money, you could lose your exchange proceeds — with limited recourse. Because the funds can be substantial (the full proceeds from a property sale) and held for up to 180 days, the exposure is significant. This is the risk that makes QI selection critical.
This regulatory gap doesn't mean QIs are inherently unsafe — many are sound, secure, and have excellent track records. But it means the responsibility falls on you (and your advisors) to vet the QI's safety, since the regulatory framework won't do it for you. A reputable, financially strong QI with proper fund-security measures is safe; a weak or fraudulent one is a real risk. The risk — that QIs aren't regulated or insured like banks, so your funds depend on the QI's soundness and integrity — is the reason QI safety deserves careful attention. Understanding this regulatory gap motivates the vetting and protection steps that follow. The QI is necessary, but choosing a secure one is your responsibility, because the regulatory safeguards you'd expect for your money simply aren't there.
Notable QI failures and frauds
The QI safety risk isn't theoretical — there have been notable cases of QI failures and frauds that cost investors. In the most significant historical cases, QIs misappropriated or lost client exchange funds, leaving investors unable to complete their exchanges and facing losses of their proceeds. Some failures occurred when QIs invested client funds in risky or illiquid assets that lost value (especially around financial downturns), and others involved outright fraud where principals stole client money.
These cases share common themes: the QI held client funds with inadequate safeguards (commingled accounts, risky investments, or no protection against misappropriation), and when the QI failed or the principals absconded, the clients' funds were gone. Because the funds weren't insured or protected, the investors had limited recourse — sometimes recovering only pennies on the dollar through bankruptcy or litigation, if anything. The losses could be devastating, as the funds represented the proceeds of a property sale.
These notable failures led to some increased awareness and, in a few states, regulation — but the fundamental gap remains, and the cases serve as cautionary tales. They demonstrate that QI risk is real and that the consequences of choosing an unsafe QI can be severe. The lesson is not to avoid QIs (they're necessary) but to vet them carefully for financial soundness and fund security. How QI fraud happens
Understanding how QI fraud and failures happen helps you guard against them. The most direct is misappropriation or theft — a QI (or its principals) with access to commingled client funds diverts or steals the money. When client funds are held in a commingled account that the QI controls without safeguards (like dual authorization or a qualified trust/escrow), a dishonest QI can access and misuse them. The lack of segregation and controls enables the theft.
Another mechanism is risky investment of client funds. Some QIs, seeking to earn returns on the float (the funds they hold), invested client money in risky or illiquid assets. When those investments lost value or became illiquid (especially in a downturn), the QI couldn't return the clients' funds. The funds were lost not to theft but to bad investment, but the result for the client was the same — money unavailable to complete the exchange. Holding client funds in risky investments rather than safe, liquid accounts creates this exposure.
Financial failure of the QI is a third path. If the QI is financially weak and commingles client funds with its own operating funds, the QI's insolvency can sweep up the client funds (treated as part of the QI's assets in bankruptcy). Without segregation, the clients become unsecured creditors of a failed company. So commingling, risky investment, and weak financials are the conditions that enable QI fraud and failure. How QI fraud happens — misappropriation from commingled accounts, risky investment of client funds, and financial failure sweeping up commingled funds — reveals the conditions to guard against: commingling, lack of controls, risky fund handling, and weak financials. Understanding these mechanisms points directly to the protections (segregation, controls, sound financials) that prevent them, which is the practical value of understanding how fraud occurs.
Vetting a QI's fund security
Vetting a QI's fund security is the core protection against the risks above. The key things to verify are how the QI holds and protects client funds. Look for segregated accounts — the QI should hold client funds in separate, segregated accounts (ideally qualified escrow or qualified trust accounts), not commingled with the QI's operating funds or other clients' funds. Segregation protects your funds from the QI's insolvency and from misuse.
Also verify the QI's financial protections: a fidelity bond (insuring against employee theft/misappropriation), errors-and-omissions insurance, and adequate financial strength (a sound balance sheet, ideally backed by a well-capitalized parent company). Some of the safest QIs are subsidiaries of large financial institutions or title companies with strong financials and robust controls. Ask about the bonding amount, the insurance coverage, and the QI's financial backing — a secure QI will readily provide this information.
Additional security measures to look for include dual authorization for fund disbursements (requiring two parties to release funds, preventing unilateral misappropriation), qualified escrow or trust arrangements (giving the funds legal protection), and a clean track record (no history of failures or complaints). The combination of segregated accounts, bonding, insurance, financial strength, and disbursement controls indicates a secure QI. Vetting a QI's fund security — verifying segregated accounts, bonding, insurance, financial strength, and disbursement controls — is the essential due diligence that protects your funds. A QI with these protections is safe; one without them is a risk. Doing this vetting (with your advisors' help) before entrusting your funds to a QI is the single most important step in managing QI risk. The fund-security vetting is how you ensure your exchange proceeds are protected.
Red flags to watch
Certain red flags signal a potentially unsafe QI and warrant caution. A major red flag is commingling — if the QI doesn't hold client funds in segregated accounts but instead pools them with operating or other funds, that's a serious warning sign. Another is a lack of bonding or insurance — a QI that can't demonstrate fidelity bonding and E&O coverage lacks basic protections. Reluctance or inability to provide information about fund security, financials, or protections is itself a red flag.
Other warning signs include promises of above-market interest on your funds (suggesting the QI may invest them riskily to generate that return), weak or opaque financials (an inability to demonstrate financial strength or backing), a lack of dual-authorization controls on disbursements, and any history of complaints, failures, or litigation. A QI that's a thinly-capitalized standalone operation, without the backing or controls of a strong institution, carries more risk than a well-capitalized, institutionally-backed QI.
Pressure tactics or unusual fund-handling requests are also red flags — a legitimate QI follows standard, secure practices, while requests to handle funds unusually (or pressure to use a particular QI without vetting) should raise concern. The presence of red flags doesn't always mean fraud, but it signals the need for deeper scrutiny or choosing a different QI. Protecting yourself
Beyond vetting the QI, there are steps to actively protect your funds during the exchange. The strongest is requesting a qualified escrow account or qualified trust account for your funds — these arrangements (permitted under the regulations) hold your funds with legal protections, segregated and often with a third-party escrow agent or trustee, reducing the risk of misappropriation or loss to the QI's insolvency. Asking for a qualified escrow or trust gives your funds added legal protection.
Dual authorization is another protection — requesting that fund disbursements require your authorization (or a two-party control) prevents the QI from unilaterally moving your funds, guarding against misappropriation. Some QIs offer accounts where the client's signature is required for disbursements, adding a layer of control. Holding the funds in a sound, well-known financial institution (rather than the QI's own accounts) also reduces risk.
Choosing a strong, institutionally-backed QI is itself a key protection — a QI that's a subsidiary of a large bank or title company, with strong financials, robust controls, and a clean track record, is inherently safer than a weak standalone operation. Combining a strong QI with protective arrangements (qualified escrow/trust, dual authorization) maximizes your funds' safety. Protecting yourself — requesting qualified escrow/trust accounts, dual authorization, sound fund custody, and choosing a strong, institutionally-backed QI — is how you actively safeguard your exchange proceeds beyond basic vetting. These protections, combined with the fund-security vetting, give you strong assurance that your funds are safe. Taking these steps is prudent given the substantial sums and the regulatory gap, ensuring your exchange proceeds are protected throughout the exchange. Protecting yourself is the active complement to vetting, together securing your funds.

Frequently Asked Questions
Are qualified intermediaries regulated?
Not the way banks are — there's no federal licensing requirement, no federal oversight of how QIs hold client funds, and no FDIC insurance on the funds. Most states have little or no specific QI regulation (a few have enacted some requirements). This regulatory gap means your funds' safety depends on the QI's soundness and integrity, not on regulatory safeguards. So vetting the QI's fund security is your responsibility, since the oversight you'd expect for your money isn't there.
Is my exchange money insured?
Not by the FDIC the way bank deposits are — exchange funds held by a QI aren't federally insured. A secure QI protects your funds through segregated accounts (qualified escrow/trust), fidelity bonding (insuring against employee theft), E&O insurance, and financial strength — but these are the QI's protections, not federal insurance. So your funds' safety depends on the QI's specific protections, which you must vet. The absence of federal insurance is why choosing a secure QI matters so much.
Has QI fraud actually happened?
Yes — there have been notable cases where QIs misappropriated or lost client exchange funds, leaving investors with severe losses (sometimes recovering only pennies on the dollar). Some failures involved risky investment of client funds that lost value (especially in downturns); others involved outright theft. Because the funds weren't insured, the investors had limited recourse. These cautionary cases demonstrate that QI risk is real and consequential, underscoring the need to vet QIs carefully.
How does QI fraud happen?
Through misappropriation or theft from commingled accounts (a dishonest QI diverts client funds), risky investment of client funds (the QI invests the float in risky assets that lose value), and financial failure (a weak QI's insolvency sweeps up commingled client funds). The common enabling conditions are commingling, lack of controls, risky fund handling, and weak financials. Guarding against these — through segregation, controls, safe custody, and sound financials — prevents the fraud.
How do I vet a QI's fund security?
Verify segregated accounts (qualified escrow or trust, not commingled), fidelity bonding (against theft), E&O insurance, financial strength (sound balance sheet, ideally institutional backing), and dual-authorization controls on disbursements. Ask about the bonding amount, insurance coverage, and financial backing — a secure QI provides this readily. The combination of segregation, bonding, insurance, financial strength, and controls indicates a secure QI. This vetting is the essential due diligence before entrusting your funds.
What are the red flags of an unsafe QI?
Commingling (no segregated accounts), lack of bonding or insurance, promises of above-market interest on your funds (suggesting risky investment), weak or opaque financials, missing dual-authorization controls, reluctance to provide information, a thinly-capitalized standalone operation, and any history of complaints, failures, or litigation. Pressure tactics or unusual fund-handling requests are also warning signs. Red flags signal the need for deeper scrutiny or choosing a different, demonstrably secure QI.
What is a qualified escrow or trust account?
A fund-holding arrangement (permitted under the regulations) that holds your exchange funds with legal protections — segregated and often with a third-party escrow agent or trustee — reducing the risk of misappropriation or loss to the QI's insolvency. Requesting a qualified escrow or trust account gives your funds added legal protection compared to funds held in the QI's own accounts. It's one of the strongest steps you can take to protect your exchange proceeds during the exchange.
How can I protect my funds during an exchange?
Request a qualified escrow or trust account (added legal protection), ask for dual authorization on disbursements (preventing unilateral fund movement), ensure the funds are held in a sound financial institution, and choose a strong, institutionally-backed QI with robust controls and a clean track record. Combining a secure QI with protective arrangements maximizes your funds' safety. Given the substantial sums and the regulatory gap, these protections are prudent for safeguarding your exchange proceeds.
Is a bigger QI safer?
Often — a QI that's a subsidiary of a large bank or title company, with strong financials, robust controls, and institutional oversight, is generally safer than a thinly-capitalized standalone operation. The institutional backing provides financial strength and controls that reduce risk. That said, verify the specific protections (segregation, bonding, insurance, controls) regardless of size — a strong, well-controlled QI is safe whether large or smaller, but institutional backing is a positive indicator of safety.
What happens to my funds if my QI fails?
It depends on how the funds were held. If they were properly segregated in a qualified escrow or trust account, they should be protected from the QI's insolvency and returned to you. If they were commingled with the QI's funds, you could become an unsecured creditor and lose some or all of the funds. This is exactly why segregation and protective arrangements matter — they determine whether your funds survive a QI failure. Proper fund security protects you if the QI fails.
Should I just avoid using a QI?
No — a QI is required for a standard deferred exchange (to prevent constructive receipt of the funds, which would disqualify the exchange). You can't safely hold the funds yourself. So the answer isn't avoiding QIs but choosing a secure one and using protective arrangements. The QI is necessary; the goal is to make its use safe through careful selection (segregation, bonding, insurance, financial strength, controls) and protections (qualified escrow/trust, dual authorization). A secure QI makes the necessary use of one safe.

Does a few states' QI regulation make those QIs safer?
A handful of states have enacted some QI requirements (such as bonding, insurance, or segregation rules), which can add a layer of protection in those states — but the requirements vary, and most states have little or no regulation. So state regulation, where it exists, is a modest positive, but you should still independently vet the QI's fund security rather than relying on regulation alone. Even a QI in a regulated state should demonstrate segregated accounts, bonding, insurance, and controls. Verify the specifics regardless of state.
How much interest should I expect on my exchange funds?
Modest, market-rate interest consistent with safe, liquid holding (e.g., money-market-type rates) is normal — a secure QI holds your funds in safe, liquid accounts, which earn limited interest. Be wary of a QI promising above-market interest, since that can signal the QI is investing your funds in riskier assets to generate the return, increasing the risk of loss. Prioritize the safety of your funds over the interest earned; a secure QI's modest, safe interest is preferable to a risky QI's higher promised return.
Glossary
Qualified Intermediary (QI) The party that holds exchange funds; not federally regulated or insured like a bank.
Fund Security The QI's measures protecting client funds — segregation, bonding, insurance, controls.
Segregated Account A separate account holding client funds apart from the QI's operating funds.
Commingling Pooling client funds with operating or other funds — a major red flag.
Qualified Escrow Account A protected fund-holding arrangement giving funds added legal protection.
Qualified Trust Account A trust arrangement holding exchange funds with legal protection.
Fidelity Bond Insurance protecting against employee theft or misappropriation of funds.
Errors-and-Omissions Insurance Coverage protecting against the QI's professional errors.
Dual Authorization A control requiring two parties to release funds, preventing unilateral misuse.
Constructive Receipt Control over funds that disqualifies the exchange, prevented by the QI.
Misappropriation The diversion or theft of client funds, a QI fraud mechanism.
Float The client funds a QI holds, sometimes risky if invested for returns.
Unsecured Creditor What a client becomes if commingled funds are caught in a QI's insolvency.
Institutional Backing A QI's support by a large bank or title company, indicating financial strength.
Red Flag A warning sign of an unsafe QI, like commingling or missing bonding.
Track Record A QI's history; a clean one (no failures or complaints) indicates safety.
