Why REITs Cut Dividends
Back to All Insights
REIT Gerald F. "Jerry" Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read
REIT dividends aren't guaranteed — they can be cut when cash flow can't sustain them. This guide explains why REITs cut payouts, what a stressed payout ratio looks like, the sector and recession triggers, the early warning signs to watch, and how income investors can protect their cash flow.
For income investors, a REIT dividend cut can be painful — it reduces your cash flow and often drives the share price lower at the same time. Understanding why REITs cut dividends helps you anticipate and avoid the worst surprises. At root, REITs cut payouts when cash flow can no longer sustain them: a stressed payout ratio (the dividend exceeding the REIT's adjusted funds from operations, or AFFO), sector and recession triggers like falling occupancy, declining rents, and tenant defaults, refinancing and liquidity stress, or a deliberate decision to preserve capital and reinvest. The 2020 pandemic, when many hotel and retail REITs slashed distributions, is a vivid example. The good news is that dividend cuts usually leave warning signs — a payout ratio creeping toward or above 100% of AFFO, declining occupancy, high leverage with near-term maturities, and distributions funded by return of capital or debt. Why REITs Cut Payouts
At the most basic level, REITs cut dividends when their cash flow can no longer sustain the payout. A REIT's distributions are funded by the cash its properties generate — rents, after operating costs, debt service, and the capital needed to maintain the buildings. When that cash flow falls short of what the REIT is paying out, something has to give, and reducing the dividend is often the responsible choice. So a dividend cut is usually a symptom of a deeper problem: the payout has outrun the cash flow that supports it.
There are a few distinct paths to that point. The most common is a stressed payout ratio, where the dividend exceeds the REIT's adjusted funds from operations (AFFO), the cash-flow measure used to judge dividend sustainability. Another is a sector or recession shock that reduces the cash flow itself — falling occupancy, declining rents, or tenant defaults. A third is refinancing or liquidity stress, where a REIT facing maturing debt and tight credit conserves cash to meet obligations. A fourth is a deliberate strategic choice to cut the dividend in order to preserve capital or reinvest in the business. So cuts stem from insufficient cash flow, whether from a stretched payout, a demand shock, financing pressure, or strategy.

Stressed Payout Ratios
The payout ratio is the single most important indicator of dividend sustainability, and a stressed payout ratio is the clearest precursor to a cut. For REITs, the right measure isn't net income (which is distorted by depreciation) but cash flow — specifically AFFO, or adjusted funds from operations, which approximates the recurring cash a REIT can actually distribute after accounting for the capital needed to maintain its properties. The AFFO payout ratio is the dividend divided by AFFO, and it tells you how much of the REIT's distributable cash is being paid out.
A healthy REIT typically pays out a high but sustainable share of AFFO, leaving a cushion. When the payout ratio creeps toward 100% of AFFO, the cushion shrinks — the REIT is distributing nearly all of its distributable cash, leaving little margin for a downturn. When the ratio exceeds 100%, the REIT is paying out more than it's generating, which is unsustainable: it must fund the shortfall from cash reserves, asset sales, new debt, or return of capital, none of which can continue indefinitely. So a payout ratio at or above 100% of AFFO is a red flag that a cut may be coming, because the math simply doesn't work over time.
So a stressed payout ratio — the dividend approaching or exceeding AFFO — is the most direct warning that a REIT may cut. So watching the AFFO payout ratio is central to judging sustainability. Sector & Recession Triggers
Even a REIT with a previously healthy payout ratio can be pushed into a cut by a sector or recession shock that reduces the cash flow funding the dividend. The mechanism is straightforward: a REIT's distributions depend on rents, and when occupancy falls, rents decline, or tenants default, the cash available to distribute shrinks. If the drop is severe enough, the payout that was sustainable last quarter becomes unsustainable, and a cut follows. So an external shock can turn a covered dividend into an uncovered one almost overnight.
The 2020 pandemic is the textbook example. Hotel REITs, whose revenue evaporated as travel stopped, and retail REITs, whose tenants closed and stopped paying rent, cut their distributions heavily — some suspended dividends entirely — because the cash flow simply wasn't there. Recessions can have similar effects more broadly: rising vacancies, tenant bankruptcies, and pressure on rents reduce REIT cash flow across affected sectors. Different sectors are exposed to different triggers — hotels to travel demand, retail to consumer and tenant health, office to remote-work trends — so sector matters a great deal. Refinancing & Liquidity Stress
A REIT can also cut its dividend because of refinancing and liquidity stress, even when its properties are still generating reasonable cash flow. REITs typically carry significant debt, and that debt must be refinanced as it matures. If a large slug of debt comes due during a period of tight credit or high interest rates, a REIT may face a difficult choice: it can refinance at a much higher cost (which reduces the cash available for dividends), or it can conserve cash — including by cutting the dividend — to pay down debt or shore up liquidity.
This dynamic is especially acute for highly leveraged REITs with near-term maturities. When credit markets tighten (as in 2007-2009), the ability to refinance can become uncertain, and preserving cash becomes a priority for survival, not just optimization. Cutting the dividend frees up cash to meet debt obligations, reduce leverage, or maintain a liquidity buffer. So a dividend cut can be a defensive financial move — a way to strengthen the balance sheet under refinancing pressure — rather than purely a sign of operating weakness. So leverage and debt maturities are a distinct source of dividend risk, separate from operating cash flow.

Early Warning Signs
The good news for income investors is that dividend cuts usually leave warning signs you can watch for in advance. The most important is a stretched AFFO payout ratio — a ratio creeping toward 100% means the cushion is thin, and a ratio above 100% means the dividend isn't covered by cash flow and is being funded from other sources. Declining funds from operations (FFO) or AFFO, or falling occupancy and rents, are related signs that the cash flow funding the dividend is eroding before the payout ratio fully reflects it.
Several other red flags deserve attention. High leverage combined with near-term debt maturities raises refinancing risk, which can force a defensive cut. Distributions being funded by return of capital or by new debt — rather than by operating cash flow — are a warning that the REIT may be paying more than it's earning to maintain the appearance of a steady dividend. An unusually high dividend yield relative to peers can also be a flag, since the market sometimes signals skepticism about a dividend's sustainability by pricing the shares low (which mechanically lifts the yield). Protecting Your Income
You can't eliminate the risk of a REIT dividend cut, but you can take steps to reduce its impact on your income. The first is diversification — across REIT sectors and across individual REITs. If your income depends on a single REIT or a single sector, one cut can devastate your cash flow; spreading across many REITs and sectors means any one cut is a smaller dent. The 2020 experience, where hotel and retail REITs cut heavily while other sectors held up, shows how diversification cushions sector-specific shocks.
The second step is to favor REITs with sustainable, well-covered dividends — those paying out a prudent share of AFFO with a real cushion, rather than stretching to fund an eye-catching yield. A lower but safer yield often beats a higher but fragile one for income reliability. The third is to monitor coverage on an ongoing basis: keep an eye on the AFFO payout ratio, FFO and occupancy trends, leverage and debt maturities, and the source of distributions. If the warning signs appear, you have time to reassess rather than being caught off guard. So protecting your income means diversifying, favoring sustainable payers, and monitoring coverage.

What is AFFO and why does it matter for dividends?
AFFO, or adjusted funds from operations, is a cash-flow measure that approximates the recurring cash a REIT can actually distribute to shareholders. It starts from FFO (funds from operations, which adds back depreciation to net income) and then subtracts recurring capital expenditures — the money needed to maintain properties in rentable condition — and makes other adjustments. AFFO matters for dividends because it's the best gauge of the sustainable cash available to fund distributions: net income understates a REIT's cash flow (because of non-cash depreciation), while FFO overstates the cash truly available (because it ignores maintenance capital). AFFO sits between them and approximates real distributable cash. The AFFO payout ratio — dividend divided by AFFO — therefore tells you whether a dividend is covered by cash flow. A dividend that exceeds AFFO isn't sustainable over time. So AFFO is the key metric for judging dividend safety: if a REIT's distributions consistently exceed its AFFO, a cut becomes likely. So when assessing a REIT's income, look at AFFO coverage, not just the headline yield.

Did REITs cut dividends during the pandemic?
Yes — the 2020 pandemic produced widespread REIT dividend cuts, concentrated in the hardest-hit sectors. Hotel REITs, whose revenue evaporated as travel stopped, and retail REITs, whose tenants closed and stopped paying rent, cut their distributions heavily, and some suspended dividends entirely, because the cash flow simply wasn't there to support them. The mechanism was straightforward: a REIT's distributions depend on rents, and when occupancy collapsed and tenants defaulted, the cash available to distribute shrank dramatically, turning sustainable dividends into unsustainable ones almost overnight. Other sectors fared very differently — industrial and data center REITs, supported by surging e-commerce and digital demand, generally maintained or even grew distributions. So 2020 is a textbook example of how a sector-specific demand shock can force dividend cuts in some sectors while leaving others intact. So the pandemic vividly illustrated both why REITs cut dividends (cash flow can't sustain the payout) and why diversifying across sectors helps protect income — a single shock devastated some sectors but not others.
Are REIT dividends guaranteed?
No — REIT dividends are not guaranteed. A REIT's distributions are funded by the cash its properties generate, and that cash flow can decline for many reasons: falling occupancy, declining rents, tenant defaults, recessions, rising interest costs, or refinancing stress. When cash flow falls short of the payout, a REIT can — and often will — cut or even suspend its dividend, as many hotel and retail REITs did in 2020. The board sets the dividend each period, and there's no obligation to maintain it. While the 90% distribution rule requires REITs to pay out most of their taxable income to keep their tax status, that taxable income itself falls when the business struggles, so the rule doesn't guarantee any particular dividend amount. So income investors should treat REIT distributions as variable, not fixed — meaningful and often substantial, but subject to being reduced when the underlying real estate income declines. So don't build a budget that assumes a REIT dividend is as certain as a bond coupon. Past performance and current yields don't guarantee future distributions.
Does a high REIT dividend yield mean it's safe?
Not necessarily — a very high dividend yield can actually be a warning sign rather than a sign of strength. Yield is the annual dividend divided by the share price, so a yield can become high not because the dividend is generous but because the share price has fallen. The market sometimes prices a REIT's shares low precisely because it doubts the dividend's sustainability, which mechanically pushes the yield up — a 'yield trap.' So an unusually high yield relative to peers should prompt you to dig deeper, not to assume the income is secure. The better question is whether the dividend is covered by cash flow: check the AFFO payout ratio, the trend in FFO and occupancy, leverage and debt maturities, and the source of distributions. A lower but well-covered yield is often safer than a higher but fragile one. So don't chase yield in isolation — judge a REIT dividend by its coverage and sustainability, not just its headline rate. What sectors are most prone to dividend cuts?
No sector is permanently immune, but some are more exposed to particular shocks. Hotel REITs are highly sensitive to travel demand and the economic cycle, so their cash flow — and dividends — can swing sharply, as 2020 showed when many cut or suspended distributions. Retail REITs are exposed to consumer spending and tenant health, so recessions or shifts in shopping behavior can pressure their rents and payouts. Office REITs face structural questions tied to remote-work trends that can affect occupancy and rents over time. By contrast, sectors with more durable demand — such as certain net-lease, healthcare, residential, industrial, and data center REITs — have often shown steadier cash flow, though none is risk-free. The key point is that sector exposure shapes dividend risk: a REIT's vulnerability depends on what drives its tenants' ability to pay rent. So when building a REIT income allocation, consider each holding's sector exposure and avoid over-concentrating in sectors most vulnerable to cyclical or structural shocks. So diversifying across sectors helps protect income. Past performance doesn't guarantee future results.
Can a REIT cut its dividend even if its properties are doing well?
Yes — a REIT can cut its dividend because of refinancing and liquidity stress even when its properties are still generating reasonable cash flow. REITs typically carry significant debt that must be refinanced as it matures. If a large amount of debt comes due during a period of tight credit or high interest rates, a REIT may face a difficult choice: refinance at a much higher cost (reducing the cash available for dividends) or conserve cash — including by cutting the dividend — to pay down debt or shore up liquidity. This is especially acute for highly leveraged REITs with near-term maturities, particularly when credit markets tighten, as they did in 2007-2009. In that situation, cutting the dividend is a defensive financial move to strengthen the balance sheet, not necessarily a sign of operating weakness. So balance-sheet pressure is a distinct source of dividend risk, separate from operating cash flow. So when assessing dividend safety, look not only at operating performance but also at leverage levels and the debt maturity schedule, since financing stress alone can trigger a cut.

What is the difference between FFO and AFFO?
FFO and AFFO are both REIT cash-flow measures, but AFFO is the more conservative and more relevant one for judging dividends. FFO — funds from operations — starts from net income and adds back real estate depreciation and amortization (large non-cash charges that understate a REIT's cash flow) while excluding gains and losses on property sales. FFO is a better measure of operating performance than net income, but it ignores the recurring capital a REIT must spend to keep its properties rentable. AFFO — adjusted funds from operations — refines FFO by subtracting those recurring maintenance capital expenditures (and making other adjustments), so it approximates the actual recurring cash available to pay dividends. Because AFFO accounts for maintenance capital, it's generally the better gauge of dividend sustainability: the AFFO payout ratio shows whether a dividend is truly covered by cash flow. So FFO measures operating cash flow broadly, while AFFO measures the distributable cash that actually supports the dividend. So for assessing dividend safety, AFFO coverage is the more telling metric. Verify a REIT's definitions, since AFFO calculations can vary.
Does the 90% distribution rule prevent dividend cuts?
No — the 90% distribution rule doesn't prevent dividend cuts. The rule requires a REIT to distribute at least 90% of its taxable income each year to qualify as a REIT and avoid corporate-level tax, but it's a percentage of taxable income, not a fixed dividend amount. When a REIT's business struggles — occupancy falls, rents decline, tenants default — its taxable income falls too, so 90% of a much smaller income figure means a much smaller required distribution. The REIT can satisfy the rule while still paying a substantially reduced dividend. In severe cases, taxable income can drop dramatically, allowing (or requiring) a deep cut. So the 90% rule ensures REITs pass through most of their taxable income, but it doesn't lock in any particular dividend level or protect against cuts when the underlying income declines. So income investors shouldn't interpret the 90% rule as a guarantee of stable distributions — it guarantees a high payout ratio relative to taxable income, not a stable dividend in dollar terms. Should I sell a REIT after it cuts its dividend?
This is educational information, not personalized advice, and the answer depends on your situation and on why the cut happened. A dividend cut driven by a temporary, sector-wide shock (like the 2020 pandemic) may be different from one driven by deep structural problems or unsustainable leverage. Before reacting, it's worth understanding whether the cut reflects a passing disruption or a lasting deterioration in the REIT's cash flow and balance sheet, and whether the new, lower dividend is now well-covered and sustainable. Sometimes a cut is a prudent move that strengthens the REIT and sets up a more durable dividend going forward; other times it's the first sign of continuing trouble. Your own goals, income needs, time horizon, and the rest of your portfolio also matter. So rather than automatically selling (or holding) after a cut, assess the cause, the sustainability of the new payout, and your own situation. So consider consulting a financial professional rather than reacting purely to the headline, and remember that past performance doesn't guarantee future results.
Does diversification protect against REIT dividend cuts?
Diversification doesn't prevent individual REITs from cutting dividends, but it does reduce the impact of any single cut on your overall income — which is exactly why it's a core defense for income investors. If your income depends on one REIT or one sector, a single cut can devastate your cash flow; spreading across many REITs and several sectors means any one cut is a smaller dent in your total income. The 2020 experience illustrates this vividly: hotel and retail REITs cut heavily, but investors diversified across sectors that also held industrial, data center, and other resilient REITs saw far less of their income disrupted. Diversification across individual REITs (not just sectors) also helps, since it reduces the chance that one REIT's leverage or tenant problems dominate your income. That said, diversification can't fully protect against a broad, economy-wide shock that pressures many sectors at once, and it doesn't replace evaluating each holding's dividend coverage. So diversification is a powerful but partial tool — it cushions sector-specific and company-specific cuts, and works best combined with favoring sustainable, AFFO-covered payers and monitoring coverage. So diversify your REIT income across sectors and REITs to make it more resilient, while still vetting each holding.
