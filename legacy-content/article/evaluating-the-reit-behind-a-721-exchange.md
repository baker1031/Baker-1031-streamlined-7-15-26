Evaluating the REIT Behind a 721 Exchange
Back to All Insights
721 Exchange Gerald F. "Jerry" Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read
In a 721 exchange, you're transitioning into a specific REIT and tying your financial outcome to it — so evaluating the REIT thoroughly is critical. This guide explains how to evaluate the REIT behind a 721 exchange: its portfolio quality and diversification, management and sponsor, financial health, track record, and the traded-vs-non-traded considerations.
When you do a 721 exchange, you're not just executing the step-by-step tax mechanics of the transaction — you're investing in a specific REIT, and your financial outcome (your returns, your holding's value) will depend on that REIT's performance. Because the 721 exchange is generally a one-way, largely irreversible move, you're committing to this REIT, so evaluating it thoroughly before proceeding is critical. A strong, well-managed REIT supports a good outcome; a weak one can lead to disappointment you can't easily escape. Evaluating the REIT means assessing its portfolio quality and diversification, its management and sponsor, its financial health, its track record, and (for liquidity) whether it's traded or non-traded. This guide explains how to evaluate the REIT behind a 721 exchange across these dimensions, so you transition into a REIT worthy of your commitment.
Why evaluating the REIT matters
Evaluating the REIT matters because, in a 721 exchange , you're tying your financial outcome to that REIT. Your returns (distributions), your holding's value, and your liquidity all depend on the REIT's performance, management, and financial health. So the REIT's quality directly determines your outcome — a strong REIT supports good returns and value; a weak one can hurt them.
This matters especially because the 721 exchange is generally a one-way, largely irreversible move — you're committing to this REIT, and you can't easily (potentially tax-free) exit if it disappoints. So a poor REIT choice is hard to undo. This makes evaluating the REIT before committing critical — you want to be confident in the REIT you're transitioning into, since you'll be tied to it.
So evaluating the REIT isn't optional diligence; it's essential to a good 721 exchange outcome. The strategy's benefits (the capital-gains deferral an UPREIT conversion provides , diversification, and the like) only materialize well if the REIT performs well. Portfolio quality and diversification
A primary evaluation dimension is the REIT's portfolio quality and diversification. Portfolio quality means the caliber of the REIT's properties — are they well-located, well-maintained, institutional-quality, income-producing properties? A REIT with high-quality properties (strong locations, good tenants, sound condition) is more likely to perform well than one with weaker properties. So assess the quality of the REIT's holdings.
Diversification means how spread the portfolio is — across how many properties, geographies, property types, and tenants. A well-diversified portfolio (many properties across markets and types, with no single property or tenant dominating) provides more stability and risk reduction than a concentrated one. So assess the REIT's diversification — the breadth that provides the risk reduction you're seeking.

Management and sponsor
The REIT's management and sponsor are crucial to evaluate, since they drive the REIT's performance. The management team makes the decisions — acquiring, managing, financing, and disposing of properties, and setting the strategy — so their competence, experience, and track record directly affect your returns. A skilled, experienced management team is more likely to perform well; a weak one is a risk.
The sponsor (the firm behind the REIT) is also important — its reputation, integrity, financial strength, and history. A reputable, financially strong sponsor with a good track record provides confidence; a problematic sponsor (poor history, conflicts, financial issues) is a warning sign. So assess the sponsor's reputation and strength, since you're relying on them.

Financial health
The REIT's financial health is an important evaluation dimension, affecting its stability and performance. Key financial factors include the REIT's leverage (debt level — excessive debt increases risk, especially if rates rise or income softens), its debt coverage (whether its income comfortably covers its debt service), its liquidity (whether it has adequate cash/resources), and its overall balance-sheet strength. A financially sound REIT (reasonable leverage, strong coverage, adequate liquidity) is more stable; a financially stressed one is riskier.
Another financial factor is the distribution coverage — whether the REIT's distributions are covered by its actual operating income (a sign of sustainability) or funded partly from other sources (like borrowings or offering proceeds, which isn't sustainable, a concern especially noted for some non-traded REITs). So assess whether the distributions are sustainably covered.

Track record and performance
The REIT's track record and performance history provide evidence of its quality, though past performance doesn't guarantee future results. A REIT with a solid track record — consistent performance, sound management decisions, weathering market cycles, delivering reasonable returns and distributions over time — provides more confidence than one with a poor or short history. So examine the REIT's performance history.
The track record to assess includes the REIT's historical returns and distributions (consistency and level), how it performed in different market conditions (especially downturns), the management's decisions and their outcomes, and any problems or red flags in its history. So the track record gives a picture of how the REIT has performed.
While past performance isn't a guarantee (and should be considered alongside the other factors), a strong track record is a positive indicator, and a poor one is a warning. Traded vs. non-traded considerations
The traded-vs-non-traded distinction is an important evaluation consideration, affecting liquidity, transparency, and the evaluation itself. For a publicly-traded REIT, much information is publicly available (financial reports, the market price, analyst coverage), making evaluation easier and the liquidity (after conversion) robust. So a traded REIT offers transparency and liquidity that aid evaluation and your future flexibility.
For a non-traded REIT, less information may be publicly available, the valuation is by periodic NAV (less transparent), the liquidity is limited (redemption programs), and the fees may be higher — all of which warrant extra scrutiny in evaluation (as FINRA emphasizes for non-traded REITs). So evaluating a non-traded REIT requires more careful diligence (on the NAV, fees, liquidity, and disclosures).
So the REIT type affects both the evaluation (traded REITs are more transparent) and your considerations (liquidity, fees, transparency). If liquidity and transparency matter to you, a traded REIT may be preferable; a non-traded REIT warrants extra scrutiny. A high-quality portfolio (strong properties) and good diversification (many properties across markets and types, no single property or tenant dominating) indicate likely performance and the risk reduction you're seeking. So assess both the quality (caliber) and diversification (breadth) of the REIT's holdings — key indicators of its performance and the risk reduction it provides.

What are red flags in evaluating a REIT?
Warning signs include weak or poorly-located properties (low portfolio quality), excessive concentration (poor diversification), inexperienced or troubled management, a problematic sponsor (poor history, conflicts, financial issues), high leverage or weak debt coverage (financial stress), distributions not covered by operating income (unsustainable, funded from other sources), a poor track record, and (for non-traded REITs) high fees, limited liquidity, or opaque valuation. So red flags span the portfolio, management, financials, and structure. If you see these warning signs, scrutinize further or consider a different REIT. Recognizing red flags helps you avoid a weak REIT you'd be committed to.
Can I evaluate a non-traded REIT thoroughly?
It's harder than a traded REIT (less public information, periodic NAV rather than market price), but you can and should evaluate it carefully — examining its offering documents (the PPM, financials), its portfolio, management, sponsor, fees, distribution coverage, and liquidity terms. Non-traded REITs warrant extra scrutiny (FINRA emphasizes careful review). So while a non-traded REIT is less transparent, thorough due diligence (with your advisor) on its disclosures and structure is essential before transitioning into it. The extra effort is important given the lower transparency. So evaluate a non-traded REIT thoroughly, scrutinizing its disclosures, fees, and liquidity given its lower transparency.

What information sources should I use to evaluate a REIT?
For a publicly-traded REIT, use its public filings (annual and quarterly reports, with financials and disclosures), the market price and analyst coverage, and ratings or research. For a non-traded REIT, use its offering documents (the private placement memorandum or prospectus, with the portfolio, financials, fees, and risk disclosures) and any periodic reports. Also consider the sponsor's overall reputation and track record. Your advisor can help gather and interpret these sources. So use the REIT's official disclosures, market information (for traded REITs), and sponsor information, with professional help interpreting them. Thorough evaluation draws on these sources to assess the REIT comprehensively.
How important is the REIT's fee structure in the evaluation?
Quite important — fees reduce your net returns over time, so a REIT with high fees (especially some non-traded REITs with significant upfront and ongoing fees) will deliver lower net returns than a comparable lower-fee REIT. So examine the fee structure (upfront fees, ongoing management and advisory fees, and other expenses) as part of the evaluation, since it directly affects your returns. A reasonable fee structure is a positive; excessive fees are a concern. FINRA has emphasized understanding non-traded REIT fees. So weigh the fees in your evaluation — they're a key factor in the REIT's net returns to you, alongside the portfolio, management, and financials.
Can a REIT look good but still be a poor choice for me?
Yes — a REIT can be high-quality (strong portfolio, management, financials) but still not fit your specific goals. For example, a sound non-traded REIT might be a poor fit if you need liquidity (which it limits), or a REIT in a sector you don't want might not fit your diversification goals. So beyond the REIT's objective quality, consider its fit with your goals (liquidity needs, sector preferences, risk tolerance). A good REIT for one investor may not suit another. So evaluate both the REIT's quality (objectively strong?) and its fit (right for your goals?). We help you assess both, ensuring the REIT is not just good but right for you.
How much time should I spend evaluating the REIT?
As much as the importance warrants — because the 721 exchange is generally irreversible and ties your outcome to the REIT, thorough evaluation is worth significant time and care, not a rushed decision. Take the time to examine the portfolio, management, financials, track record, fees, and structure (with your advisor), and to understand what you're committing to. There's no need to rush (a standalone 721 has no statutory deadlines), so use the time to evaluate carefully. Given the stakes (your committed outcome), thorough evaluation is time well spent. Don't shortcut the evaluation; the irreversibility and your dependence on the REIT make careful diligence essential, however long it takes.
Should I compare multiple REITs before choosing?
Yes, when possible — comparing multiple REIT (or DST-to-REIT) options helps you choose the strongest, best-fitting one for your goals, rather than accepting the first available. Comparing the portfolio quality, diversification, management, financials, track record, fees, liquidity, and fit across options gives you a basis for selecting the best. Of course, your options may be limited by which REITs/DSTs are available for your property and situation, but where there are choices, comparing them is valuable. So compare the available options (with your advisor) to choose a strong, well-fitting REIT.
