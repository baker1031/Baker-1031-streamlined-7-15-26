Do Production Payments Qualify for a 1031 Exchange?
Back to All Insights
Mineral & Royalty Gerald F. "Jerry" Baker, III · Updated June 2026 · 16 min read
A production payment looks like a royalty but is taxed like a loan. That distinction — codified in IRC §636 — usually puts it outside Section 1031. This guide explains what a production payment is, why it's treated as debt, the narrow situations where eligibility questions arise, and the safer alternatives for deferring the tax.
Among oil and gas interests, the production payment is the one that most reliably surprises owners trying to do a 1031 exchange. It pays like a royalty — a cost-free share of production arriving as periodic checks — so owners naturally assume it's real property that can be exchanged like any mineral interest. But the tax law sees it differently: a production payment is treated as a loan, not a real-property interest, under Section 636, which means it generally cannot be exchanged under Section 1031. The cash flow looks like ownership; the tax characterization is debt. This guide explains what a production payment is, why the law treats it as a loan, the narrow situations where an eligibility question might arise, and — most usefully — the safer alternatives for deferring tax when a production payment is what you hold.
To see how production payments fit alongside the interests that do qualify, read our pillar guide on 1031 exchanges for minerals and royalties .
What is a production payment?
A production payment is a right to a specified sum of money, or a specified quantity of production, carved out of an oil and gas property and payable solely out of that property's production. Its defining feature is that it's capped: the holder is entitled to a defined amount — a fixed dollar sum or a set volume of oil or gas, often plus an interest-like factor — after which the production payment terminates. It doesn't continue for the life of the reserve; it ends once the specified amount has been delivered.
Production payments are used to monetize part of a property's future production without selling the underlying interest. An owner might carve out a production payment to raise cash, transferring the right to a defined slice of future production to an investor in exchange for an up-front sum. The investor recovers their money (plus a return) out of production over time, and when the defined amount is paid, the production payment is extinguished and full economic ownership reverts to the original owner.
This structure is precisely what gives a production payment its economic character: an up-front sum repaid out of production over time, with a defined endpoint. That is the structure of a loan secured by the property's production, not the structure of continuing ownership. The cash flow resembles a royalty in form — periodic, cost-free payments — but the underlying economics are a financing arrangement, which is the key to understanding its tax treatment.
Why it's often treated as a loan
Section 636 of the Internal Revenue Code addresses production payments directly and treats them, for most purposes, as mortgage loans rather than economic interests in the mineral property. Under this treatment, a carved-out production payment is regarded as a loan made by the holder to the owner, secured by and repaid from the property's production. The holder's receipts are treated as repayments of principal and interest, not as royalty income from a real-property interest.
This treatment follows the economic substance. Because a production payment is capped and self-liquidating — it pays off a defined amount and then ends — it functions like debt: an advance of capital recovered over time with a return. Congress codified in Section 636 what the economics imply, removing the ambiguity that might otherwise arise from the royalty-like cash flow. The result is a clear rule: a production payment is debt for tax purposes, with limited exceptions.
The consequence for 1031 is direct and decisive. Section 1031 defers gain on exchanges of like-kind real property; a loan is not real property, and debt instruments aren't like-kind to real estate. So a production payment, treated as a loan, generally cannot be the relinquished or replacement property in a 1031 exchange . This isn't a close call rooted in duration analysis — it's a statutory characterization that places production payments outside the like-kind regime by treating them as financing.

Production payments and the like-kind test
Applying the like-kind test to a production payment is straightforward once its debt character is understood. The test asks whether the interest is real property held for investment; a production payment, treated as a loan under Section 636, is not real property at all but a financing arrangement. It therefore fails the threshold requirement of the like-kind rules, which is being real property in the first place. The duration analysis that governs royalties and overrides isn't even reached, because the statutory debt characterization is dispositive.
This places production payments at the clear non-qualifying end of the oil and gas spectrum. Where a perpetual royalty is clearly real property and qualifies, and a term royalty is uncertain and fact-specific , a production payment is clearly debt and clearly doesn't qualify. There's little ambiguity for a standard carved-out production payment — the answer is generally no, it cannot be exchanged under Section 1031, regardless of how the cash flow looks.
The importance of this clarity is practical: it means an owner holding a production payment should not attempt a 1031 with it on the assumption that its royalty-like payments make it exchangeable. They don't. Recognizing that the interest is debt, not real property, redirects the planning toward the alternative deferral tools discussed below, rather than toward a 1031 that would be disallowed. Understanding the like-kind test's verdict on production payments — generally no — is what steers an owner to a workable strategy.
When an eligibility question might still arise
While the general rule is clear, a few narrow situations warrant careful analysis rather than a flat assumption. The first is characterization itself: not every interest labeled or assumed to be a production payment actually is one under Section 636, and conversely, an interest titled as a royalty might function as a production payment in substance. The threshold question — is this actually a production payment? — should be confirmed by reading the instrument, because the label can mislead in either direction.
Section 636 also contains specific rules and exceptions for certain production payments — for example, those retained in leasing transactions or carved out in particular ways are subject to nuanced treatment. The detailed operation of these rules is a matter for a tax specialist, and in unusual structures the treatment may differ from the standard 'it's a loan' outcome. This doesn't generally open a path to like-kind treatment, but it means the analysis of a specific production payment should be done by someone who knows Section 636's contours, not assumed.
Finally, the way an interest is structured before a transaction can matter. An owner contemplating monetizing production has choices about whether to use a production payment (debt, non-qualifying) or a different structure that might preserve a real-property interest. The eligibility question, in other words, is often better addressed at the structuring stage than after a production payment already exists. Once you hold a true production payment, the like-kind door is generally closed; the time to influence the outcome is before the interest is created.
Safer alternatives for deferral
If you hold a production payment and want to defer or manage the tax on monetizing it, several alternatives exist even though a 1031 generally isn't available. An installment sale can spread the gain over the years you receive payments, deferring tax on each portion until received — though, because a production payment is already debt-like, the interaction of these rules should be modeled carefully with your CPA. The installment approach can smooth a large gain across tax years and manage your bracket.
A Qualified Opportunity Fund offers a different path: it can defer gain from any source — not just real estate — by reinvesting the gain into a qualified fund, with the potential to make a decade of appreciation potentially tax-free. Because it isn't tied to like-kind real property, it can be available for gains where a 1031 isn't, making it worth considering for production-payment proceeds. It has its own rules and timelines, which your CPA can walk through.
The most powerful alternative is often structural foresight: if you haven't yet created the production payment, consider whether a different structure — retaining a perpetual real-property interest that could later be exchanged — better serves your goals. And for owners who simply want to monetize, a deliberate taxable sale, timed and planned with your CPA, may be the cleanest path. The key is to recognize early that a production payment closes the 1031 door, so you can choose among these alternatives deliberately rather than discovering the limitation after the fact.

Structuring with foresight
The recurring lesson of production payments is that the most valuable decisions happen before the interest exists. An owner who wants to raise cash from a mineral property has a menu of structures, and they differ sharply in tax consequences. A production payment delivers cash but creates a debt interest that can't be exchanged; selling a fractional perpetual royalty delivers cash while leaving the buyer with an exchangeable real-property interest; other arrangements have their own profiles. Choosing among them with the 1031 consequences in mind is far better than discovering the limitation later.
This foresight matters on both sides of a transaction. A party considering acquiring a production payment as an investment should understand that they're acquiring debt, not real property, and that it won't be available for a future 1031 — which affects its after-tax appeal and exit options. A party creating one should understand what they're giving up in exchangeability. The tax characterization isn't a technicality; it shapes the economics for everyone involved.
For owners who already hold production payments, foresight still helps with what comes next. Knowing the interest is debt directs planning toward the alternative deferral tools and away from a doomed 1031, and it informs decisions about whether and when to let the production payment run off versus monetize it further. In every case, the production payment's debt character is a fact to plan around deliberately — and the earlier it's recognized, the more options remain. This is why understanding the answer to 'do production payments qualify?' — generally no — is so useful even though the answer is unfavorable: it points you toward the strategies that do work.
Common confusions about production payments
Several recurring confusions trip up owners and even some advisers when production payments meet 1031 questions. The first is conflating a production payment with a royalty because both pay cost-free from production. The distinction is the cap: a royalty (when perpetual) continues for the reserve's life with no defined ceiling, while a production payment is capped at a fixed sum or volume and then ends. That cap is the difference between real property and debt, and missing it leads owners to assume exchangeability they don't have.
The second confusion is assuming the label controls. An instrument titled 'royalty' might function as a production payment in substance if it's capped, and the tax law follows substance over label. Conversely, something loosely called a 'production payment' in conversation might not meet Section 636's definition. Only a careful read of the instrument's actual terms — does it cap the recovery? — resolves the characterization, which is why the document, not its title, governs.
The third confusion is hoping the duration analysis that helps borderline royalties might rescue a production payment. It doesn't, because the production-payment characterization is statutory and reached before any duration spectrum is considered — a production payment is debt by definition under Section 636, full stop. Recognizing these three confusions — cap versus no cap, substance versus label, and statutory debt versus duration analysis — clears up most of the misunderstanding around production payments and 1031, and points owners toward the alternative strategies that actually work for a debt-characterized interest.

Frequently Asked Questions
Do production payments qualify for a 1031 exchange?
Generally no. A production payment is treated as a loan under IRC Section 636, not a real-property interest, and debt isn't like-kind to real estate. So a standard carved-out production payment cannot be the relinquished or replacement property in a 1031 exchange, despite its royalty-like cash flow.
What is a production payment?
A right to a specified sum of money or quantity of production, carved out of a mineral property and payable solely from its production. It's capped — the holder receives a defined amount and then the interest ends. It's used to monetize part of future production for an up-front sum, functioning economically like a loan repaid from production.
Why is a production payment treated as a loan?
Because it's capped and self-liquidating — an up-front sum recovered from production over time with a return, then extinguished. That's the economic structure of debt. Section 636 codifies this, treating a production payment as a mortgage loan for tax purposes, with the holder's receipts regarded as repayments of principal and interest rather than royalty income.
What is IRC Section 636?
The Internal Revenue Code section governing the tax treatment of mineral production payments. It generally treats a carved-out production payment as a mortgage loan rather than an economic interest in the mineral property, which is the statutory basis for production payments failing the like-kind test.
Why does the debt characterization matter for 1031?
Because Section 1031 only defers gain on exchanges of like-kind real property. A production payment treated as a loan isn't real property, so it fails the threshold requirement — it isn't even reached by the duration analysis that governs royalties. The statutory debt characterization places it outside the like-kind regime.
But a production payment pays like a royalty — doesn't that make it real property?
No. The royalty-like cash flow is the surface form, but the underlying economics — a capped, self-liquidating recovery of an up-front sum — are debt. The tax law looks to economic substance, codified in Section 636, not the appearance of the payments. This is why owners are often surprised that a 'royalty-like' interest can't be exchanged.
Is every interest called a production payment actually one?
Not necessarily — the label can mislead. An interest titled as a royalty might function as a production payment in substance, and vice versa. The threshold step is having a tax adviser read the instrument to confirm whether it's actually a production payment under Section 636, because the characterization, not the title, determines the treatment.
Are there exceptions where a production payment might be treated differently?
Section 636 has specific rules and nuances for certain production payments, such as those retained in leasing transactions or structured in particular ways, which a tax specialist should analyze. These generally don't open a path to like-kind treatment, but they mean a specific production payment's treatment should be confirmed by someone who knows the section's contours.
What can I do instead of a 1031 with a production payment?
Consider alternatives: an installment sale to spread the gain over the years payments are received, a Qualified Opportunity Fund to defer gain by reinvesting it (available for gains where a 1031 isn't), structural foresight if the interest isn't yet created, or a deliberate, well-timed taxable sale. Your CPA can model which fits your situation.
Can a Qualified Opportunity Fund help with production-payment proceeds?
Possibly. A Qualified Opportunity Fund can defer gain from any source by reinvesting the gain, and isn't tied to like-kind real property, so it may be available for gains where a 1031 isn't — including production-payment proceeds. It has its own rules and timelines, which your CPA can walk through to see if it fits.
Should I avoid creating a production payment if I want to exchange later?
If future exchangeability matters, yes — consider it at the structuring stage. A production payment creates a debt interest that can't be exchanged, while selling a fractional perpetual royalty or retaining a real-property interest preserves like-kind eligibility. Choosing the structure with the 1031 consequences in mind is far better than discovering the limitation afterward.
If I already hold a production payment, what are my options?
Plan around its debt character: use alternative deferral tools (installment sale, Qualified Opportunity Fund) for any monetization, decide whether to let it run off or monetize further, and avoid attempting a 1031 that would be disallowed. Recognizing early that it's debt, not real property, directs you to strategies that actually work.
Does the buyer of a production payment get real property?
No — the buyer acquires debt, not real property, so they too can't use it in a future 1031, which affects its after-tax appeal and exit options. Anyone considering acquiring a production payment as an investment should understand they're buying a financing instrument, with the tax profile of debt rather than mineral ownership.
Is the production-payment rule a close call?
No — unlike the uncertain analysis for term royalties or net profits interests, the treatment of a standard production payment is clear: Section 636 makes it debt, and debt isn't like-kind to real estate. The main analysis is confirming the interest is actually a production payment; once that's established, its non-qualification is well-settled.
Who should analyze my production payment?
A tax adviser or attorney familiar with Section 636 and oil and gas taxation. They confirm whether the interest is truly a production payment, explain the debt characterization and its 1031 consequences, and help you choose among the alternative deferral strategies. The analysis is specialized, so a generalist may not catch the nuances.
How is a production payment different from a royalty in one sentence?
A royalty (when perpetual) is uncapped and continues for the reserve's life, making it real property; a production payment is capped at a fixed sum or volume and then ends, making it debt under Section 636. The cap is the dividing line between an exchangeable real-property interest and a non-exchangeable loan.
Does it matter what the document calls the interest?
No — substance controls, not the label. An instrument titled 'royalty' can be a production payment if it's capped, and the tax law follows the economic reality. Only a careful read of the actual terms — does it cap the recovery? — resolves the characterization, which is why the document's substance, not its title, governs the 1031 analysis.
